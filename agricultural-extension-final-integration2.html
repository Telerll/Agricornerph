13. Communication in Extension

Communication is the process of sharing meaning through interaction. In extension work, communication is important because agricultural knowledge and innovations must be properly understood by farmers.

Attributes of Communication

  • Dynamic – Communication changes continuously.
  • Systemic – Communication involves interconnected elements.
  • Interaction through Symbols – Uses words, signs, gestures, and language.
  • Meaning is Personally Constructed – People interpret messages differently.

Field of Experience

Communication becomes effective when the sender and receiver share similar experiences and understanding.

Barriers to Effective Communication

  • Perception
  • Language or jargon
  • Emotions
  • Preconceived notions
  • Attention problems
  • Wordiness

14. Basic Elements of Communication

1. Source

The sender or originator of the message.

Characteristics of the Source
  • Homophily – Similarity between sender and receiver.
  • Credibility – Trustworthiness of the sender.
Dimensions of Credibility
  • Character
  • Competence
  • Composure
  • Dynamism

2. Message

Message Factors
  • Code – Language or symbols used.
  • Content – Information conveyed.
  • Treatment – Way the message is presented.

3. Channels

Medium used to deliver the message such as interpersonal communication, radio, television, and print materials.

4. Receiver

Person receiving and interpreting the message.

Psychological Orientation
  • Selective exposure
  • Selective perception
  • Selective retention
  • Selective discussion

5. Feedback

The receiver’s response to the message.

Types of Response
  • Cognitive effect
  • Neutralization
  • Boomerang effect
  • Conservation

15. Communication Models

  1. Berlo’s Model – Focuses on source, message, channel, and receiver.
  2. Lasswell’s Model – “Who says what in which channel to whom with what effect?”
  3. Shannon and Weaver Model – Emphasizes transmission and noise.
  4. Osgood and Schramm Model – Communication as a circular process with feedback.

18. Community Organizing

Community organizing is a process of educating, organizing, and mobilizing people toward self-reliance and development.

Community Elements

  • Manners
  • Customs
  • Tradition
  • Mode of speech

Community Characteristics

  • Common system of values
  • Normatively defined relation
  • Interdependence
  • Recognition of belongings
  • System of stratification
  • Locality

Roles of Community Organizer

  • Facilitator
  • Trainer
  • Advocate
  • Researcher
  • Planner
  • Catalyst

Community Organizing Approaches

  1. Issue-based approach
  2. Socio-economic project-based approach
  3. Church/Faith-based approach

19. Some Approaches to Rural Change

  1. Concentrated Approach – Focuses on a limited area using selected cooperators.
  2. Team Approach – Specialists work together as a team.
  3. Cooperative Approach – Different agencies collaborate.
  4. Institutional Approach – Organizes farmers into associations and cooperatives.
  5. Utilizing Local Leaders – Uses formal and informal leaders to influence communities.

20. The Adoption Process

Innovation

An idea, practice, or technology perceived as new.

Adoption

Acceptance and continuous use of an innovation.

Characteristics Affecting Adoption

  • Relative Advantage – Innovation is perceived as better.
  • Compatibility – Fits beliefs, culture, and needs.
  • Complexity – Easy to understand and use.
  • Trialability – Can be tested on a small scale.
  • Observability – Results can easily be seen.

21. Diffusion Process

Diffusion is the spread of innovations through communication channels over time.

Stages of Diffusion

  1. Awareness
  2. Interest
  3. Evaluation
  4. Trial
  5. Adoption

26. Explanations for Rejection / Adoption of Technology

Individual-Blame Hypothesis

Blames personal limitations such as poverty and low education.

System-Blame Hypothesis

Blames social and institutional systems.

Pro-Innovation Bias

Assumes innovation is always correct and failure is the farmer’s fault.

Sustainable agriculture maintains the productive capacity of natural resources while continuously meeting human needs.

It Involves

  • Conservation of natural resources
  • Proper technological orientation
  • Institutional change
  • Continuous satisfaction of present and future needs
