Accounting

1. Accounting as a Language

Accounting has been called the “Language of Business.”

2. Basic Accounting Principles or Concepts

1. First Principle: Money, the Common Denominator
The advantage of expressing facts in monetary terms is that money is a common denominator by which heterogeneous facts about a business can be expressed in numbers that can be added and subtracted.
2. Second Principle: The Business Entity
Accounts are kept for business entities, as distinguished from the persons who are associated with these entities.
3. Third Principle: Value Equals Cost
Valuable things owned by a business are called assets in accounting. A fundamental concept is that assets are ordinarily recorded in the accounting books of the company at the price paid to acquire them.
4. Fourth Principle: The Dual Aspect Principle

a. The valuable things owned by a business are called assets.
The claims of various parties against these assets are:
1. Liabilities (claims of creditors)
2. Owner’s equity / Members’ equity / Net worth / Capital
5. Fifth Principle: The Accrual Principle
Net income refers to changes in the owners’ equity resulting from operations rather than changes in cash.

3. Balance Sheet Application

The balance sheet is the fundamental accounting report where every transaction can be recorded in terms of its effect on the financial position of the business. It shows where the money came from and where it is placed, reflecting the financial position on a specific date.

4. Fundamental Conventions of Accountants

Consistency
Once a company decides on a method, it must apply the same method to all subsequent transactions.
Conservatism
When there is a choice, the accountant will show the lower value of assets or lower owner’s equity.
Materiality
Trivial matters are not recorded if not significant to decision-making.

5. Growth and Expansion Framework

Horizontal and Vertical Integration
• Horizontal – grouping similar business activities
• Vertical – extension to another stage of operation
Diversification
Broadening services by adding new lines of business.
Consolidation and Merger
• Consolidation – forming a new association
• Merger – one absorbs another

6. Member Volume Strategies

1. Building up a large number of quality members
2. Providing economical and efficient services
3. Effective volume-generating practices

7. Pooling Method of Marketing

Pooling is a method where members’ products are combined and sold collectively, then paid an average price after deducting expenses.

Objectives of Pooling

1. Diffuse marketing risk
2. Systematic marketing programs
3. Economies in distribution

8. Financing

Equity Capital – capital from members
Loan Capital – borrowed funds

9. Equity Capital Methods

• Sale of Shares of Stock (Common & Preferred)
• Member Capital Contribution
• Capital Retains
• Deferred Patronage Refund

10. Accumulation of Reserves

Valuation Reserves – cover bad debts and depreciation
Liquidity Reserves – cover emergencies and losses
Capital Reserves – used for expansion and investment

11. Loan Capital Methods

1. Borrowings
2. Deferred Payments
3. Trade Credit
4. Bonds and Debentures
5. Donations, Grants, Gifts
