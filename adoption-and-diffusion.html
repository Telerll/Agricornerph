The Adoption Process

Innovation

An innovation is an idea, practice, or technology perceived as new by a person.

Adoption

Adoption is the process by which a person is exposed to, evaluates, and accepts/practices or rejects a particular innovation.

It is the decision to make full use of the technology as the best option possible.

Characteristics of an Innovation that Determine Adoption

1. Relative Advantage

The degree to which a farmer recognizes an innovation as better than the idea or practice he/she is currently practicing.

2. Compatibility

The degree to which a farmer perceives the innovation or new idea to be consistent with:

  • Socio-cultural values and beliefs
  • Previously introduced ideas
  • Felt needs

3. Complexity

The degree to which a farmer can understand and follow an innovation.

4. Trialability

The degree to which an innovation can be experimented or duplicated on a small-scale basis.

5. Observability

The degree to which the results of an innovation can easily be observed by farmers.

Diffusion Process

The diffusion process is defined as the spreading of information from a source to its intended or unintended receivers/users and the stages through which adoption passes.

It is also a process by which an innovation is communicated through certain channels over time among members of a social system.

Stages in the Adoption Process

1. Awareness

A stage when a person knows that an idea exists but lacks details and information about it.

Further Explanation

At this stage, an individual becomes aware or exposed to the new idea. The person may know only the name of the idea or product but may not know:

  • What it is
  • What it will do
  • How it works

2. Interest

A stage when a person seeks more factual information about:

  • What the innovation is
  • How it works
  • What its advantages are

Further Explanation

The individual becomes curious and wants more information. The person may think the innovation could:

  • Increase income
  • Help control pests and diseases
  • Improve farming or home life

3. Evaluation

A stage when an individual mentally applies the innovation to his/her farming operation or family situation.

Further Explanation

The farmer:

  • Assesses the innovation mentally
  • Considers advantages and disadvantages
  • Anticipates future situations
  • Decides whether or not to try the innovation

4. Trial

A stage whereby an individual weighs the advantages and risks involved and tries the innovation on a small scale.

Further Explanation

The farmer tests the innovation on a limited basis to determine if it is beneficial.

Even if individuals already gathered information for a long time, they still need actual testing before deciding.

5. Adoption

The final stage of the diffusion process characterized by:

  • Full-scale use
  • Continuous use of the innovation

Further Explanation

This stage is characterized by continued satisfaction with the innovation in preference to the old method.

Notes on Adoption Process

  1. A farmer may reject the innovation based on observation and assessment.
  2. The five stages may not always occur in exact order.
  3. Trial may be skipped.
  4. Evaluation may occur throughout the whole process.
  5. Adoption may not always be the final stage because more information may still be needed to confirm, reinforce, or switch the decision.

Innovation-Decision Process Theory

1. Knowledge

  • Stage when the individual learns about the existence of the innovation.
  • Gains understanding of how it functions.

2. Persuasion

The individual forms a favorable or unfavorable opinion regarding the innovation.

3. Decision

The individual engages in activities that lead to a choice between:

  • Adoption
  • Rejection

4. Implementation

The individual puts the innovation into actual use.

5. Confirmation

The individual seeks reinforcement for the decision already made or reverses the decision if conflicting messages about the innovation are found.

Individual Innovativeness Theory

Innovativeness

Innovativeness is the degree to which an individual is relatively earlier in adopting new ideas compared to other members of the system.

It is considered the best single indicator of modernization.

Modernization

Modernization is the process by which individuals change from a traditional way of life to a more:

  • Technologically advanced
  • Rapidly changing
  • Complex style of life

Individual Innovativeness Theory

This theory states that individuals who are risk takers or otherwise innovative will adopt an innovation earlier in the continuum of adoption and diffusion.

Categories of Adopters

  1. Innovators
  2. Early Adopters
  3. Early Majority
  4. Late Majority
  5. Laggards

1. Innovators / Venturesome

  • First people in the locality to adopt innovations.
  • Have larger farms and higher net worth.
  • More educated and informed.
  • Highly respected and have prestige.
  • Take calculated risks.
  • Experimenters.

2. Early Adopters / Respectable

  • Follow the innovators.
  • Usually younger than average.
  • Have higher education than slower adopters.
  • Participate actively in community organizations and programs.
  • More informed.
  • Read newspapers and magazines.

3. Early Majority / Deliberate

  • Slightly above average in age, education, and farming experience.
  • Not elected leaders but active in the community.
  • Most likely informal leaders.
  • Have fewer resources than innovators and early adopters.
  • Adopt new ideas after being convinced of their value.

4. Late Majority / Skeptical

  • Make up a large block of farmers in the community.
  • Have less education and are older than the early majority.
  • Less active in organizations and associations.
  • Skeptical about innovations and new ideas.
  • Hesitant to discard old knowledge and techniques.
  • Adopt practices only when they become majority practice in the community.

5. Laggards / Traditional

  • Last people to adopt innovations.
  • Suspicious of innovations, innovators, and change agents.
  • Very conservative.
  • Usually oldest, least educated, and less wealthy.
  • Not risk takers.
  • Believe strongly in superstitious and indigenous knowledge.
  • Adoption decisions depend on past experiences.

Adoption Rate

The rate of adoption is the relative speed with which an innovation is adopted by members of a social system.

It is measured by the number of receivers who adopt a new idea within a specified period of time.

S-Shaped Curve of Adoption

The theory states that innovations diffused over time resemble an S-shaped curve.

Pattern of Adoption

  1. Slow gradual growth
  2. Rapid dramatic increase
  3. Stabilization
  4. Decline

Strategies to Effect Adoption

  1. Extension Worker Intervention
  2. Progressive Farmer Strategy
  3. Mass Marketing
  4. Follower-Farmer Strategy
  5. Selective Farmer-to-Farmer

1. Extension Worker Intervention

Extension workers are valuable agents for quick diffusion and adoption of innovations.

They must possess:

  • Credibility
  • Understanding of farmers and their problems
  • Knowledge of technology
  • Extension methods
  • Research results

2. Progressive Farmer Strategy

Extension agents work closely with progressive farmers from whom innovations diffuse to others.

This strategy is useful especially when working under pressure to meet targets such as:

  • Fertilizer distribution
  • Planting of new varieties

3. Mass Marketing

Mass marketing:

  • Uses advertising to reach target markets.
  • Uses communication systems to provide information.
  • Creates public awareness.
  • Introduces new products and technologies.

It may also be used for:

  • Introducing new brands
  • Modifying existing products
  • Promoting changes in practices and institutions

Mass marketing requires careful product design and delivery for properly identified target markets.

4. Follower-Farmer Strategy

This strategy emerged from the Training and Visit (T & V) system introduced in many Third World countries.

Features

  • Contact farmers are identified from groups of households in villages.
  • Extension workers work closely with contact farmers.
  • Information is transferred from contact farmers to farm households.

5. Selective Farmer-to-Farmer

Knowledge acquired by one farmer is transferred to another farmer through:

  • Informal discussions
  • Field visits
  • Village interactions

Discussions may also happen during:

  • Leisure time
  • Social occasions
  • Religious gatherings

Relatives, friends, and neighbors participate in the discussions.

Explanations for Rejection / Adoption of Technology

“Individual-Blame” Hypothesis

“I am poor, low educated, traditional, and fatalistic.”

“System-Blame” Hypothesis

Leadership is traditional and exploitative, and power relationships are unequal.

“Pro-Innovation” Bias

“The innovation is okay; it is the farmer’s fault.”
