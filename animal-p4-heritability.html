Genetic Variation and Heritability

Genetic Variation

Genetic variation is the difference in genetic makeup among animals of the same species.

It causes differences in growth, milk production, fertility, disease resistance, and feed efficiency.

Genetic variation is the raw material for selection.

Importance of Genetic Variation

Without variation, breeders cannot select superior animals.

  • Identify superior animals
  • Select desirable traits
  • Improve future generations
No variation = no selection = no genetic progress.

Sources of Genetic Variation

  • Mutation
  • Recombination
  • Segregation
  • Independent assortment
  • Fertilization
Variation comes from gene changes and gene reshuffling.

Mutation

Mutation is a sudden change in DNA sequence.

It creates new genetic variation by changing the genetic code.

Mutation creates new genes and new variation.

Recombination

Recombination is the exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during meiosis.

It creates new combinations of genes in offspring.

Recombination reshuffles genes.

Segregation

Segregation is the separation of paired alleles during gamete formation.

Each gamete receives only one allele from each pair.

Segregation creates variation among offspring.

Independent Assortment

Independent assortment is the random distribution of chromosomes into gametes during meiosis.

This produces different combinations of traits in offspring.

Independent assortment increases diversity.

Fertilization

Fertilization is the random union of sperm and egg.

It combines genes from two parents and creates new genetic combinations.

Fertilization increases genetic diversity.

Environmental Variation

Environmental variation is variation caused by non-genetic factors.

  • Nutrition
  • Management
  • Climate
  • Disease
Not all observed variation is genetic.

Phenotypic Variation

Phenotypic variation is the observable difference in traits among animals.

  • Body weight
  • Milk yield
  • Fertility
  • Growth rate
Phenotypic variation is what breeders observe.

Components of Phenotypic Variation

Phenotypic variation is composed of genetic variation and environmental variation.

P = G + E

P = Phenotype / observable trait
G = Genotype / genetic contribution
E = Environment / environmental contribution

Heritability

Heritability (h²) is the proportion of phenotypic variation that is due to genetic variation.

It measures how much of the observed trait is inherited.

Heritability ranges from:

  • 0 to 1
  • 0% to 100%
Heritability measures inheritance strength.

Heritability Formula

h² = Vg / Vp

h² = Heritability
Vg = Genetic variance / variation due to genes
Vp = Phenotypic variance / total observed variation

Meaning of Heritability

Heritability tells breeders:

  • How much a trait is controlled by genes
  • How well selection will work
Heritability predicts response to selection.

High Heritability Traits

Traits with high heritability respond quickly to selection.

  • Carcass traits
  • Body size
  • Growth rate
High heritability = faster genetic progress.

Low Heritability Traits

Traits with low heritability respond slowly to selection.

  • Fertility
  • Reproductive efficiency
  • Survival

These traits are influenced more by environment and management than genetics.

Low heritability = slower genetic progress, stronger management effect.

Moderate Heritability Traits

Some traits have moderate heritability.

  • Milk production
  • Egg production
  • Weaning weight
Moderate heritability = moderate selection response.

Repeatability

Repeatability is the consistency of repeated performance records of the same animal over time.

It predicts whether future performance will be similar to past performance.

Example:

  • Milk yield in one lactation compared with the next

Repeatability is usually equal to or higher than heritability because it includes permanent environmental effects.

Repeatability predicts future performance.

Breeding Value

Breeding value (BV) is the genetic worth of an animal as a parent.

It estimates the value of genes an animal can pass to offspring.

Breeding value predicts genetic usefulness.

Selection Differential

Selection differential (S), also called selection reach, is the difference between the mean of selected parents and the mean of the whole population.

It measures selection intensity.

Stronger selection = greater possible genetic gain.

Response to Selection

Response to selection (R) is the amount of genetic improvement achieved after selection.

R = h²S

R = Response to selection / genetic gain achieved
h² = Heritability / inheritance strength
S = Selection differential / selection intensity

Generation Interval

Generation interval is the average age of parents when their offspring are born.

It affects how fast genetic progress occurs per year.

  • Short generation interval = faster yearly progress
  • Long generation interval = slower yearly progress
Faster turnover = faster yearly genetic gain.

Board Exam Master Concept

Genetic progress depends on:

  • Variation
  • Heritability
  • Selection
  • Generation interval
No variation = no selection = no progress.
