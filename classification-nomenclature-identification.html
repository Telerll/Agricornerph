📚 Classification, Nomenclature and Identification

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

Definition of Terms

Classification

Classification is the ordering of organisms into a hierarchy of categories based on similarities and differences.

Nomenclature

Nomenclature is the naming of organisms. It comes from the words nomen, meaning name, and calare, meaning to call.

Identification

Identification is the major application of classification. It is the process of determining the correct name or group of an unknown specimen.

Keys

Keys are sequences of paired statements or questions that allow the user to eliminate alternative options and eventually associate an unknown specimen with a name.

Taxonomy

Taxonomy involves the theoretical basis for classification and the study of classification systems.

Importance of Classification, Nomenclature and Identification

  • Organizes the study of insects into clear groups.
  • Helps identify pest and beneficial insects correctly.
  • Provides a universal system of naming organisms.
  • Supports biological control, quarantine, and pest management.
  • Allows scientists and agriculturists to communicate accurately.

Taxonomic Hierarchy

Taxonomic hierarchy arranges organisms from broad categories to more specific categories.

The Class Insecta

The Class Insecta is divided into orders based on the structure of the wings and mouthparts, the type of metamorphosis, and other morphological characteristics.

Insects are broadly divided into two subclasses: Apterygota and Pterygota.

Subclass Apterygota

Apterygota are primitively wingless insects.

  1. Protura – proturans
  2. Collembola – springtails
  3. Diplura – diplurans
  4. Thysanura – bristletails and silverfish

Subclass Pterygota

Pterygota includes winged insects and secondarily wingless insects.

Division Exopterygota

Exopterygota undergo simple body change during growth or incomplete metamorphosis.

  1. Ephemeroptera – mayflies
  2. Odonata – dragonflies and damselflies
  3. Plecoptera – stoneflies
  4. Dermaptera – earwigs
  5. Orthoptera – grasshoppers, crickets, roaches, mantids, walking sticks
  6. Isoptera – termites
  7. Embioptera – webspinners
  8. Zoraptera – zorapterans
  9. Psocoptera – psocids
  10. Mallophaga – chewing lice
  11. Anoplura – sucking lice
  12. Thysanoptera – thrips
  13. Hemiptera – bugs
  14. Homoptera – aphids, scale insects, hoppers, cicadas, psyllids, whiteflies

Division Endopterygota

Endopterygota undergo complex body change during growth or complete metamorphosis.

  1. Neuroptera – alderflies, antlions, dobsonflies, fishflies, lacewings, snakeflies, owlflies
  2. Coleoptera – beetles
  3. Strepsiptera – twisted-winged parasites
  4. Mecoptera – scorpionflies
  5. Trichoptera – caddisflies
  6. Lepidoptera – moths and butterflies
  7. Diptera – flies
  8. Siphonaptera – fleas
  9. Hymenoptera – ants, bees, wasps, sawflies

Distinguishing Insect Orders

Insect orders are distinguished using morphological characteristics such as antennae, mouthparts, wings, body shape, metamorphosis, and abdominal appendages.

1. Order Protura

  • Microscopic and elongated insects.
  • Possess piercing mouthparts.
  • No antennae, cerci, compound eyes, and metamorphosis.

2. Order Diplura

  • Microscopic, wingless, and elongated insects.
  • Have cerci and chewing mouthparts.
  • No compound eyes and metamorphosis.

3. Order Thysanura

  • Small to moderate-sized insects.
  • Primitively wingless and elongated.
  • Possess three tail-like filaments.
  • Body covered with gray silvery scales.
  • No metamorphosis.

4. Order Collembola

  • Microscopic, primitively wingless insects.
  • Usually possess six-segmented abdomen.
  • Have abdominal forked structure called furcula.
  • No metamorphosis.

5. Order Ephemeroptera

  • Small to moderate-sized insects with four delicate membranous wings.
  • Have setaceous antennae and long multi-segmented cerci.
  • Adults are short-lived and may die in one to two days.
  • Mating often occurs in swarms.

6. Order Odonata

  • Large insects with large compound eyes.
  • Have strong chewing mouthparts and setaceous antennae.
  • Elongated abdomen with small cerci.
  • Includes dragonflies and damselflies.
  • Anisoptera: dragonflies; wings extended horizontally when at rest.
  • Zygoptera: damselflies; wings held vertically above the abdomen when at rest.

7. Order Orthoptera

  • Moderate to large insects.
  • Forewings are leathery tegmina; hind wings are membranous.
  • Have chewing mouthparts.
  • Most are phytophagous.

8. Order Isoptera

  • Also known as termites or white ants.
  • Soft-bodied, small to medium-sized insects.
  • Winged forms have four membranous wings that are exactly alike.
  • Have prognathous head, chewing mouthparts, and moniliform antennae.

9. Order Plecoptera

  • Moderate-sized insects.
  • Naiads are abundant under stones in creeks or streams.
  • Have plaited wings; hind wings are larger than forewings.
  • Have long filiform antennae, chewing mouthparts, and long multisegmented cerci.

10. Order Embioptera

  • Small insects with depressed and elongated body.
  • Have four membranous wings.
  • Foretarsi are enlarged and have silk glands.

11. Order Psocoptera

  • Small insects with filiform and long antennae.
  • Hind wings are smaller than forewings.
  • Wings are held roof-like over the body at rest.
  • Have chewing mouthparts.

12. Order Dermaptera

  • Elongated and moderately sized insects.
  • Forewings are very small and leathery.
  • Hind wings are folded lengthwise and crosswise.
  • Forcep-like cerci are found at the tip of the abdomen.
  • Many earwigs are predatory to major pests such as corn borer.

13. Order Mallophaga

  • Small wingless insects.
  • Head is broader than the thorax.
  • Have chewing mouthparts, prominent claws, and reduced eyes.
  • Parasitic on birds; feed on feathers and dried blood.

14. Order Anoplura

  • Small wingless insects with dorsal spiracles.
  • Dorsoventrally flattened bodies.
  • Live as ectoparasites on mammals.
  • Legs are adapted for clinging on hair.
  • Eggs are called nits and attach singly on hair.

15. Order Thysanoptera

  • Extremely elongated and minute insects.
  • Have rasping-sucking mouthparts with conical beak.
  • Four membranous wings are fringed with long hairs.

16. Order Hemiptera

  • Small to moderate-sized insects with four wings.
  • Forewings or hemelytra have thickened leathery base and membranous tip.
  • Hind wings are entirely membranous.
  • Piercing-sucking mouthparts arise from the anterior end of the head.

17. Order Homoptera

  • Small insects with four membranous or leathery wings.
  • Wings slope at the sides of the body when at rest.
  • Piercing-sucking mouthparts arise from the posterior side of the head near the first pair of legs.
  • Many species transmit plant viruses and other disease-causing agents.

18. Order Coleoptera

  • Most numerous insect order, making up a large portion of known insects.
  • Front pair of wings are hard or thickened and called elytra.
  • Membranous hind wings are used for flight.
  • Have chewing mouthparts with well-developed mandibles.

19. Order Lepidoptera

  • Includes attractive and colorful insects.
  • Adults have siphoning mouthparts.
  • Larvae or caterpillars have chewing mouthparts.
  • Larvae feed on roots, stems, and leaves of plants.
  • Wings are covered with scales.

20. Order Diptera

  • Have one pair of functional membranous forewings.
  • Hind wings are modified into knob-like halteres for balance.
  • Mouthparts vary from sponging type to cutting-sponging type.

21. Order Hymenoptera

  • Contains many beneficial insects.
  • Both wings are membranous.
  • Smaller hind wings are interlocked with larger forewings by hook-like hamuli.
  • Have chewing-lapping mouthparts.
  • Parthenogenesis is common.
  • Ovipositor may be adapted for sawing, piercing, or stinging.

22. Order Siphonaptera

  • Small, wingless insects with laterally compressed body.
  • Hind legs are enlarged and adapted for jumping.
  • Have piercing-sucking mouthparts.
  • Ectoparasites of mammals, including humans.

23. Order Neuroptera

  • Small to medium-sized insects with four membranous wings.
  • Wings have numerous veins and cross veins.
  • Have chewing mouthparts.
  • Many are predatory; lacewings are used to control insect pests.

24. Order Mecoptera

  • Medium-sized insects with four membranous wings.
  • Head is modified into a beak.
  • Have chewing mouthparts.
  • Predatory insects.

25. Order Trichoptera

  • Small to medium-sized insects with four membranous wings.
  • Wings are covered with hair.
  • Adult mouthparts are not well developed.

26. Order Strepsiptera

  • Very small endoparasitic insects considered true parasites.
  • Only males have wings.
  • Forewings are reduced to club-shaped appendages.
  • Hind wings are fan-shaped and large compared to the body.

Nomenclature

Scientific names are used because common names may vary by place or language. Scientific names provide a universal identity for organisms.

Binomial Nomenclature

Binomial nomenclature was developed by Carolus Linnaeus. Each organism is named using two parts: Genus and species.

Example: Nilaparvata lugens
Nilaparvata = genus
lugens = species

Rules in Writing Scientific Names

  • The genus name begins with a capital letter.
  • The species name begins with a small letter.
  • Both names are italicized when typed.
  • When handwritten, both names are underlined separately.

Identification

Identification is the process of determining the identity of an insect specimen using observable characteristics.

Basis of Insect Identification

  • Body shape and body size
  • Antennae
  • Mouthparts
  • Wings and wing venation
  • Legs
  • Metamorphosis
  • Abdominal structures such as cerci, ovipositor, and genitalia

Taxonomic Keys

Taxonomic keys are tools used to identify unknown specimens. They usually contain paired choices that guide the user step by step toward the correct identification.

The most common type is the dichotomous key, where the user chooses between two contrasting statements at each step.

Summary

Classification organizes insects into groups, nomenclature gives them scientific names, and identification determines the correct identity of specimens. The Class Insecta is divided into major subclasses, divisions, and orders based on wings, mouthparts, metamorphosis, and other morphological characteristics.
