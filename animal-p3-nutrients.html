Nutrients and Their Functions

Nutrients

Nutrients are chemical substances in feed required by animals for maintenance, growth, reproduction, and production.

Animals require six major nutrients:

  • Water
  • Carbohydrates
  • Proteins
  • Fats
  • Vitamins
  • Minerals

Water

Water is the most essential nutrient.

Functions:

  • Regulates body temperature
  • Transports nutrients
  • Removes waste
  • Supports digestion
  • Maintains cell function
  • Supports milk production
Animals die faster without water than without feed.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the main energy source in animal diets.

Functions:

  • Provide energy
  • Maintain body heat
  • Support metabolism
  • Provide fiber for digestive health

Examples:

  • Starch
  • Sugar
  • Cellulose
  • Fiber
In ruminants, fiber is fermented by rumen microbes to produce volatile fatty acids, which are major energy sources.

Proteins

Proteins are body-building nutrients made of amino acids.

Functions:

  • Build muscles
  • Repair tissues
  • Form enzymes
  • Form hormones
  • Support milk, egg, wool, and meat production
  • Support immunity
Protein quality depends on amino acid balance and digestibility.

Limiting Amino Acids

The most common limiting amino acids are:

  • Lysine
  • Methionine

These are especially important in swine and poultry.

If lysine or methionine is lacking, the animal cannot fully use the rest of the protein.

Fats / Lipids

Fats are concentrated sources of energy.

Functions:

  • Provide stored energy
  • Increase energy density of feed
  • Provide insulation
  • Support hormone production
  • Help absorb fat-soluble vitamins

Fat-Soluble Vitamins:

  • K
  • A
  • D
  • E
Memory trick: KADE
Fats provide 2.25 times more energy than carbohydrates or proteins.

Vitamins

Vitamins are organic compounds needed in small amounts for normal body functions.

Functions:

  • Regulate metabolism
  • Support immunity
  • Support reproduction
  • Support growth
  • Prevent deficiency diseases

Types:

  • Fat-soluble vitamins: A, D, E, K
  • Water-soluble vitamins: B-complex and C
Ruminants can synthesize some B vitamins through rumen microbes.

Minerals

Minerals are inorganic nutrients needed for body structure and regulation.

Functions:

  • Bone formation
  • Nerve function
  • Muscle contraction
  • Fluid balance
  • Enzyme activity
  • Reproduction and immunity

Macrominerals:

  • Calcium
  • Phosphorus
  • Sodium
  • Potassium
  • Magnesium
  • Chloride
  • Sulfur

Microminerals:

  • Iron
  • Copper
  • Zinc
  • Manganese
  • Iodine
  • Selenium
  • Cobalt
Calcium and phosphorus are especially important for bones, growth, and milk production.

Calcium : Phosphorus Ratio

The ideal Calcium : Phosphorus ratio is 2 : 1.

Poor balance may cause:

  • Rickets
  • Osteomalacia
  • Weak bones

Macronutrients and Micronutrients

Macronutrients

Nutrients needed in large amounts:

  • Water
  • Carbohydrates
  • Proteins
  • Fats

Micronutrients

Nutrients needed in small amounts:

  • Vitamins
  • Minerals

Energy Nutrients

Energy is mainly supplied by:

  • Carbohydrates
  • Fats
  • Protein, only when needed

Energy is used for:

  • Maintenance
  • Growth
  • Reproduction
  • Lactation
  • Egg production
  • Movement
  • Body heat
Energy deficiency causes weight loss, poor growth, and poor production.

Nutrient Balance

Nutrient balance means providing nutrients in correct amounts and proportions.

  • Too little nutrient causes deficiency
  • Too much nutrient may cause toxicity or waste
A good ration must be balanced, digestible, palatable, and economical.

Final Ultra Master Summary
