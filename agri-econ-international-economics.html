International Economics

International Economics is the branch of economics that studies trade and financial relationships among countries. It explains how nations exchange goods, services, labor, technology, capital, and financial resources with one another.

In the Philippine setting, international economics is important because the country imports products such as fuel, fertilizers, machinery, feeds, wheat, and some food products, while exporting agricultural and industrial products such as bananas, coconut products, tuna, mangoes, pineapples, garments, and electronics.

International Trade

International trade refers to the exchange of goods and services between people, firms, or countries. It allows countries to obtain products that they cannot efficiently produce domestically.

Export

Export refers to the sale of goods and services abroad.

Examples of Philippine exports include bananas, coconut oil, tuna, mangoes, pineapples, and electronics.

Import

Import refers to the purchase of goods and services from abroad.

Examples of imports include petroleum, fertilizers, wheat, machinery, imported rice, and feeds.

Net Exports

Net exports refer to exports minus imports.

Net Exports = Exports − Imports

If exports are greater than imports, the country has a trade surplus. If imports are greater than exports, the country has a trade deficit.

Benefits of International Trade

Mutual Gains from Voluntary Exchange

Countries benefit from trade because buyers and sellers voluntarily exchange goods and services when both sides expect to gain. Consumers gain access to more products, while producers gain access to larger markets.

Increased Competition

International trade increases competition among producers. Competition may improve product quality, lower prices, encourage efficiency, and promote innovation.

Division of Labor and Specialization

Countries specialize in producing goods where they are more efficient. Specialization lowers production costs, improves productivity, and increases total output.

Better Use of Skills and Resources

Countries export products they can efficiently produce and import products better produced by other nations.

The Philippines exports tropical agricultural products because of favorable climate, natural resources, and farming experience.

Specialization and Comparative Advantage

Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage exists when a country can produce a product at a lower opportunity cost compared to another country.

Even if one country is more efficient in producing all goods, trade may still be beneficial if countries specialize based on comparative advantage.

The Philippines may specialize in coconut and banana production while importing products that are more efficiently produced in temperate countries.

Absolute Advantage

Absolute advantage exists when a country can produce more goods using fewer resources compared to another country.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost refers to the value of the next best alternative forgone. It helps explain why comparative advantage becomes the basis of international trade.

Gains from Specialization

Specialization and international trade increase productivity, improve efficiency, lower costs, expand production, increase consumer choices, and improve income opportunities.

David Ricardo expanded Adam Smith’s ideas by emphasizing comparative advantage as the basis for international trade.

Agents of Trade Growth

Transportation Technology

Improved transportation lowers trade costs and speeds up the movement of goods from one country to another.

  • Cargo ships
  • Airplanes
  • Container vans
  • Refrigerated transport

Communication Technology

Modern communication systems improve international business transactions and link traders across the world.

  • Internet
  • Mobile communication
  • Digital banking
  • Online marketplaces

General Decline in Tariffs

The reduction of tariffs encourages freer international trade and allows more goods and services to move across borders.

Terms of Trade

Terms of trade refer to the quantity of imports a country can obtain in exchange for exports.

Improved terms of trade mean that a country receives more imported goods for each unit of export.

World Price

World price refers to the global market price determined by worldwide supply and demand.

Financial Linkages

Countries are connected through different economic flows. These linkages allow goods, services, capital, labor, information, technology, and money to move across countries.

Goods and Services Flows

Goods and services flows involve exports and imports between countries.

Capital and Labor Flows

Capital and labor flows involve the movement of investments, workers, and businesses across countries.

OFWs working abroad contribute to international labor flows and send remittances back to the Philippines.

Information and Technology Flows

Information and technology flows involve the exchange of knowledge, production techniques, innovations, prices, interest rates, and investment opportunities.

Financial Flows

Financial flows refer to the movement of money between countries for investments, loans, imports, exports, remittances, foreign aid, and debt payments.

Trade Liberalization and Free Trade

Free Trade

Free trade refers to the complete absence or significant reduction of restrictions on international trade.

Free trade may provide wider product choices, lower prices, greater efficiency, and increased competition. However, it may also create pressure on domestic producers who compete with cheaper imported goods.

Trade Barriers

Trade barriers are government restrictions that limit international trade. Governments sometimes impose trade barriers to protect domestic industries.

Protectionist Policy

Protectionism refers to government policies designed to protect local producers from foreign competition.

Tariffs

Tariffs are taxes imposed on imported goods. They may be used to protect local industries, generate government revenue, and reduce import competition.

Revenue Tariff

A revenue tariff is imposed mainly to raise government revenue.

Protective Tariff

A protective tariff is imposed to protect domestic producers from foreign competition by making imported goods more expensive.

Ad Valorem Tariff

An ad valorem tariff is based on a percentage of the value of imports.

Specific Tariff

A specific tariff is based on the quantity or number of units imported.

Economic Effects of Tariffs

  • Increase the prices of imported goods
  • Reduce imports
  • Encourage domestic production
  • Generate government revenue
  • Reduce consumer choices

Import Quota

Import quota refers to a law or policy that limits the quantity of imports allowed within a given period.

Quotas protect local industries by reducing foreign competition, but they may also raise prices and reduce product availability for consumers.

Economic Effects of Quotas

  • Protect domestic industries
  • Increase local production
  • Raise consumer prices
  • Reduce product availability

Net Costs of Tariffs and Quotas

Tariffs and quotas may benefit domestic producers, but they impose costs on consumers because imported goods become more expensive or less available.

Protection may raise domestic production, but it can also reduce consumer welfare and create inefficiencies.

Non-Tariff Barriers (NTB)

Non-tariff barriers are trade restrictions other than tariffs. These may include regulations, licensing requirements, product standards, inspection rules, and safety regulations.

Voluntary Export Restraint (VER)

Voluntary Export Restraint is an agreement where exporting countries voluntarily limit exports to another country to avoid stricter trade restrictions.

Justifications for Trade Barriers

Military Self-Sufficiency Argument

Certain industries must be protected for national security because they produce materials essential for national defense.

Domestic Employment Argument

Trade barriers may protect local employment by reducing competition from imported goods.

Diversification-for-Stability Argument

Protection may help economies develop different industries and reduce dependence on a few export products.

Infant Industry Argument

New domestic industries may require temporary protection while they develop the ability to compete with established foreign producers.

Strategic Trade Policy

Governments may protect industries with high growth and technological potential to help them become internationally competitive.

Protection Against Dumping Argument

Protection may be needed when foreign firms sell goods in another country at very low prices, sometimes below production cost.

Dumping can harm local producers because imported goods may be sold at prices that domestic producers cannot match.

Cheap Foreign Labor Argument

Domestic firms and workers may need protection from foreign producers that have much lower labor costs.

How to Reduce Trade Barriers

Unilateral Reduction

Unilateral reduction occurs when one country independently reduces trade barriers without requiring other countries to do the same.

Multilateral Negotiations

Multilateral negotiations involve simultaneous tariff reductions among many countries.

The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade or GATT is an example of a multilateral trade arrangement.

Regional Trading Areas

Regional trading areas are trade agreements among countries in a specific region.

Examples include ASEAN, NAFTA, and the European Union.

Managed Trade

Managed trade involves government actions that influence trade by persuading firms to buy or sell larger or smaller quantities of goods in other countries.

Voluntary Restraint Agreements (VRAs)

Voluntary Restraint Agreements are self-imposed restrictions by an exporting country on the quantity of goods exported to another country.

Voluntary Import Expansion (VIE)

Voluntary Import Expansion is a government agreement to expand imports from a particular country.

Balance of Payments (BOP)

Balance of Payments is the record of all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world.

It records exports, imports, investments, loans, remittances, foreign aid, and other international payments.

Accounts in the BOP

Current Account

The Current Account records transactions involving goods, services, income, and transfer payments.

Components of Current Account

Merchandise Trade

Merchandise trade refers to trade in physical goods.

Exports are expenditures by foreigners on domestically produced goods. Imports are domestic expenditures on foreign goods.

Investment Income

Investment income consists of international interest and dividend payments, including earnings of domestically owned firms operating abroad.

Other Services

Other services include payments for tourism, shipping, freight, insurance, legal assistance, royalty payments, interest, and profits on assets abroad.

Transfer Payments

Transfer payments include remittances of domestic residents working abroad, gifts, and grants.

Capital Account

The Capital Account records international transactions involving the purchase or sale of assets and other existing forms of wealth.

Equilibrium in the BOP

BOP equilibrium exists when total receipts from the rest of the world are equal to total payments made to foreigners.

BOP Deficit

A BOP deficit occurs when payments to foreign countries exceed receipts from foreign countries. This may indicate borrowing or accumulation of debts from abroad.

BOP Surplus

A BOP surplus occurs when receipts from the rest of the world exceed payments made to them.

Foreign Exchange

Foreign exchange refers to currencies of other countries used to make international payments.

Examples include the US dollar, Japanese yen, and euro.

Foreign Exchange Market

The foreign exchange market is the market where international currencies are bought and sold.

Major Players in the Foreign Exchange Market

  • Commercial banks
  • Central banks
  • Corporations engaged in international trade
  • Non-bank financial institutions

Exchange Rate System

Exchange Rate

Exchange rate refers to the price of one currency in terms of another currency.

Example: ₱56 = $1

Flexible or Floating Exchange Rate System

A flexible or floating exchange rate system allows exchange rates to be determined by market demand and supply, with little or no direct government intervention.

Fixed Exchange Rate System

A fixed exchange rate system exists when the government or central bank determines and maintains a specific exchange rate.

Determinants of Exchange Rates

Changes in Tastes

Changes in consumer preferences for foreign products may affect demand for foreign currency and alter exchange rates.

Relative Income Changes

A country’s currency may depreciate if its national income grows faster than that of other countries because imports may increase.

Relative Price-Level Changes

Differences in price levels between countries affect the demand and supply of currencies.

Relative Interest Rates

Changes in interest rates between countries may affect capital flows and exchange rates.

Speculation

Currency speculators buy and sell currencies expecting to gain from future changes in exchange rates.

Instruments of Foreign Exchange

Cable or Telegraphic Transfers

Cable or telegraphic transfers are electronic transfers of funds used to make payments abroad.

Bank Drafts

Bank drafts are checks drawn by one bank on another and may be used instead of telegraphic transfers.

Letter of Credit

A letter of credit is a banking document that guarantees payment in international trade. It may be denominated and made payable in the currency of either the exporter or importer.

Effects of International Trade on Philippine Agriculture

International trade affects Philippine agriculture through export opportunities, import competition, price fluctuations, foreign investments, and technology transfer.

Advantages

  • Larger markets for farmers
  • Higher export earnings
  • Access to better technology
  • More product choices for consumers

Disadvantages

  • Competition from imported agricultural products
  • Unstable world prices
  • Possible dependence on foreign markets
  • Pressure on small local producers
