🧪 Insecticides and Pesticides

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

Introduction

Pesticides are among the most widely used tools in pest management. They provide rapid and effective control of pests and help prevent economic losses in agricultural production.

However, improper use may result in pest resistance, pest resurgence, environmental pollution, health hazards, and pesticide residue problems.

Pesticides should be used responsibly and should form only one component of an Integrated Pest Management (IPM) program.

Definition of Pesticide

A pesticide is any substance or mixture of substances intended to prevent, destroy, repel, or control pests.

The term pesticide includes products used against insects, weeds, diseases, rodents, nematodes, mites, snails, and other harmful organisms.

Classification of Pesticides According to Target Organism

1. Insecticide

Used to control insect pests.

Examples

  • Malathion
  • Cypermethrin
  • Imidacloprid

2. Herbicide

Used to control weeds.

Examples

  • Glyphosate
  • 2,4-D

3. Fungicide

Used to control fungal diseases.

Examples

  • Mancozeb
  • Copper fungicides

4. Rodenticide

Used to control rodents.

Examples

  • Zinc phosphide
  • Warfarin
  • Brodifacoum

5. Nematicide

Used to control nematodes.

Examples

  • Carbofuran
  • Fenamiphos

6. Molluscicide

Used to control snails and slugs.

Examples

  • Niclosamide
  • Metaldehyde

7. Acaricide

Used to control mites and ticks.

Examples

  • Abamectin
  • Dicofol

Definition of Insecticide

An insecticide is a pesticide specifically designed to kill, repel, or suppress insect pests.

Classification of Insecticides According to Mode of Entry

1. Contact Insecticides

Contact insecticides kill insects through direct contact with the body.

Examples

  • Cypermethrin
  • Deltamethrin

Characteristics

  • No need to be eaten
  • Acts through insect cuticle
  • Rapid knockdown effect

2. Stomach Poisons

These insecticides must be ingested before they become effective.

Examples

  • Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt)
  • Arsenicals (historical examples)

Best Against

  • Chewing insects
  • Caterpillars
  • Beetles

3. Fumigants

Fumigants enter the insect body through the respiratory system.

Examples

  • Phosphine
  • Methyl bromide (restricted)

Uses

  • Stored grain protection
  • Warehouse treatment
  • Quarantine treatment

4. Systemic Insecticides

Systemic insecticides are absorbed by the plant and transported through plant tissues.

Examples

  • Imidacloprid
  • Thiamethoxam

Most Effective Against

  • Aphids
  • Leafhoppers
  • Planthoppers
  • Whiteflies

Systemic insecticides are highly effective against piercing-sucking insects because these insects feed directly on plant sap.

Classification of Insecticides According to Mode of Action

1. Neurotoxic Insecticides

These insecticides affect the insect nervous system.

Examples

  • Organophosphates
  • Carbamates
  • Pyrethroids
  • Neonicotinoids

2. Respiratory Poisons

These interfere with respiration and oxygen utilization.

Example

  • Phosphine

3. Insect Growth Regulators (IGRs)

These interfere with insect growth, molting, and development.

Examples

  • Methoprene
  • Diflubenzuron

4. Feeding Inhibitors

These stop or reduce insect feeding activity.

Example

  • Azadirachtin (Neem)

Classification According to Origin

A. Inorganic Insecticides

Derived from mineral sources.

Examples

  • Sulfur
  • Arsenicals

B. Botanical Insecticides

Derived from plants.

Examples

Nicotine

Source: Tobacco plant

Pyrethrum

Source: Chrysanthemum flowers

Rotenone

Source: Derris roots

Neem

Source: Azadirachta indica

C. Microbial Insecticides

Derived from microorganisms.

Examples

  • Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt)
  • Beauveria bassiana
  • Metarhizium anisopliae

D. Synthetic Organic Insecticides

Artificially manufactured chemical compounds.

Organophosphates

  • Malathion
  • Chlorpyrifos

Carbamates

  • Carbaryl
  • Carbofuran

Pyrethroids

  • Cypermethrin
  • Deltamethrin

Neonicotinoids

  • Imidacloprid
  • Thiamethoxam

Advantages of Insecticides

  • Fast acting
  • Rapid reduction of pest populations
  • Effective during severe outbreaks
  • Easy application
  • Can prevent major economic losses

Disadvantages of Insecticides

  • Pest resistance
  • Pest resurgence
  • Secondary pest outbreaks
  • Pesticide residues
  • Environmental pollution
  • Harm to natural enemies
  • Health risks to humans and animals

Economic Importance of Insecticides

When properly used, insecticides help farmers protect crops, increase yield, improve quality, and reduce economic losses caused by insect pests.

However, misuse may create long-term agricultural and environmental problems.

Role of Insecticides in IPM

Insecticides remain important tools in pest management but should not be used as the sole method of control.

Within an IPM program, insecticides should be applied only when pest populations exceed the Economic Threshold Level (ETL).

ALE High-Yield Exam Tips

Board Trap #1

Contact insecticides do not need to be eaten. Stomach poisons must be ingested.

Board Trap #2

Systemic insecticides are most effective against piercing-sucking insects.

Board Trap #3

Bt is classified as a microbial insecticide, not a chemical insecticide.

Board Trap #4

Neem is classified as a botanical insecticide.

Board Trap #5

Phosphine is a fumigant insecticide.

Board Trap #6

Imidacloprid is both a systemic insecticide and a neonicotinoid.

Board Trap #7

Pyrethrum is botanical, while pyrethroids are synthetic. Many students confuse these two terms.

Summary

Pesticides are substances used to control pests. Insecticides specifically target insect pests and may be classified according to target organism, mode of entry, mode of action, and origin. Major categories include contact insecticides, stomach poisons, fumigants, and systemic insecticides. Understanding pesticide classification is essential for proper selection, effective pest management, resistance prevention, and safe pesticide use. This topic is one of the most important areas in Entomology and Pest Management for the AgLE Board Examination.
