Slaughtering Procedures

Slaughtering procedures are the step-by-step operations used to convert live animals into carcasses for meat production.

These procedures ensure humane slaughter, hygienic meat production, efficient carcass handling, and safe meat processing.

Slaughtering follows a systematic process from live animal to carcass.

Importance of Proper Slaughtering Procedure

Proper slaughtering procedures are important to:

  • Reduce animal stress
  • Improve meat quality
  • Prevent contamination
  • Ensure humane killing
  • Maintain carcass cleanliness
Proper procedure protects meat quality and safety.

General Steps in Slaughtering

The general slaughter process includes:

  • Receiving
  • Resting / Lairage
  • Antemortem inspection
  • Stunning
  • Sticking / Bleeding
  • Skinning / Scalding
  • Evisceration
  • Splitting
  • Washing
  • Chilling
Slaughter follows a fixed sequence.

Receiving

Receiving is the arrival and acceptance of animals at the slaughter facility.

Animals are checked for:

  • Identification
  • Condition
  • Transport stress
  • Injuries
Receiving is the first control point.

Resting / Lairage

Resting or lairage is the holding of animals before slaughter to allow recovery from transport stress.

Purpose:

  • Reduce fatigue
  • Reduce stress
  • Allow animals to rest
  • Improve meat quality

Animals are commonly fasted for 12–24 hours before slaughter, with water still provided.

This reduces gut fill and lowers contamination risk during evisceration.

Rested and fasted animals produce cleaner carcasses.

Antemortem Inspection

Antemortem inspection is the examination of live animals before slaughter.

It ensures only healthy animals proceed for slaughter.

Only fit animals proceed to slaughter.

Stunning

Stunning is rendering the animal unconscious before bleeding.

Purpose:

  • Reduce pain
  • Reduce stress
  • Ensure humane slaughter

Methods:

  • Mechanical stunning
  • Electrical stunning
  • Gas stunning
Stunning makes slaughter humane.

Importance of Stunning

Proper stunning:

  • Prevents pain
  • Minimizes suffering
  • Improves worker safety
  • Improves bleeding efficiency
Stunning protects both animal and meat quality.

Sticking

Sticking is the act of inserting the knife after stunning to cut major blood vessels.

This must be done immediately after stunning to prevent recovery of consciousness.

Sticking must follow stunning within seconds.

Bleeding / Exsanguination

Bleeding or exsanguination is the removal of blood after sticking.

It is done by severing major blood vessels in the neck.

Purpose:

  • Cause death
  • Remove blood
  • Improve meat keeping quality
Proper bleeding is essential for good meat quality.

Importance of Bleeding

Proper bleeding:

  • Improves meat color
  • Reduces spoilage
  • Improves shelf life
  • Prevents blood retention
Incomplete bleeding lowers meat quality.

Skinning

Skinning is the removal of the hide or skin from the carcass.

Used in:

  • Cattle
  • Goats
  • Sheep
Skinning removes hide without contaminating meat.

Scalding and Dehairing

Scalding is dipping the carcass in hot water to loosen hair.

Dehairing is removal of hair after scalding.

Used mainly in:

  • Swine

The usual scalding temperature is around 60°C (140°F).

  • Too cold = hair remains attached
  • Too hot = skin is damaged and hair becomes harder to remove
Proper scalding is critical for clean dehairing.

Evisceration

Evisceration is the removal of internal organs from the carcass.

This includes:

  • Digestive organs
  • Respiratory organs
  • Reproductive organs
Evisceration removes internal organs for inspection.

Importance of Evisceration

Proper evisceration:

  • Prevents contamination
  • Allows organ inspection
  • Improves carcass cleanliness
Careless evisceration causes contamination.

Splitting

Splitting is dividing the carcass into halves.

Common in:

  • Cattle
  • Swine
Splitting improves handling and chilling.

Washing

Washing is cleaning the carcass to remove:

  • Blood
  • Dirt
  • Hair
  • Contaminants
Washing improves carcass cleanliness.

Chilling

Chilling is cooling the carcass after slaughter.

Purpose:

  • Reduce bacterial growth
  • Preserve meat
  • Improve shelf life
Chilling protects meat after slaughter.

Importance of Chilling

Proper chilling:

  • Slows spoilage
  • Preserves freshness
  • Improves meat safety
Chilling is critical after slaughter.

Dirty vs Clean Zones

Slaughter facilities are divided into zones to prevent contamination:

  • Dirty zone = stunning, bleeding, hide removal
  • Clean zone = evisceration, washing, chilling

This physical separation reduces cross-contamination.

Dirty and clean zones must be separated.

Hygienic Slaughter

A hygienic slaughter process requires:

  • Clean equipment
  • Clean water
  • Proper handling
  • Sanitation
  • Trained workers
Hygiene prevents carcass contamination.

Critical Control Points

Important control points in slaughter:

  • Antemortem inspection
  • Stunning
  • Sticking / Bleeding
  • Evisceration
  • Chilling
These steps strongly affect meat safety.

Slaughtering procedures focus on:

  • Humane killing
  • Hygienic processing
  • Contamination control
  • Carcass preservation
Proper slaughter procedure = clean and safe carcass.
