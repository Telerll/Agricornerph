Extension Approach

Definitions

1. Approach

  • An approach embraces the entire spectrum of the process.
  • It states a point of view, a philosophy, or an article of faith.
  • Within an approach, there can be several methods.

2. Method

  • A method is procedural.
  • It consists of a series of actions arranged logically for smooth operation.
  • It serves as a step-by-step guide for clients.

3. Technique

A technique is a specific trick, strategy, or individual artistry used by the teacher or extension worker.

Extension Approach

An Extension Approach is an organized and coherent combination of strategies and methods designed to make rural extension effective in a certain area.

Characteristics

  • Embodies the philosophy of a system
  • Spells the doctrine of the system
  • Determines the style and direction of action
  • Guides leadership, programs, methods, resources, and linkages

Strategies

Strategies are approaches and methods chosen or developed to reach a particular goal.

  • Define operational design
  • Guide implementation of policies
  • Help sponsoring organizations achieve objectives

Different Extension Approaches

  1. General Agricultural Extension Approach
  2. Commodity Specialized Approach
  3. Project Approach
  4. Farming Systems Development Approach
  5. Training and Visit Approach
  6. Cost-Sharing Approach
  7. Agricultural Extension Participatory Approach
  8. Educational Institution Approach

1. General Agricultural Extension Approach (GAE)

General Nature

  • Transfer of Technology (TOT)

Basic Assumption

  • Technology and information are available but are not being used by farmers.
  • If communicated properly, farming practices will improve.

Purpose

  • To help farmers increase production.

Program Planning

  • Controlled by the government.
  • National priorities guide planning.

Implementation

  • Large field staff assigned nationwide.
  • Demonstration plots are major techniques.

Advantages

  • Rapid communication from ministry to rural people.
  • Covers the entire nation.
  • Interprets government policies locally.

Disadvantages

  • Weak two-way communication.
  • Messages may not fit local conditions.
  • Field staff are not accountable to farmers.
  • Expensive and inefficient.

Measure of Success

  • Increase in national production of emphasized commodities.

2. Commodity Specialized Approach (CSA)

General Nature

  • Highly specialized.
  • Focuses on one export crop or farming aspect.

Basic Assumption

Productivity increases when extension is concentrated on one commodity together with research, marketing, credit, and input support.

Purpose

  • Increase production of a specific commodity.
  • Increase use of agricultural inputs.

Program Planning

  • Controlled by commodity organizations.

Implementation

  • Large field staff nationwide.
  • Demonstration plots are commonly used.

Advantages

  • Technology fits production problems.
  • Timely delivery of information.
  • Easier supervision and monitoring.
  • More cost-effective.

Disadvantages

  • Farmers’ interests may become secondary.
  • Ignores other farming concerns.
  • Commodity promotion may continue even if no longer nationally important.

Measure of Success

  • Increased yield and production of target crops.

3. Project Approach (PA)

General Nature

  • Fixed time period.
  • Large funding support.

Basic Assumption

Better results can be achieved in a short period using outside resources and intensive activities.

Purpose

  • Demonstrate what can be accomplished quickly.
  • Test alternative extension methods.

Program Planning

  • Managed by donors, government, or both.

Implementation

  • Strong project management.
  • Better facilities, transportation, and staffing.

Advantages

  • Allows experimentation of new methods.
  • Gives quick visible results.

Disadvantages

  • Usually short-term.
  • Costly.
  • Double standards may occur.
  • Programs often end when funds stop.

Measure of Success

  • Increased crop production and yield.

4. Farming Systems Development Approach (FSDA)

General Nature

  • Interdisciplinary approach.
  • Involves several scientific disciplines.

Basic Assumption

  • Technology suited for small farmers must be locally generated.

Purpose

  • Provide research-based solutions tailored to local farming systems.

Program Planning

  • Evolves slowly.
  • Considers climate, plants, animals, and people.

Implementation

  • Partnership between researchers, extension workers, and farmers.
  • Uses a systems approach.

Advantages

  • Greater local relevance.
  • Higher adoption rates.
  • Better communication.
  • Lower costs.

Disadvantages

  • Difficult multidisciplinary coordination.
  • Slow technology development.

Measure of Success

  • Continuous farmer adoption of developed technologies.

5. Training and Visit Approach (T & V)

General Nature

  • Highly disciplined and scheduled training system.

Basic Assumption

  • Extension workers are poorly trained and need regular updates and farmer visits.

Purpose

  • Increase production of specified crops.

Program Planning

  • Centralized.
  • Experts decide what and when to teach.

Implementation

  • Fortnightly training of extension workers.
  • Regular farmer visits.

Advantages

  • Strong supervision.
  • Improves worker discipline.
  • Keeps workers updated.

Disadvantages

  • Very expensive.
  • Weak two-way communication.
  • Technology may not fit farmers’ needs.
  • Rigid structure.

Measure of Success

  • Increased crop yield and production.

6. Cost-Sharing Approach (CSA)

Basic Assumption

  • Programs succeed better when beneficiaries share part of the cost.

Purpose

  • Make agricultural extension affordable and sustainable.

Program Planning

  • Shared funding among stakeholders.

Advantages

  • Greater local participation.
  • Better relevance to local needs.
  • Higher adoption rates.
  • Lower government cost.

Disadvantages

  • Harder for government control.
  • Poor farmers may struggle to contribute.

Measure of Success

  • Farmers’ willingness and ability to share costs.

7. Agricultural Extension Participatory Approach (AEPA)

General Nature

  • Farmers help determine extension programs.

Basic Assumption

  • Farmers possess valuable indigenous knowledge.
  • Participation improves extension efficiency.

Purpose

  • Increase production and improve rural quality of life.

Program Planning

  • Controlled locally.
  • Often led by farmers’ organizations.

Implementation

  • Meetings and discussions with farmers.
  • Joint planning with researchers and NGOs.

Advantages

  • Relevant and practical programs.
  • Strong participant relationships.
  • Lower cost.
  • Builds confidence among farmers.

Disadvantages

  • Difficult management and reporting.
  • Less central government control.
  • Political pressure may occur.

Measure of Success

  • Stakeholder participation.
  • Continuity of local organizations.
