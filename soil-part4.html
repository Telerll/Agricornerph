🏗 Part 4: Soil Texture & Structure

Soil Texture

  • Soil texture refers to the relative proportion of sand, silt, and clay particles in a soil.
  • It describes the fineness or coarseness of the soil material.
  • Texture is considered a basic and permanent physical property of soil because it does not easily change under normal field conditions.
  • It strongly affects soil water, aeration, drainage, nutrient retention, tillage, and plant root development.

Soil Separates

Sand

  • Sand is the largest among the major mineral separates.
  • It feels coarse and gritty when rubbed between the fingers.
  • Large pore spaces allow rapid movement of water and air.
  • Sandy soils are well-drained and well-aerated but have low water-holding capacity.
  • They also have low capacity to retain nutrients because of their small surface area.

Silt

  • Silt is intermediate in size between sand and clay.
  • It feels smooth, soft, and flour-like when dry.
  • It holds more water than sand but usually has less stickiness than clay.
  • Silty soils are generally fertile, but they may be easily eroded if not protected.

Clay

  • Clay is the smallest soil separate.
  • It feels sticky and plastic when wet and becomes hard when dry.
  • Clay soils have high surface area and high water-holding capacity.
  • They usually retain more nutrients than sandy soils.
  • However, too much clay may cause poor drainage, poor aeration, and difficulty in tillage.
Sand = large particles | Silt = medium particles | Clay = very fine particles

Importance of Soil Texture

  • Affects infiltration and percolation of water.
  • Influences water-holding capacity and plant-available water.
  • Determines ease of plowing, harrowing, and seedbed preparation.
  • Affects nutrient retention and fertilizer response.
  • Influences soil temperature and aeration.
  • Helps determine suitability of soil for certain crops.

Coarse-, Medium-, and Fine-Textured Soils

  • Coarse-textured soils are dominated by sand particles.
  • Medium-textured soils have a balanced mixture, often including loam and silt loam.
  • Fine-textured soils contain high proportions of clay.

Textural Classes

  • Sand
  • Loamy Sand
  • Sandy Loam
  • Loam
  • Silt Loam

Loam is often considered ideal for many crops because it has a good balance of sand, silt, and clay.

Soil Textural Triangle

  • The textural triangle is used to determine the textural class of a soil.
  • It is based on the percentages of sand, silt, and clay.
  • The total percentage of sand, silt, and clay must always equal 100%.
  • Once the percentages are known, the textural class can be identified on the triangle.

Field Estimation of Texture

  • Texture may be estimated in the field by feel.
  • Sandy soil feels gritty.
  • Silty soil feels smooth and silky.
  • Clayey soil feels sticky and plastic when wet.
  • The ribbon test is commonly used to help estimate clay content.

Soil Structure

  • Soil structure refers to the arrangement of individual soil particles into aggregates called peds.
  • While texture refers to particle size, structure refers to how the particles are grouped together.
  • Good structure improves movement of air and water and promotes better root growth.

Importance of Soil Structure

  • Improves infiltration and internal drainage.
  • Enhances soil aeration.
  • Facilitates root penetration and development.
  • Improves workability and tilth.
  • Reduces runoff, crusting, and erosion when stable.

Types of Soil Structure

Granular

  • Small, rounded, porous aggregates.
  • Common in surface soils, especially those rich in organic matter.
  • Usually favorable for plant growth.

Crumb

  • Similar to granular but more porous and friable.
  • Often associated with well-managed topsoil.

Blocky

  • Irregular block-like peds.
  • Common in subsoil horizons.
  • May be angular blocky or subangular blocky.

Prismatic

  • Vertical peds longer than wide with flat tops.
  • Usually found in subsoil horizons.

Columnar

  • Similar to prismatic but with rounded tops.
  • Often associated with sodium-affected soils.

Platy

  • Thin, flat, plate-like peds arranged horizontally.
  • May restrict downward movement of water and roots.

Structureless

  • No distinct peds or aggregates are present.
  • This may be single-grained as in loose sandy soils, or massive as in compact soils.

Grades of Soil Structure

  • Weak – aggregates are poorly formed and barely observable.
  • Moderate – aggregates are fairly well formed and distinct.
  • Strong – aggregates are durable, well developed, and clearly visible.

Classes of Soil Structure

  • Very fine
  • Fine
  • Medium
  • Coarse
  • Very coarse

Factors Affecting Soil Structure

  • Organic matter helps bind soil particles into stable aggregates.
  • Clay content influences the formation of peds.
  • Wetting and drying, freezing and thawing, and biological activity affect aggregation.
  • Tillage, compaction, and improper management may destroy structure.

Texture vs. Structure

  • Texture refers to the relative proportion of sand, silt, and clay.
  • Structure refers to the arrangement of these particles into aggregates.
  • Texture is a more permanent property, while structure can be improved or degraded by management.

Good Soil Condition for Crop Production

  • A productive soil should have favorable texture and good structure.
  • It should provide a good balance of water, air, and support for roots.
  • It should also allow easy tillage and efficient nutrient use.
Texture affects particle size composition, while structure affects arrangement of particles.
