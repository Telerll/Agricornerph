🏗 Part 4: Soil Texture & Structure

Soil Texture

  • Soil texture refers to the relative proportion of sand, silt, and clay particles in the soil.
  • It is one of the most important physical properties of soil.
  • Texture affects water holding capacity, drainage, aeration, nutrient retention, and tillage.

Soil Separates

Sand

  • Largest soil particles among the three separates.
  • Feels gritty.
  • Has good drainage and aeration.
  • Low water and nutrient holding capacity.

Silt

  • Medium-sized soil particles.
  • Feels smooth or flour-like when dry.
  • Has better water retention than sand.

Clay

  • Smallest soil particles.
  • Feels sticky and plastic when wet.
  • High water and nutrient holding capacity.
  • Poor drainage and aeration when excessive.

Importance of Soil Texture

  • Determines the ease of cultivation.
  • Affects root penetration and plant growth.
  • Influences water movement and retention.
  • Affects soil fertility and nutrient availability.

Textural Classes

  • Sand
  • Loamy Sand
  • Sandy Loam
  • Loam
  • Silt Loam
  • Silt
  • Sandy Clay Loam
  • Clay Loam
  • Silty Clay Loam
  • Sandy Clay
  • Silty Clay
  • Clay
Loam is considered ideal for many crops because it has balanced sand, silt, and clay.

Soil Textural Triangle

  • The soil textural triangle is used to determine the textural class of a soil.
  • It is based on the percentage of sand, silt, and clay.
  • The percentages of the three separates always total 100%.

Soil Structure

  • Soil structure is the arrangement of soil particles into aggregates or peds.
  • It affects porosity, aeration, permeability, water movement, and root growth.

Types of Soil Structure

Granular

  • Small rounded aggregates.
  • Common in surface soils rich in organic matter.

Blocky

  • Irregular, roughly cube-shaped aggregates.
  • Common in subsoil horizons.

Prismatic

  • Vertical aggregates longer than wide.
  • Usually found in subsoils.

Columnar

  • Similar to prismatic but with rounded tops.

Platy

  • Thin, flat, plate-like aggregates.
  • May restrict water movement and root penetration.

Structureless

  • No distinct aggregates.
  • Can be single-grained or massive.

Importance of Soil Structure

  • Improves water infiltration and drainage.
  • Enhances root development.
  • Promotes good aeration.
  • Reduces erosion and crusting.

Good Soil for Crop Production

  • Has favorable texture and structure.
  • Allows proper balance of air and water.
  • Supports easy root growth and nutrient absorption.
