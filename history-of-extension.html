History of Extension

The term extension was first used to describe adult education programs organized by Oxford and Cambridge universities in England starting in 1867.

These educational programs helped extend the work of universities beyond the campus and into neighboring communities.

The term was later formally adopted in the United States in connection with land-grant universities that were originally established as teaching institutions during the 1860s.

During the early twentieth century, the United Kingdom transferred responsibility for agricultural extension activities to the Ministry of Agriculture. These activities were then officially called Advisory Services.

The same term in English was used by most European countries as they developed and expanded similar advisory services within their respective ministries of agriculture.

A. Stirrings in 19th Century England

1840s

The term University Extension was first used in Britain.

1850s

Discussions began in Oxford and Cambridge about how they could serve the needs of the rapidly growing population in the industrial and urban areas.

1867

The first practical attempt was made in what was designated as University Extension.

It was intended for students enrolled in universities by bringing the university outside its campus to people who could not qualify for entry into the university.

1871

James Stuart became known as the Father of University Extension.

  • He was a Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge.
  • He appealed to the authorities of the University of Cambridge to organize Centers of Extension Lectures under university supervision.

1872

University of Cambridge adopted the system.

1876

University of London adopted the system.

1878

University of Oxford adopted the system.

1880s

The extension system became well-established and developed into what was called the Extension Movement.

B. Birth of Modern Agricultural Extension Service

Europe

1841

The Royal Agricultural Improvement Society, or RAIS, in Britain was founded.

1845

The outbreak of potato blight in Europe, particularly in Ireland, was caused by Phytophthora infestans.

The effect was most severe in Ireland where the predominantly peasant community relied on potatoes as staple food.

1847

The newly appointed British viceroy to Ireland, the Earl of Clarendon, urged the RAIS to appoint lecturers to travel around the distressed districts.

Their task was to inform and teach small farmers how to improve farming and grow other nutrient-rich crops.

1890s

Universities incorporated agricultural subjects in their lectures.

United States of America

1850s

a. Morrill Act of 1862

The Morrill Act of 1862 was signed by President Abraham Lincoln.

  • It led to the creation of Land Grant Colleges and Universities.
  • It supported the establishment of demonstration centers and experimental stations.

b. Farmers’ Institute Movements

These were organized meetings for farmers, usually lasting one to two days.

The speakers were professors from state colleges and universities.

1890

The Second Morrill Act extended the land-grant concept in other areas of the United States of America.

1914

The Smith-Lever Act established the Cooperative Extension Service.

Cooperative Extension Services became a tripartite cooperation among the federal government, state government, and local county government in association with the State Universities and Colleges as extension agencies.
