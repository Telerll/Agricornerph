Feeding Standards and Requirements

Feeding standards are established guidelines that indicate the amount of nutrients animals need for normal body functions, growth, reproduction, and production.

Nutrient requirements are the specific amounts of nutrients needed by an animal per day.

Feeding standards tell us how much to feed and what nutrients are needed.

Importance of Feeding Standards

Feeding standards are important because they help:

  • Prevent underfeeding
  • Prevent overfeeding
  • Improve efficiency
  • Reduce feed cost
  • Support health and productivity
Feeding standards make feeding more accurate and efficient.

Nutrient Requirements

Animals require nutrients for:

  • Maintenance
  • Growth
  • Reproduction
  • Lactation
  • Egg production
  • Work
Nutrient needs change depending on purpose and production stage.

Maintenance Requirement

Maintenance requirement is the amount of nutrients needed to keep the animal alive and healthy without gain or production.

It supports:

  • Body temperature
  • Breathing
  • Circulation
  • Digestion
  • Normal activity
Maintenance is always the first priority.

Growth Requirement

Growth requirement is the amount of nutrients needed for tissue development and body weight gain in growing animals.

It supports:

  • Tissue development
  • Bone growth
  • Muscle growth
  • Body weight gain
Young animals need more nutrients per unit body weight.

Reproductive Requirement

Reproductive requirement is the amount of nutrients needed for successful breeding and reproduction.

It supports:

  • Estrus
  • Conception
  • Fetal development
  • Sperm production
Poor nutrition lowers fertility.

Lactation Requirement

Lactation requirement is the amount of nutrients needed for milk production in lactating animals.

It supports:

  • Milk synthesis
  • Milk volume
  • Milk quality
Lactation is one of the highest nutrient-demand stages.

Egg Production Requirement

Egg production requirement is the amount of nutrients needed for egg formation and shell production in laying birds.

It supports:

  • Egg formation
  • Shell formation
  • Sustained laying performance

Important nutrients:

  • Energy
  • Protein
  • Calcium
  • Phosphorus
Egg production requires high calcium and balanced nutrition.

Work Requirement

Work requirement is the extra nutrient demand needed for muscular activity and physical labor.

It supports:

  • Muscular activity
  • Endurance
  • Energy expenditure

Examples:

  • Draft animals
  • Transport animals
Work increases energy requirement.

Factors Affecting Nutrient Requirements

Requirements vary depending on:

  • Species
  • Breed
  • Age
  • Sex
  • Body size
  • Activity
  • Environment
  • Health status
Nutrient needs are not the same for all animals.

Species Difference

Species difference means different animal species have different nutrient needs.

Examples:

  • Poultry need higher protein density
  • Ruminants need more fiber
  • Swine require high-energy concentrate diets
Species determines feeding strategy.

Age Difference

Age difference means nutrient requirements change as animals grow older.

  • Young animals need more nutrients for growth
  • Mature animals need more for maintenance and production
Age changes nutrient priority.

Production Stage

Production stage is the current physiological or productive condition of the animal.

Examples:

  • Growing
  • Breeding
  • Gestating
  • Lactating
  • Finishing
Feeding must match production stage.

Environmental Effects

Environmental effects are external conditions that influence nutrient needs.

Examples:

  • Heat stress reduces feed intake
  • Cold stress increases maintenance requirement
Environment changes feeding needs.

Feeding Standards Systems

Feeding standards systems are scientific references used to estimate nutrient requirements.

Common systems:

  • NRC — National Research Council
  • ARC — Agricultural Research Council
Feeding standards are scientific feeding guides.

Nutrient Density

Nutrient density is the concentration of nutrients per unit of feed.

High-producing animals need:

  • More nutrient-dense rations
High production requires high nutrient density.

Dry Matter Intake (DMI)

Dry Matter Intake (DMI) is the amount of feed consumed excluding water.

DMI is important because nutrient intake depends on how much dry matter is eaten.

Intake drives nutrient supply.

Metabolic Body Size

Metabolic body size is body weight expressed as BW0.75 and is used to estimate maintenance nutrient needs.

It explains why:

  • Smaller animals eat more relative to body size
  • Larger animals eat less relative to body size
Metabolic body size is used for maintenance calculations.

Flushing

Flushing is the practice of increasing feed intake 2–3 weeks before breeding to improve ovulation and conception rate.

Common in:

  • Sheep
  • Swine
Flushing improves reproductive performance before breeding.

Phase Feeding

Phase feeding is the adjustment of ration composition according to the animal’s growth or production stage.

Common examples:

  • Starter
  • Grower
  • Finisher

Common in:

  • Poultry
  • Swine
Phase feeding matches nutrient supply to changing requirements.

Negative Energy Balance

Negative energy balance is a condition where an animal uses more energy than it consumes.

Common in:

  • Early lactation dairy cows

Effects:

  • Body fat mobilization
  • Body condition loss
  • Production stress
Negative energy balance = output greater than intake.

Board Exam Master Concept

Animals do not all need the same ration.

Requirements change with:

  • Age
  • Production
  • Environment
Feeding must match requirement.
