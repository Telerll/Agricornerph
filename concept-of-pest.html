⚠️ Concept of Pest

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

Introduction

Agricultural production is constantly threatened by various organisms that interfere with crop growth, crop yield, crop quality, and farm profitability.

Not all organisms found in agricultural ecosystems are harmful. Many organisms are beneficial and help maintain ecological balance.

An organism becomes a pest only when it causes economic loss or interferes with human activities.

Understanding the concept of pest is fundamental to crop protection and Integrated Pest Management (IPM).

Definition of Pest

A pest is any organism that interferes with human activities or causes economic damage to crops, livestock, stored products, structures, or agricultural operations.

In agriculture, a pest is an organism whose activities reduce crop yield, crop quality, or economic returns.

Important Principle: Not All Organisms Are Pests

Many organisms found in farms are beneficial and should not be considered pests.

Beneficial Insects

  • Honey bees
  • Ladybird beetles
  • Dragonflies
  • Parasitoid wasps

Beneficial Vertebrates

  • Barn owls
  • Frogs
  • Lizards

Beneficial Microorganisms

  • Nitrogen-fixing bacteria
  • Mycorrhizal fungi

These organisms help maintain ecological balance and may assist in pollination, decomposition, nutrient cycling, or biological control.

When Does an Organism Become a Pest?

An organism becomes a pest when its population or activity causes measurable harm or economic loss.

  • Its population exceeds acceptable levels.
  • It causes measurable economic damage.
  • It interferes with agricultural production.
  • The cost of damage exceeds acceptable limits.

Economic Damage

Economic damage occurs when pest injury results in measurable reduction in yield, crop quality, market value, or farmer income.

Examples

  • Rice stem borers reducing grain yield
  • Golden apple snails destroying rice seedlings
  • Rats consuming and contaminating stored grains
  • Fruit flies damaging marketable fruits

Characteristics of Pests

High Reproductive Capacity

Many pests reproduce rapidly, allowing their populations to increase quickly under favorable conditions.

Adaptability

Pests can survive under different environmental conditions and may adjust to changes in cropping systems.

Efficient Feeding Ability

Pests can consume or damage important plant parts such as leaves, stems, roots, fruits, seeds, and grains.

Mobility

Many pests can spread rapidly within a field or migrate from one area to another.

Survival Mechanisms

Pests possess adaptations that allow them to survive unfavorable conditions such as drought, absence of host plants, or chemical exposure.

Major Groups of Agricultural Pests

1. Insect Pests

  • Stem borers
  • Aphids
  • Armyworms
  • Whiteflies
  • Fruit flies

2. Mites

  • Spider mites
  • Broad mites

3. Mollusks

  • Golden Apple Snail
  • Slugs

4. Rodents

  • Rice Field Rat
  • Roof Rat

5. Birds

  • Sparrows
  • Pigeons

6. Plant Pathogens

  • Fungi such as rice blast fungus
  • Bacteria such as bacterial leaf blight pathogen
  • Viruses such as rice tungro virus
  • Nematodes such as root-knot nematodes

7. Weeds

  • Barnyard grass
  • Cogon grass
  • Sedges

Pest Classification Based on Feeding Habit

1. Phytophagous Insects (Herbivores)

Phytophagous insects feed on living plants.

A. Leaf Feeders

  • Grasshoppers
  • Orthopterans
  • Most larvae of Lepidoptera

B. Leaf Miners

  • Agromyzid flies

C. Stem and Root Borers

  • Pyralid larvae
  • Cerambycid larvae

D. Root Feeders

  • Root grubs
  • Mole crickets

E. Gall Makers

  • Gall wasps

F. Sap Feeders

  • Aphids
  • Leafhoppers
  • Planthoppers
  • True bugs

2. Saprophagous Insects

Saprophagous insects feed on dead organic matter.

General Scavengers

  • Cockroaches

Dung Feeders

  • Scarab beetles

3. Mycetophagous Insects

Mycetophagous insects feed on fungi.

Fungus Feeders

  • Mycetophagid beetles

4. Zoophagous Insects (Carnivores)

Zoophagous insects feed on animals.

A. Parasites

Parasites live on or inside other animals.

  • Sucking lice
  • Parasitic wasps
  • Ichneumonidae
  • Braconidae

Blood Feeders

  • Mosquitoes

B. Predators

Predators capture and consume prey.

  • Ladybird beetles
  • Water striders
  • Dragonflies
  • Mantids
  • Lacewings

Pest Classification Based on Origin

A. Exotic Pest

Exotic pests are pests introduced from outside the locality.

Characteristics

  • Non-native species
  • Introduced intentionally or accidentally
  • Often lack natural enemies in the new area
  • May become serious invasive pests

Examples

  • Golden Apple Snail
  • Fall Armyworm

B. Endemic Pest

Endemic pests are local or native pest species.

Characteristics

  • Naturally occurring in the locality
  • Usually have established natural enemies
  • May become damaging when population increases

Pest Classification Based on Abundance or Number

A. Key Pest or Major Pest

A key pest is a pest that is regularly present and consistently causes economic damage.

Characteristics

  • Always present in the field
  • Usually occurs every cropping season
  • Often reaches high population levels
  • Regularly causes economic damage
  • Requires regular control or management

Examples

  • Rice stem borers
  • Corn borers
  • Green leafhopper
  • Diamondback moth
  • Mango hopper

B. Potential Pest or Minor Pest

A potential pest is usually an endemic species that is always found in the field at low population levels.

Characteristics

  • Usually present at low population
  • Does not normally cause economic damage
  • Controlled by many natural enemies
  • May become serious when ecological balance is disturbed

Examples

  • Rice whorl maggot
  • Rice skippers

Potential pests may become serious pests when natural enemies are destroyed. This phenomenon is called pest resurgence.

C. Occasional Pest

Occasional pests cause damage only under certain conditions.

Characteristics

  • Not always present
  • May appear suddenly
  • Often migratory
  • Outbreaks occur only occasionally
  • May appear after long drought or favorable weather conditions

Examples

  • Armyworms
  • Cutworms
  • Locusts

Storage Pest Classification According to Number

Major Insect Pest

Major storage pests are frequently found in abundance or in great number in stored products.

Examples

  • Rice weevil
  • Lesser grain borer
  • Angoumois grain moth

Pest Classification Based on Host Range

Monophagous Pest

Monophagous pests feed on only one host species.

Oligophagous Pest

Oligophagous pests feed on several closely related hosts.

Example: Diamondback moth feeds mainly on cruciferous crops.

Polyphagous Pest

Polyphagous pests feed on many unrelated host plants.

Examples

  • Armyworms
  • Cutworms
  • Fruit flies

Pest Status

Pest status refers to the importance of a pest in a specific area, cropping system, or agricultural situation.

Factors Affecting Pest Status

  • Population density
  • Environmental conditions
  • Host availability
  • Cropping system
  • Presence or absence of natural enemies

Factors Influencing Pest Outbreaks

Favorable Weather

Temperature, rainfall, and humidity may favor rapid pest multiplication.

Continuous Cropping

Continuous cropping provides a constant food supply for pests.

Monocropping

Planting large areas with a single crop encourages pest buildup.

Misuse of Pesticides

Improper pesticide use may destroy natural enemies and cause pest resurgence.

Lack of Natural Enemies

Without predators, parasitoids, and pathogens, pest populations may increase rapidly.

Economic Threshold Level (ETL)

Economic Threshold Level is the pest population level at which control measures should begin to prevent economic damage.

Importance of ETL

  • Reduces unnecessary pesticide application
  • Saves production costs
  • Protects beneficial organisms
  • Supports Integrated Pest Management

Economic Injury Level (EIL)

Economic Injury Level is the lowest pest population level capable of causing economic damage equal to the cost of control.

ALE Exam Trap: ETL is the action level, while EIL is the damage level. ETL comes before EIL.

Relationship to Integrated Pest Management

The concept of pest serves as the foundation of Integrated Pest Management.

IPM Emphasizes

  • Pest monitoring
  • Use of ETL and EIL
  • Conservation of natural enemies
  • Rational and need-based pest control

ALE Quick Facts

  • Pest = Any organism causing economic loss
  • Not all organisms are pests
  • Phytophagous = Plant feeders
  • Saprophagous = Feed on dead organic matter
  • Mycetophagous = Fungus feeders
  • Zoophagous = Animal feeders
  • Exotic Pest = Introduced species
  • Endemic Pest = Native species
  • Key Pest = Regularly causes economic damage
  • Potential Pest = Controlled by natural enemies
  • Occasional Pest = Causes damage only under certain conditions
  • Monophagous = One host
  • Oligophagous = Few related hosts
  • Polyphagous = Many hosts
  • ETL = Economic Threshold Level
  • EIL = Economic Injury Level
  • ETL comes before EIL

ALE High-Yield Exam Tips

Board Trap #1

Not every insect is a pest. Honey bees, ladybird beetles, dragonflies, and parasitoid wasps are beneficial insects.

Board Trap #2

Potential pests become major pests when natural enemies are destroyed. This is called pest resurgence.

Board Trap #3

ETL is the control point. EIL is the damage point. Control should begin at ETL before pest populations reach EIL.

Board Trap #4

  • Phyto = Plants
  • Sapro = Dead organic matter
  • Myceto = Fungi
  • Zoo = Animals

Board Trap #5

Polyphagous pests are generally harder to manage because they can survive on many different host plants.

Summary

A pest is any organism that causes economic damage or interferes with agricultural production. Pests may include insects, mites, mollusks, rodents, birds, pathogens, and weeds. They can be classified based on feeding habit, origin, abundance, and host range. Understanding pest concepts, ETL, EIL, pest outbreaks, and pest classifications provides the foundation for effective crop protection and Integrated Pest Management. This topic is one of the most frequently tested areas in Entomology and Pest Management in the AgLE Board Examination.
