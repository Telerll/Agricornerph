I. What is Entomology?

Entomology is the branch of zoology that deals with the scientific study of insects. In agriculture, entomology focuses on insects that affect crop production either as pests or beneficial organisms.

  • Insect structure
  • Classification
  • Development
  • Behavior
  • Ecology
  • Pest status
  • Pest management

II. Concept of Pest

A pest is any organism that interferes with human activities and causes economic loss.

  • Insects
  • Mites
  • Nematodes
  • Mollusks
  • Rodents
  • Birds
  • Weeds
  • Pathogens

III. Pest Classification

Based on Origin

  • Exotic Pest — introduced from another country or locality.
  • Endemic Pest — native or locally occurring pest.

Based on Population

  • Major Pest — causes serious economic damage and is usually present every season.
  • Minor Pest — present at low population and usually controlled naturally.
  • Occasional Pest — causes damage only under certain conditions.

Based on Feeding Habit

  • Phytophagous — plant feeders.
  • Saprophagous — feed on dead organic matter.
  • Mycetophagous — feed on fungi.
  • Zoophagous — animal feeders.

IV. Insect Structure

Head

The head contains the eyes, antennae, and mouthparts. It functions mainly in feeding, vision, and sensory perception.

Thorax

The thorax is the locomotory center of the insect. It bears the legs and wings and is divided into prothorax, mesothorax, and metathorax.

Abdomen

The abdomen contains digestive organs, reproductive organs, and spiracles. It is important in reproduction, respiration, and excretion.

V. Important Insect Structures

Eyes

  • Simple eyes or ocelli — detect light intensity.
  • Compound eyes — form images and detect movement.

Antennae

  • Smell
  • Touch
  • Orientation
  • Detection of chemicals and host plants

Mouthparts

  • Chewing — grasshoppers and beetles.
  • Piercing-sucking — aphids and leafhoppers.
  • Siphoning — butterflies.
  • Sponging — houseflies.

VI. Types of Legs

  • Cursorial — running; example: cockroach.
  • Saltatorial — jumping; example: grasshopper.
  • Raptorial — grasping; example: praying mantis.
  • Natatorial — swimming; example: water beetles.
  • Fossorial — digging; example: mole cricket.

VII. Types of Wings

  • Membranous — dragonflies.
  • Tegmina — grasshoppers.
  • Elytra — beetles.
  • Hemelytra — true bugs.
  • Scaly wings — butterflies and moths.
  • Fringed wings — thrips.

VIII. Classification and Identification

  • Classification — arranging organisms into groups.
  • Nomenclature — scientific naming of organisms.
  • Identification — determining the identity of an organism.
  • Taxonomy — science of classification.

IX. Important Insect Orders

  • Orthoptera — grasshoppers and crickets.
  • Hemiptera — leafhoppers, planthoppers, and rice bugs.
  • Coleoptera — beetles and weevils.
  • Lepidoptera — moths and butterflies.
  • Diptera — flies.
  • Hymenoptera — ants, bees, and wasps.

X. Metamorphosis

  • Ametabolous: Egg → Young → Adult.
  • Incomplete Metamorphosis: Egg → Nymph → Adult.
  • Complete Metamorphosis: Egg → Larva → Pupa → Adult.

XI. Larval Types

  • Eruciform — caterpillar.
  • Scarabaeiform — white grub.
  • Campodeiform — active predatory larva.
  • Vermiform — maggot-like larva.

XII. Pupal Types

  • Exarate — appendages free.
  • Obtect — appendages attached.
  • Coarctate — pupa enclosed in puparium.

XIII. Reproduction

  • Sexual reproduction — most common in insects.
  • Parthenogenesis — development without fertilization.
  • Fecundity — number of eggs produced.
  • Fertility — ability of eggs to hatch.

XIV. Mollusks as Agricultural Pests

The Golden Apple Snail, Pomacea canaliculata, is a major pest of rice. It attacks young seedlings and lays bright pink egg masses above the water surface.

Board Tip: Golden Apple Snail = pink eggs above water.

XV. Rodents and Other Vertebrate Pests

  • Rice Field Rat — Rattus argentiventer.
  • Roof Rat — Rattus rattus.
  • Norway Rat — Rattus norvegicus.
  • House Mouse — Mus musculus.

XVI. Integrated Pest Management

Integrated Pest Management combines cultural, mechanical, physical, biological, genetic, legislative, and chemical control methods to keep pest populations below economic injury levels.

  • ETL — Economic Threshold Level or action level.
  • EIL — Economic Injury Level or damage level.

XVII. Pesticides

  • Insecticides — control insects.
  • Herbicides — control weeds.
  • Fungicides — control fungi.
  • Rodenticides — control rodents.
  • Molluscicides — control snails and slugs.

XVIII. Insecticide Classification

  • Contact poisons — kill by direct contact.
  • Stomach poisons — must be eaten.
  • Fumigants — enter through respiration.
  • Systemic insecticides — absorbed by plants.

XIX. Calibration and Pesticide Calculations

  • Application Rate = Volume Sprayed ÷ Area Sprayed
  • Area Sprayed = Swath × Distance ÷ 10,000
  • Commercial Formulation = Required ai ÷ % ai

Final Summary

Agricultural Entomology integrates insect biology, classification, morphology, development, pest status, economic importance, pest management, pesticide use, crop-pest relationships, and biological control. Understanding the major pests of important crops together with their natural enemies and management strategies forms the foundation of modern crop protection and represents one of the highest-yield subject areas in the AgLE Board Examination.
