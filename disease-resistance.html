🛡️ Mechanisms of Disease Resistance

Crop Protection Reviewer

Definition of Disease Resistance

Disease resistance is the ability of a plant to prevent, suppress, or reduce the growth and development of pathogens. Resistant plants possess natural or induced defense mechanisms that help protect them from infection and disease development.

  • Protects plants from pathogens
  • Reduces disease severity
  • Limits pathogen growth and spread
  • Improves crop survival and yield

A. Pre-Formed Resistance

Pre-formed resistance refers to defense mechanisms already present in plants even before pathogen attack.

I. External Physical Barriers

1. Trichomes

Trichomes are hair-like structures found on leaves and stems. They protect plants by preventing pathogen attachment and reducing insect feeding.

2. Cuticle

The cuticle is a waxy protective covering on plant surfaces that acts as both a physical and chemical barrier against pathogens.

  • Prevents pathogen penetration
  • Reduces water accumulation
  • Limits fungal spore germination

3. Stomata

Stomata are openings on leaves that regulate gas exchange. Some pathogens enter plants through stomata.

4. Root Cap and Mucilage

Root caps and mucilage protect root tissues from mechanical injury and pathogen invasion.

5. Thick Seed Coat and Epidermis

Thick coverings protect seeds and plant surfaces against pathogen penetration and infection.

6. Waxy Layers

Waxy layers cause water droplets to run off plant surfaces, reducing opportunities for infection.

II. Internal Physical Barriers

  • Suberized tissues
  • Lignified tissues
  • Cellulosic walls
  • Middle lamella
  • Deposition of gums, resins, and tannins
  • Deposition of silicic acid

These internal structures strengthen plant tissues and help block pathogen movement inside the plant.

III. Defense Through Lack of Essential Factors

Some plants resist pathogens because they lack substances or structures required by the pathogen for successful infection.

  • Lack of recognition between host and pathogen
  • Absence of host receptors
  • Lack of essential nutrients for pathogens

IV. Pre-Formed Chemical Factors

1. Phenolics

Phenolic compounds are toxic to many pathogens and help inhibit disease development.

2. Tannins

Tannins possess fungitoxic properties and inhibit pathogen enzymes.

3. Saponins

Saponins damage fungal cell membranes and alter membrane permeability.

4. Resistance Enzymes

Plants produce enzymes that degrade pathogen structures and strengthen plant defenses.

B. Induced Resistance

Induced resistance develops only after pathogen attack or exposure to inducing agents.

I. Active Defense Mechanisms

Plants activate defense reactions when pathogens attempt to infect host tissues.

  • Production of antimicrobial compounds
  • Cell wall strengthening
  • Activation of defense proteins

II. Mechanical Barriers to Pathogenesis

Plants may form corky tissues around infected areas to isolate pathogens and prevent further spread.

III. Hypersensitivity Reaction

Hypersensitivity is the rapid localized death of host cells around the infection site.

  • Confines pathogens to small areas
  • Limits spread of infection
  • May lead to pathogen death

Phytoalexins

Phytoalexins are antimicrobial compounds produced by plants in response to infection.

IV. Plant Immunization

Plants do not naturally produce antibodies like animals, but genetic engineering allows plants to produce antibody-like proteins called plantibodies.

V. Other Post-Infectional Toxic Substances

Plants accumulate toxic compounds around infected tissues to help inhibit pathogen growth.

  • Phenolic glucosides
  • Polyphenols
  • Flavonoids
  • Anthocyanins
  • Coumarin derivatives

These compounds become toxic to pathogens and help strengthen disease resistance.
