Cooperatives Final Integration

Complete Summary Reviewer

1. Meaning of Cooperative

A cooperative is an autonomous association of persons united voluntarily to meet their common economic, social, and cultural needs through a jointly-owned and democratically-controlled enterprise.

2. Characteristics of Cooperatives

  • Members are united by at least one common interest.
  • Members aim to improve their economic and social condition.
  • Members use a jointly owned and operated organization.
  • Resources are pooled to provide goods and services for members.

3. Main Concepts of Cooperation

  • Social – cooperation as a means of social reform.
  • Sociopolitical – cooperation improves the social life of members.
  • Legalistic – cooperative as a legally recognized organization.
  • Economic – cooperative promotes business efficiency and social improvement.

4. Seven Principles of Cooperatives

  1. Voluntary and Open Membership
  2. Democratic Member Control
  3. Member Economic Participation
  4. Autonomy and Independence
  5. Education, Training, and Information
  6. Cooperation Among Cooperatives
  7. Concern for Community

5. Types of Cooperatives

Cooperatives may be classified based on sector, organization level, membership, capital structure, services performed, legal personality, geographical coverage, and adherence to cooperative principles.

6. Cooperative Core Functions

  • Marketing – extends control of members’ products through processing, distribution, and sale.
  • Purchasing – provides affordable supplies and goods.
  • Service – provides needed services to members.

7. Cooperative Management and Advantages

Sound cooperative management means efficient and effective use of cooperative resources to serve members while following cooperative principles.

Advantages include economies of scale, reduced transaction costs, reduced uncertainty, avoidance of linked markets, and access to additional services and resources.

8. Accounting in Cooperatives

Accounting is called the language of business. It records, summarizes, and reports financial information of the cooperative.

Important principles include money as common denominator, business entity, value equals cost, dual aspect principle, and accrual principle.

9. Bookkeeping

Bookkeeping involves recording business transactions through accounts, debit and credit, journal, ledger, and trial balance.

10. Cooperative Financing

Cooperative capital is classified into:
  • Equity Capital – capital contributions from members.
  • Loan Capital – borrowings from outside creditors.

11. Reserves

  • Valuation Reserves – for bad debts, depreciation, and obsolescence.
  • Liquidity Reserves – for losses, emergencies, and liabilities.
  • Capital Reserves – for working capital and investments.

12. Financial Ratios

Financial ratios help evaluate liquidity, solvency, leverage, surplus generation, efficiency, loan collection, and institutional capital strength of the cooperative.

Final Key Idea

A cooperative is both a business organization and a social institution. It must be financially sound, democratically controlled, efficiently managed, and focused on improving the lives of its members.
