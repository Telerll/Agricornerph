Cooperatives Final Integration

Complete Summary Reviewer

I. Foundations and Legal Definitions

1. Meaning of a Cooperative

A cooperative is an autonomous association of persons who voluntarily unite to address their common economic, social, and cultural needs and aspirations through a jointly owned and democratically controlled enterprise.

2. Characteristics of Cooperatives

Common Bond
Members are connected through at least one shared interest, occupation, or objective.

Socio-Economic Goal
Members seek improvement in both their economic condition and social welfare.

Joint Enterprise
The cooperative is jointly owned, operated, and managed by its members.

Pooled Resources
Human and financial resources are combined to reduce dependence on middlemen and improve access to goods and services.

3. Four Core Dimensions of Cooperation

Social Dimension
Cooperatives promote social justice, equity, and poverty reduction.

Sociopolitical Dimension
They strengthen grassroots democracy and encourage active community participation.

Legalistic Dimension
Cooperatives operate as separate legal entities recognized and protected by law, particularly under RA 9520 or the Philippine Cooperative Code.

Economic Dimension
They promote efficiency, economies of scale, and mutual financial growth.

II. International Cooperative Principles

1. Voluntary and Open Membership

Membership is open to all individuals capable of using the cooperative’s services and willing to accept membership responsibilities without discrimination.

2. Democratic Member Control

Cooperatives are democratic organizations controlled by their members.

One Member = One Vote

This differs from ordinary corporations where voting depends on the number of shares owned.

3. Member Economic Participation

Members contribute equitably to the capital of the cooperative and democratically control its use.

  • Limited interest on share capital
  • Patronage refunds based on transaction volume

4. Autonomy and Independence

Cooperatives are self-help organizations controlled by members. Even when dealing with governments or external funders, democratic control must remain with the members.

5. Education, Training, and Information

Cooperatives provide education and training to members, officers, managers, and employees to ensure effective participation and development.

6. Cooperation Among Cooperatives

Cooperatives strengthen the movement by working together through local, national, regional, and international structures.

7. Concern for Community

Cooperatives support sustainable community development through member-approved policies and programs.

III. Classification and Typologies

1. Classification Parameters

Sector

  • Agricultural
  • Credit
  • Consumer
  • Transport
  • Health
  • Electric cooperatives

Organizational Level

  • Primary Cooperative — individual persons are the members
  • Secondary Cooperative — cooperatives are the members
  • Tertiary Cooperative — federations of federations

Membership Type

  • Regular Members — members with voting rights and full privileges
  • Associate Members — members with limited privileges and usually no voting rights

Other Classifications

  • Capital-heavy or asset-heavy cooperatives
  • Single-purpose or multipurpose cooperatives
  • Barangay, municipal, provincial, regional, or national coverage

2. Cooperative Core Functional Typologies

Marketing Cooperatives
These cooperatives strengthen member control over agricultural products by aggregating, processing, packaging, distributing, and selling products to eliminate exploitative middlemen.

Purchasing or Consumer Cooperatives
They purchase goods and production inputs in bulk to lower member costs, such as fertilizers, feeds, seeds, and grocery items.

Service Cooperatives
These cooperatives provide specialized services such as housing, medical care, transportation, insurance, and electricity.

IV. Management, Economics, and Financial Accounting

1. Agribusiness Management Advantages

Economies of Scale
Bulk operations reduce average cost per unit.

Reduced Transaction Costs
Lower expenses related to transportation, communication, and negotiation.

Reduced Market Uncertainty
Provides stable buyers for farmers and reliable suppliers for consumers.

Avoidance of Linked Markets
Prevents abusive practices where traders tie expensive loans to low crop buying prices.

2. Foundational Accounting Principles

Money as Common Denominator
Only transactions measurable in monetary value are recorded.

Business Entity Concept
The cooperative’s financial records are separate from members’ personal finances.

Historical Cost Principle
Assets are recorded according to original acquisition cost.

Dual Aspect Principle

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Accrual Principle
Revenues and expenses are recognized when earned or incurred, regardless of actual cash movement.

3. Bookkeeping Essentials

Accounts
Systematic records showing increases and decreases in assets, liabilities, and equity.

Debit and Credit
Debit refers to the left side of an account while credit refers to the right side.

Journal
The book of original entry where transactions are recorded chronologically.

Ledger
The book where journal entries are classified into individual accounts.

Trial Balance
A summary of account balances used to verify equality of debits and credits.

V. Capitalization, Reserves, and Financial Performance

1. Financial Capitalization Structure

Equity Capital
Funds owned by the cooperative, including share capital, membership fees, and retained earnings.

Loan Capital
Borrowed funds obtained from banks, government agencies, and creditors, usually with interest obligations.

2. Operational Fund Reserves

Valuation Reserves
Funds reserved for bad debts, depreciation, and asset value decline.

Liquidity Reserves
Cash reserves maintained for emergencies and sudden withdrawals.

Capital or Statutory Reserves
Long-term reserves intended for expansion, asset acquisition, and institutional protection.

3. Core Financial Ratios Checklist

Current Ratio

Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

Measures short-term liquidity. Ideal target is 1.25 to 1.5.

Quick Ratio

(Current Assets − Inventory − Prepaids) ÷ Current Liabilities

Measures emergency liquidity capacity.

Solvency Ratio

(Members' Equity ÷ Total Assets) × 100

Measures financial independence.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio

Total Liabilities ÷ Members' Equity

Measures dependence on borrowed funds.

Net Surplus Margin

(Net Surplus ÷ Total Revenue) × 100

Measures operational efficiency.

Return on Assets

(Net Surplus ÷ Total Assets) × 100

Evaluates asset utilization efficiency.

Collection Efficiency Ratio

(Amount Collected ÷ Amount Due) × 100

Target collection efficiency is 95% or higher.

Past Due Ratio

(Past Due Loans ÷ Total Loan Portfolio) × 100

Should generally remain below 10%.

Final Integration Takeaway

A cooperative is unique because it functions both as a business enterprise and a social institution.

Unlike ordinary corporations that primarily focus on profit generation, cooperatives are established to improve the economic and social welfare of their members through collective action, democratic participation, and shared ownership.

To remain stable and sustainable, cooperatives must maintain adequate liquidity, manage debts responsibly, strengthen institutional capital, ensure efficient collection systems, minimize past due accounts, control operational expenses, and generate sufficient net surplus.

Financial ratios such as Current Ratio, Quick Ratio, Solvency Ratio, Debt-to-Equity Ratio, Net Surplus Margin, Return on Assets, Collection Efficiency Ratio, and Past Due Ratio are important tools used to evaluate the financial health, efficiency, and long-term viability of the cooperative.

Ultimately, the success of a cooperative depends on active member participation, transparent leadership, responsible financial management, and sustained commitment to cooperative values and principles.
