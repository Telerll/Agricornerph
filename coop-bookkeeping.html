Bookkeeping Reviewer

1. Account

An account refers to a record used to summarize and classify financial transactions related to a specific item or activity.

2. Debit and Credit

The left side of an account is known as the debit side, while the right side is referred to as the credit side.

3. Ledger

A ledger is a collection of accounts used to organize and classify accounting information.

4. Journal

The journal is the chronological record of accounting transactions showing which accounts are debited and credited.

5. Trial Balance

A trial balance is a listing of account titles together with their balances at a specific time, showing debit balances in one column and credit balances in another.

Assets

Assets provide information regarding all properties and resources owned by the cooperative and their corresponding values.

These may include cash on hand, bank deposits, land, buildings, machinery, tools, furniture, motor vehicles, shop stocks, and similar resources.

1. Current Assets

These are properties acquired mainly for trading purposes and assets expected to circulate quickly, such as cash and amounts collectible from customers.

2. Cash

Cash consists of funds that are immediately available for use without restrictions.

3. Accounts Receivable

These are amounts due to the company, commonly coming from customers who purchased on credit.

4. Inventory

Inventory refers to the total stock of tangible goods or merchandise owned by the cooperative.

5. Prepaid Expenses and Deferred Charges

These represent assets, usually intangible in nature, whose benefits will be consumed or expire in the near future.

6. Fixed Assets

Fixed assets are long-term properties used in business operations such as buildings, land, vehicles, machinery, and furniture.

7. Investments

Investments include shares in cooperative corporations as well as investments in bonds, securities, debentures, and related financial instruments.

Liabilities

Liabilities represent all obligations, debts, and borrowed funds used to finance the assets of the cooperative.

Three Categories of Liabilities

  1. Current liabilities
  2. Long-term liabilities
  3. Share capital (members’ equity)

Current Liabilities

These are short-term obligations or trade financing expected to be paid within one year.

Long-term Liabilities

Long-term liabilities refer to financing arrangements payable beyond one year. Medium-term loans ranging from one to three years may sometimes be classified separately.

Share Capital

Share capital refers to the funds contributed by members. Members’ equity may include different types of funds such as share capital, reserves, surplus, or losses generated through operations.

Liquidity and Financial Measurement

Liquidity is determined by comparing current assets or liquid resources with current liabilities or short-term obligations.

Cash and bank deposits are considered highly liquid because they are readily available for payment to creditors. Receivables and inventories are considered less liquid assets.

Net Working Capital

Net working capital represents the excess of current assets over current liabilities.

Formula:
Net working capital = Current assets − Current liabilities

Quick (Acid) Ratio

The quick ratio measures the ability of the cooperative to settle short-term obligations using immediately available assets.

Only quick assets such as cash, bank deposits, and receivables are considered in the computation.

Formula:
Quick ratio = Current assets (less stocks) ÷ Current liabilities

A favorable liquidity position exists when quick assets are at least equal to current liabilities.

Assessing the Financial Strength of the Cooperative

A sound financial strategy should:

  • Maintain adequate short-term and long-term funds to avoid financial difficulties or bankruptcy
  • Promote efficient use and circulation of working capital
  • Encourage proper investment planning and a strong capital structure

The balance sheet provides useful information regarding the strengths and weaknesses of the cooperative’s financial condition.

Short-term Liquidity

Liquidity refers to the capability of the cooperative to pay short-term obligations that mature within one year.

It is evaluated by comparing current assets against current liabilities.

Long-term Capital Structure

Long-term capital such as members’ equity and share capital serves as the foundation of a stable and successful cooperative.

The capital structure reflected in the balance sheet helps determine whether the cooperative is financially strong or weak.

Solvency

Solvency refers to the financial stability of the cooperative based on how its assets are financed.

Financing may come from:

  • External lenders or creditors
  • Members’ equity and share capital

Solvency Ratio Formula:
Solvency =
(Members’ funds: share capital + reserves + surplus) × 100 ÷ Total liabilities (total financing)

Indebtedness

Indebtedness is the opposite of solvency and indicates the portion of the cooperative’s total capital financed through debts owed to creditors and banks.

It is commonly expressed as a percentage ratio.

Indebtedness Ratio Formula:
Indebtedness % =
(Total liabilities − members’ funds) × 100 ÷ Total liabilities (total financing)

Value of the Cooperative

Net worth represents the cash value of the cooperative attributable to its members at a specific date.

Net worth is equivalent to members’ equity or owner’s funds, which may include:

  • Share capital
  • Reserves
  • Accumulated surplus

It also represents the amount that members may receive if the cooperative is liquidated on the balance sheet date.

Net Worth Formula:
Net worth = Equity (members’ funds)
= Share capital + reserves + accumulated surplus
