Basic Elements of Communication

1. Source

An extension worker should have credibility because this determines whether the audience will accept or reject the idea.

  • Adequate knowledge of the subject matter
  • Knowledge of effective communication channels
  • Genuine concern for the welfare of the audience
  • Clear preparation and presentation of the message
  • Use of language understood by the receivers
  • Awareness of time limitations

Characteristics of the Source

Homophily refers to the degree by which a receiver perceives the source as similar in age, sex, language, beliefs, values, and background.

Credibility refers to the receiver’s perception of the believability of the source in a particular situation.

Dimensions of Credibility

  • Character: honesty, trustworthiness, reliability, friendliness, and warmth.
  • Competence: intelligence, experience, skillfulness, and brightness.
  • Composure: self-confidence, dignity, poise, and calmness.
  • Dynamism: boldness, aggressiveness, and extroversion.

2. Message

The message should contain the desired purpose or outcome such as change in behavior, knowledge, skills, or attitude.

Message treatment refers to how the message is organized and presented so it becomes understandable and meaningful.

Message Factors

  • Code: symbols used in communication that both sender and receiver understand.
  • Content: the idea or substance selected for communication.
  • Treatment: the arrangement and presentation of materials to make them meaningful to the receiver.

Categories of Non-verbal Communication

  • Physical: facial expressions, tone of voice, touch, smell, and body movements.
  • Aesthetic: communication through music, dancing, painting, and sculpturing.
  • Signs: signal flags, salutes, horns, and sirens.
  • Symbolic: religious, status, or ego-building symbols.

Message Meaning and Understanding

  • Meanings exist in people.
  • Meanings change as experiences change.
  • No two people have exactly the same meanings.
  • Denotative meaning refers to literal meaning.
  • Connotative meaning refers to personal meaning associated with experience.

3. Channels

Channels refer to the various methods available to communicators in reaching their audience.

  • Visual
  • Spoken
  • Written
  • Combination of methods

Dimensions of Channels

  • Seen
  • Heard
  • Touched
  • Smelled
  • Tasted

Types of Channels

  • Interpersonal channels
  • Mediated channels
  • Mass media channels
  • Folk media channels

Interpersonal Channels

Gatekeeper refers to someone who controls the flow of information and decides what information to transmit and to whom.

Opinion Leader refers to a person approached by others for advice and perceived as credible, influential, and authoritative.

4. Receiver

The audience in extension education is composed of farmers, homemakers, and youth.

As receivers of the message, they differ in intelligence, beliefs, experience, social standing, and interpretation of messages.

Receiver Characteristics

Psychological orientation includes:

  • Selective exposure
  • Selective perception
  • Selective remembering
  • Selective acceptance

Selective Processes

  • Selective Exposure: receivers expose themselves only to information that agrees with or supports their existing behavior.
  • Selective Perception: receivers notice or assign meaning only to messages that serve a purpose, reinforce mood, fit their cognitive structure, or are meaningful to them.
  • Selective Retention: receivers remember only information that supports or agrees with their attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors.
  • Selective Discussion: receivers discuss only information that may interest them and their listeners.

5. Feedback

Feedback is information sent or fed back by the receiver, intentionally or unintentionally, to the source.

Feedback makes communication a two-way process and helps determine whether the message was understood or whether modifications are needed.

Types of Response

  • Cognitive Effect: knowledge, awareness, thought, and skills.
  • Neutralization: shifting an existing attitude to the neutral zone.
  • Boomerang Effect: a shift opposite to the intended direction.
  • Conservation: maintenance of an existing attitude.
  • Attitude Reinforcement: strengthening an existing attitude.

Barriers to Effective Communication

1. Perception

People interpret messages differently depending on their experiences, beliefs, culture, education, and background.

The same message may have different meanings to different people.

2. Language or Jargon

The use of difficult, technical, scientific, or unfamiliar words creates confusion.

Communication becomes ineffective when receivers cannot understand the terms used.

3. Semantics

Semantics refers to misunderstanding caused by the meaning of words.

Some words may have multiple meanings depending on context, culture, or local usage.

4. Inflections

Inflections refer to tone, stress, pitch, or emphasis used while speaking.

A wrong tone may change the meaning of the message and may cause misunderstanding.

5. Personal Interests

People tend to listen only to information related to their own needs, benefits, or interests.

Messages that do not relate to their concerns may be ignored.

6. Emotions

Strong emotions such as anger, fear, excitement, anxiety, or sadness can affect understanding and interpretation.

Emotional receivers may misinterpret messages or fail to understand them clearly.

7. Preconceived Notions

People may already have existing beliefs, opinions, or biases before receiving messages.

These prior beliefs may prevent them from accepting new ideas.

8. Attention Problems

Communication fails when receivers are distracted, tired, uninterested, or mentally absent.

Being physically present does not always mean that the receiver is mentally attentive.

9. Wordiness

Using too many unnecessary words makes communication confusing, lengthy, and boring.

Clear, short, and direct explanations are usually more effective.

10. Inferences

Inferences are conclusions made based on assumptions rather than the actual intended meaning.

Receivers may assume meanings that the sender did not intend.
