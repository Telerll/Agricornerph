Agricultural Extension and Communication

Overview

Agricultural Extension and Communication is an educational process that helps farmers, homemakers, rural youth, and communities improve their knowledge, attitudes, skills, productivity, livelihood, and quality of life through communication, participation, and technology transfer.

History and Development of Extension

Agricultural extension started in 19th century England and later spread to Europe and the United States. In the Philippines, extension developed during the Spanish, American, and post-war periods and eventually expanded through DA agencies, SUCs, NGOs, LGUs, and private organizations.

Extension Terminologies

  • Extension Teaching – educating rural people
  • Learning Experience – activities producing behavioral change
  • Extension Model – framework for extension work
  • Extension Strategy – plan for achieving objectives
  • Extension Method – specific communication technique

Agricultural Extension Education

Agricultural Extension Education is an applied science using education, communication, leadership, and community organization to improve agricultural practices and rural development.

Objectives of Agricultural Extension

  • Increase agricultural productivity
  • Improve income and quality of life
  • Develop skills and leadership
  • Encourage adoption of innovations
  • Promote rural and community development

Philosophy and Principles of Extension

Extension starts where the people are and uses their experiences, needs, and available resources. People learn by doing, and extension works with and through people.

Principles

  • Two-way process linkage
  • Cooperation with agencies

The Adoption Process

Innovation

An idea, practice, or technology perceived as new.

Adoption

Acceptance and continuous use of an innovation.

Characteristics Affecting Adoption

  • Relative Advantage
  • Compatibility
  • Complexity
  • Trialability
  • Observability

Diffusion Process

Diffusion is the spread of innovations through communication channels over time.

Stages

  1. Awareness
  2. Interest
  3. Evaluation
  4. Trial
  5. Adoption

Innovation-Decision Process Theory

  1. Knowledge
  2. Persuasion
  3. Decision
  4. Implementation
  5. Confirmation

Individual Innovativeness Theory

Innovativeness is the degree to which a person adopts innovations earlier than others.

Modernization refers to movement from traditional to more advanced and technological ways of living.

Categories of Adopters

  1. Innovators
  2. Early Adopters
  3. Early Majority
  4. Late Majority
  5. Laggards

Strategies to Effect Adoption

  1. Extension Worker Intervention
  2. Progressive Farmer Strategy
  3. Mass Marketing
  4. Follower-Farmer Strategy
  5. Selective Farmer-to-Farmer

Explanations for Rejection / Adoption of Technology

  • Individual-Blame Hypothesis – blames personal limitations
  • System-Blame Hypothesis – blames social and institutional problems
  • Pro-Innovation Bias – assumes innovation is always correct and failure is the farmer’s fault
