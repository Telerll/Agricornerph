Four Major Paradigms of Agricultural Extension

Overview

The terms Extension and Advisory Services may be used interchangeably. The framework below provides useful perspectives on the approaches used by different countries and donors in organizing and implementing effective extension systems.

1. Technology Transfer

  • This extension model was prevalent during colonial times and re-emerged strongly during the 1970s and 1980s when the Training and Visit (T&V) system was established in many Asian and Sub-Saharan African countries.
  • This “top-down” model primarily delivers specific recommendations from research, especially for staple food crops, to all types of farmers.
  • The main goal of this model is to increase food production, which helps reduce food costs.

2. Advisory Services

  • Both public extension workers and private-sector firms respond to specific farmer inquiries regarding production problems.
  • Farmers are advised to use a specific practice or technology to solve identified production constraints.
  • Public extension organizations should provide validated information from research regarding the effectiveness of different inputs and methods.
  • Farmers should receive objective and research-based information.

3. Non-formal Education (NFE)

  • In Europe and North America, this paradigm became dominant when universities trained rural people who could not afford or did not have access to formal vocational and technical agricultural education.
  • This approach continues to be used in most extension systems.
  • The focus shifted toward helping farmers improve management skills and technical knowledge to increase production efficiency.
  • It also promotes integrated pest management (IPM) through Farmer Field Schools (FFS).

4. Facilitation Extension

  • This approach evolved from participatory extension methods used 20–30 years ago.
  • It focuses on helping farmers with common interests work together to achieve both individual and shared objectives.
  • Front-line extension agents act as “knowledge brokers” facilitating the teaching-learning process among all farmers, including women and rural youth.
