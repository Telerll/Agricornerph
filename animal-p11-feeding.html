Feeding and Nutrition in Dairy Cattle

This lesson covers nutrients, feed resources, ration, feeding systems, dry matter intake, calf feeding, lactating cow feeding, and nutrition management.

Feeding and Nutrition in Dairy Cattle

Feeding and Nutrition

Feeding and nutrition are essential in dairy production because milk production requires large amounts of nutrients and energy.

  • Milk yield
  • Milk quality
  • Reproduction
  • Growth
  • Health
Proper nutrition improves dairy productivity.

Nutrients

Nutrients are substances required for maintenance, growth, reproduction, and milk production.

  • Water
  • Carbohydrates
  • Proteins
  • Fats
  • Vitamins
  • Minerals
Nutrients are essential for life and production.

Water

Water is the most essential nutrient in dairy cattle. It regulates body temperature, aids digestion, transports nutrients, and supports milk production.

Water is the most important nutrient.

Importance of Water

Milk contains high water content; therefore dairy cattle require abundant clean water.

Adequate water intake supports milk production.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the major source of energy.

  • Grasses
  • Corn
  • Grains
  • Silage
Carbohydrates provide energy.

Proteins

Proteins are necessary for growth, tissue repair, and milk synthesis.

  • Legumes
  • Soybean meal
  • Copra meal
Protein supports milk production.

Fats

Fats are concentrated energy sources that provide energy, improve body condition, and support milk fat production.

Fats are high-energy nutrients.

Vitamins

Vitamins regulate body functions and maintain health.

  • Vitamin A
  • Vitamin D
  • Vitamin E
Vitamins support normal body processes.

Minerals

Minerals are important for bone formation, milk production, and reproduction.

  • Calcium
  • Phosphorus
  • Sodium
  • Potassium
Minerals support growth and production.

Calcium

Calcium supports bones, muscles, and milk secretion.

Calcium is essential for milk production.

Phosphorus

Phosphorus is important in energy metabolism, reproduction, and bone formation.

Phosphorus supports body metabolism.

Balanced Ration

A balanced ration contains nutrients in proper amounts needed by dairy cattle.

Balanced ration improves productivity.

Ration

Ration refers to the total feed consumed within 24 hours.

Ration = daily feed allowance.

Feed

Feed refers to materials consumed by animals for nutrients.

Feed supplies nutrients to livestock.

Roughages

Roughages are bulky feeds high in fiber.

  • Grasses
  • Hay
  • Silage
Roughages are high-fiber feeds.

Concentrates

Concentrates are feeds rich in nutrients and low in fiber.

  • Grains
  • Bran
  • Oilseed meals
Concentrates provide energy and protein.

Forages

Forages are edible plant materials consumed by livestock.

  • Pasture grasses
  • Legumes
Forages are major feed resources.

Pasture and Grazing

Pasture refers to grazing land used for feeding cattle. Grazing is feeding animals directly on pasture.

Grazing reduces feed cost.

Hay

Hay is dried forage preserved for future feeding.

Hay = preserved dry forage.

Silage

Silage is fermented forage stored under anaerobic conditions.

Silage = preserved fermented forage.

Legumes

Legumes are forage crops rich in protein.

  • Ipil-ipil
  • Centrosema
  • Stylo
Legumes are protein-rich feeds.

Feeding Systems

  • Grazing
  • Stall feeding
  • Cut-and-carry
Feeding systems affect productivity.

Stall Feeding

Stall feeding involves feeding cattle inside housing facilities.

Stall feeding allows controlled feeding.

Cut-and-Carry System

In cut-and-carry system, forage is harvested and brought to animals.

Cut-and-carry reduces overgrazing.

Dry Matter

Dry matter refers to feed content excluding water.

Dry matter is important in ration formulation.

Dry Matter Intake

Dry matter intake refers to feed consumed excluding water content.

High milk production requires high DMI.

Energy Requirement

Dairy cattle require high energy for maintenance, milk production, and reproduction.

Energy supports productivity.

Maintenance Requirement

Maintenance requirement refers to nutrients needed for normal body functions.

Maintenance nutrients support survival.

Lactation Requirement

Lactating cows require additional nutrients for milk production.

Lactation increases nutrient requirement.

Feeding Lactating Cows

  • High-quality forage
  • Adequate concentrates
  • Abundant clean water
Lactating cows require high-quality diets.

Feeding Dry Cows

Dry cows require proper feeding before the next lactation.

Proper dry cow feeding improves future milk production.

Feeding Calves

  • Colostrum
  • Milk
  • Calf starter
Proper calf feeding supports growth.

Colostrum Feeding

Colostrum supplies antibodies, nutrients, and immunity.

Colostrum provides passive immunity.

Calf Starter

Calf starter is concentrate feed given to young calves.

Calf starter promotes rumen development.

Weaning

Weaning is the transition from milk feeding to solid feeds.

Weaning promotes independence from milk feeding.

Feed Efficiency

Feed efficiency refers to efficient conversion of feed into milk.

Feed efficiency improves profitability.

Feed Conversion

Feed conversion measures productivity relative to feed intake.

Better feed conversion reduces production cost.

Nutritional Deficiency

Nutritional deficiencies reduce growth, milk production, and reproduction.

Deficiency causes poor performance.

Overfeeding

Overfeeding may cause obesity, metabolic disorders, and reduced efficiency.

Excess feeding may be harmful.

Underfeeding

Underfeeding reduces milk yield, fertility, and growth.

Underfeeding lowers productivity.

Feed Cost

Feed cost is the largest expense in dairy production.

Efficient feeding reduces expenses.

Feed Quality

Good quality feeds improve nutrient utilization, milk production, and animal health.

Feed quality affects productivity.

Feed Storage

Proper feed storage prevents spoilage, contamination, and nutrient loss.

Proper storage preserves feed quality.

Nutrition and Reproduction

Proper nutrition improves fertility, conception, and pregnancy maintenance.

Nutrition affects reproduction.

Nutrition and Health

Good nutrition improves immunity and disease resistance.

Nutrition improves herd health.

Board Exam Trap Terms

  • Ration = feed consumed in 24 hours
  • Roughages = high-fiber feeds
  • Concentrates = nutrient-rich feeds
  • Hay = dried forage
  • Silage = fermented forage
  • Dry matter = feed excluding water
  • DMI = dry matter intake
  • Colostrum = first milk rich in antibodies
  • Weaning = transition to solid feed
These are common board recall terms in dairy nutrition.
