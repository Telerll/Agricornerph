🌧 Part 2: Soil Formation (Minerals, Rocks & Weathering)

Minerals

  • Minerals are naturally occurring inorganic substances with definite chemical composition.
  • They are the basic building blocks of rocks.
  • Soil minerals are derived from weathered rocks.

Types of Minerals

  • Primary Minerals – original minerals from rocks such as Quartz, Feldspar, and Mica.
  • Secondary Minerals – formed from weathering such as Clay minerals and Iron oxides.
Primary minerals come from rocks, while secondary minerals are products of weathering.

Rocks

  • Rocks are aggregates of minerals.
  • They serve as the parent material of soils.
  • The study of rocks is called Petrology.

Types of Rocks

  • Igneous Rocks – formed from cooling and solidification of magma.
    • Intrusive – cooled slowly beneath the earth’s surface; example: Granite
    • Extrusive – cooled rapidly at the surface; example: Basalt
  • Sedimentary Rocks – formed from deposited and compacted materials; examples: Sandstone, Limestone, Shale
  • Metamorphic Rocks – formed by heat and pressure; examples: Marble, Slate

Importance of Rocks in Soil Formation

  • Determine soil mineral composition – the kind of rock controls the minerals present in the soil.
  • Influence soil fertility – some rocks release more nutrients than others.
  • Affect soil texture and structure – rock origin influences particle size and soil condition.
  • Control rate of weathering and soil development – hard rocks weather slowly, while softer rocks weather faster.
Rock type is the foundation of many soil properties.

Weathering

  • Weathering is the breakdown of rocks into smaller particles.
  • It is the first step in soil formation.

Types of Weathering

  • Physical Weathering – breakdown without chemical change.
  • Chemical Weathering – breakdown with chemical reactions that form new substances.
  • Biological Weathering – caused by living organisms such as plant roots and microorganisms.

Processes of Chemical Weathering

  • Hydrolysis – reaction of minerals with water forming new minerals, often clay minerals.
  • Oxidation – reaction with oxygen, often producing red or yellow colors like rusting.
  • Hydration – absorption of water by minerals causing expansion and weakening.
  • Carbonation – reaction of carbon dioxide with water forming carbonic acid that dissolves minerals.
  • Solution – complete dissolving of soluble minerals in water.
Chemical weathering changes the composition of minerals.

Soil Formation (Pedogenesis)

  • Soil formation is the process of developing soil from rocks and minerals.
  • It involves weathering and the action of soil-forming factors over time.

Factors of Soil Formation (CLORPT)

  • Climate – controls temperature and rainfall which affect rate of weathering and soil development.
  • Organisms – plants, animals, and microorganisms add organic matter and influence soil processes.
  • Relief – slope and landscape position affect drainage, erosion, and soil depth.
  • Parent Material – original rock or material from which soil develops.
  • Time – determines the degree of soil development and horizon formation.
CLORPT = Climate, Organisms, Relief, Parent Material, Time

Importance of Soil Formation

  • Determines soil fertility – affects the amount and availability of nutrients for plants.
  • Influences soil depth and development – deeper and more developed soils often support better root growth.
  • Affects soil texture and structure – influences water movement, aeration, and root penetration.
  • Controls water holding capacity and drainage – affects irrigation needs and drought resistance.
  • Determines suitability for crop production – some soils are more favorable for certain crops than others.
  • Influences biological activity – affects the number and activity of soil organisms.
Soil formation affects fertility, depth, texture, water, and crop suitability.

Concept Flow

  • Minerals form rocks.
  • Rocks undergo weathering.
  • Weathering leads to soil formation.
