Feed Classification

Feed Classification

Feed classification is the grouping of feeds based on their nutrient content, digestibility, and use in animal feeding.

It helps determine:

  • What type of feed is being used
  • What nutrients it provides
  • How it should be fed
Feed classification helps in proper ration formulation.

Importance of Feed Classification

Feed classification is important because it helps:

  • Select the right feed
  • Balance rations
  • Reduce feed cost
  • Improve animal performance
  • Avoid nutrient deficiencies
Proper feed classification improves feeding efficiency.

Main Classes of Feed

Feeds are commonly classified into:

  1. Roughages
  2. Concentrates
  3. Supplements
  4. Additives

Roughages

Roughages are bulky feeds high in fiber and low in digestible nutrients.

Usually contain:

  • More than 18% crude fiber
  • Lower energy density

Examples:

  • Pasture grasses
  • Hay
  • Silage
  • Crop residues
Roughages are the main fiber source.

Types of Roughages

Dry Roughages

Dry roughages are feeds with low moisture content.

Examples:

  • Hay
  • Straw
  • Dried grasses

Succulent Roughages

Succulent roughages are feeds with high moisture content.

Examples:

  • Pasture
  • Green chop
  • Silage
  • Root crops
Roughages are important for rumen health.

Silage

Silage is a succulent roughage preserved by anaerobic fermentation.

Key Preservative:

  • Lactic acid

Lactic acid lowers pH and preserves the feed.

Silage = fermented roughage preserved by lactic acid.

Functions of Roughages

Roughages:

  • Provide fiber
  • Stimulate chewing
  • Promote rumination
  • Maintain rumen function
  • Reduce digestive disorders
Fiber is essential for ruminants.

Concentrates

Concentrates are feeds low in fiber and high in digestible nutrients.

Usually contain:

  • Less than 18% crude fiber
  • High energy or high protein

Examples:

  • Corn
  • Wheat bran
  • Soybean meal
  • Rice bran
Concentrates are nutrient-dense feeds.

Types of Concentrates

Energy Concentrates

Energy concentrates are low in fiber and protein but high in energy.

Examples:

  • Corn
  • Cassava
  • Molasses
  • Rice bran

Protein Concentrates

Protein concentrates are feeds high in protein.

Examples:

  • Soybean meal
  • Fish meal
  • Copra meal
  • Meat meal
Concentrates support growth and production.

Concentrate Quality

Not all concentrates are equal.

Soybean Meal

  • Gold standard protein concentrate
  • High in protein
  • Rich in lysine

Corn

  • Excellent energy concentrate
  • High in starch
  • Low in lysine
Corn = energy
Soybean meal = protein

Functions of Concentrates

Concentrates:

  • Provide energy
  • Support growth
  • Improve feed efficiency
  • Support milk, egg, and meat production
Concentrates are used to increase productivity.

Supplements

Supplements are feeds added to improve nutrient balance.

Used to supply:

  • Protein
  • Vitamins
  • Minerals

Examples:

  • Mineral mix
  • Vitamin premix
  • Protein supplements
Supplements correct nutrient deficiencies.

Feed Additives

Feed additives are non-nutritive substances added to improve feed efficiency, health, or performance.

Examples:

  • Probiotics
  • Prebiotics
  • Enzymes
  • Coccidiostats
  • Mold inhibitors
Additives improve feed utilization and health.

Modern Feed Additives

Traditional antibiotic growth promoters are being reduced or phased out in many systems.

Modern alternatives include:

  • Probiotics
  • Prebiotics
  • Enzymes
Modern additives focus on gut health and feed efficiency.

Anti-Nutritional Factors

Anti-nutritional factors are substances in feeds that reduce nutrient use or may cause toxicity.

Examples:

  • Raw soybean – contains trypsin inhibitors
  • Cottonseed – contains gossypol

These reduce feed value and may be harmful.

Control:

  • Heat treatment
  • Proper processing
Processing improves feed safety and nutrient use.

Dry Matter Basis

Feeds can also be classified based on dry matter (DM).

Dry matter means feed minus water.

This is important because:

  • Nutrients are compared on a dry matter basis
  • Moisture affects nutrient concentration
Dry matter is the true nutrient portion of feed.

Palatability

Palatability is how acceptable a feed is to an animal.

It affects:

  • Feed intake
  • Appetite
  • Performance

Factors Affecting Palatability:

  • Smell
  • Taste
  • Texture
  • Freshness
Animals must eat feed before they can benefit from it.

Digestibility

Digestibility is the proportion of feed that can be digested and used by the animal.

Higher digestibility means:

  • Better nutrient use
  • Better feed efficiency
  • Less waste
Digestibility determines feed value.

Board Exam Master Concept

Not all feeds are equal.

Feeds differ in:

  • Nutrient content
  • Digestibility
  • Palatability
  • Feeding value
Feed value depends on classification and quality.
