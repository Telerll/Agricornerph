Common Diseases and Disorders in Beef Cattle

This lesson covers common infectious, non-infectious, parasitic, metabolic, nutritional, reproductive, and management-related diseases and disorders affecting beef cattle.

Common Diseases and Disorders in Beef Cattle

Common Diseases and Disorders

Diseases and disorders greatly affect cattle productivity, growth, reproduction, and profitability.

Proper disease prevention and early treatment reduce economic losses and improve animal welfare.

Diseases reduce productivity and profitability.

Disease

Disease refers to any abnormal condition that disrupts normal body functions.

Diseases may be caused by:

  • Bacteria
  • Viruses
  • Parasites
  • Fungi
  • Nutritional deficiencies
  • Environmental stress
Disease affects normal body function.

Infectious Diseases

Infectious diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms and may spread between animals.

Infectious diseases are transmissible.

Non-Infectious Diseases

Non-infectious diseases are not caused by infectious agents.

Examples include nutritional deficiency, poisoning, and metabolic disorders.

Non-infectious diseases are not transmissible.

Bacterial Diseases

Bacterial diseases are caused by bacteria.

  • Anthrax
  • Blackleg
  • Mastitis
  • Foot rot
Bacteria cause infectious diseases.

Viral Diseases

Viral diseases are caused by viruses.

  • Foot-and-mouth disease
  • Rabies
Viral diseases are highly contagious.

Parasitic Diseases

Parasitic diseases are caused by internal and external parasites.

  • Liver fluke
  • Roundworms
  • Ticks
  • Mange
Parasites reduce growth and productivity.

Fungal Diseases

Fungal diseases are caused by fungi.

Example: ringworm.

Fungal diseases affect skin and tissues.

Anthrax

Anthrax is a highly fatal bacterial disease caused by Bacillus anthracis.

Signs may include sudden death, bleeding from body openings, and fever.

Anthrax is zoonotic, meaning it can be transmitted from animals to humans.

Anthrax = Bacillus anthracis.

Blackleg

Blackleg is a bacterial disease affecting muscles of young cattle.

Signs may include swelling, lameness, and fever.

Blackleg affects muscles.

Mastitis

Mastitis is inflammation of the udder usually caused by bacterial infection.

Signs may include swollen udder, abnormal milk, and pain.

Mastitis affects the udder.

Foot Rot

Foot rot is an infectious hoof disease.

Signs may include foul odor, swelling, and lameness.

Foot rot affects the hooves.

Foot-and-Mouth Disease

Foot-and-mouth disease or FMD is a highly contagious viral disease.

Signs may include mouth blisters, excessive salivation, and lameness.

FMD is highly contagious.

Rabies

Rabies is a fatal viral disease affecting the nervous system.

Signs may include abnormal behavior, paralysis, and aggression.

Rabies is zoonotic.

Rabies affects the nervous system.

Liver Fluke

Liver fluke is an internal parasite affecting the liver.

It may cause weakness, poor growth, anemia, and reduced productivity.

Liver fluke damages the liver.

Roundworms

Roundworms are internal parasites affecting digestion and nutrient absorption.

Effects may include weight loss, diarrhea, and poor growth.

Roundworms reduce feed efficiency.

Tick Infestation

Ticks are external parasites that suck blood and transmit diseases.

They may cause irritation, anemia, and disease transmission.

Ticks reduce animal performance.

Mange

Mange is a skin disease caused by mites.

Signs may include itching, hair loss, and skin thickening.

Mange is caused by mites.

Ringworm

Ringworm is a fungal skin disease.

Signs may include circular skin lesions and hair loss.

Ringworm is a fungal disease.

Bloat

Bloat is excessive gas accumulation in the rumen.

Signs may include swollen abdomen, difficulty breathing, and discomfort.

Bloat = gas accumulation in rumen.

Ketosis

Ketosis is a metabolic disorder caused by energy deficiency.

Signs may include reduced appetite, weakness, and weight loss.

Ketosis results from energy imbalance.

Milk Fever

Milk fever is caused by calcium deficiency.

Signs may include weakness, muscle tremors, and inability to stand.

Milk fever is associated with low calcium.

Acidosis

Acidosis occurs when excessive acid accumulates in the rumen.

It is commonly associated with excessive grain feeding.

Signs may include diarrhea, weakness, and reduced appetite.

Acidosis is related to rumen acidity.

Pneumonia

Pneumonia is inflammation of the lungs.

Signs may include coughing, fever, nasal discharge, and difficulty breathing.

Pneumonia affects the respiratory system.

Diarrhea

Diarrhea involves frequent loose feces and may lead to dehydration.

Possible causes include infection, poor feeding, and parasites.

Severe diarrhea may cause dehydration.

Lameness

Lameness refers to difficulty in walking.

Causes may include injuries, hoof problems, and foot rot.

Lameness affects movement.

Poisoning

Poisoning occurs when cattle consume toxic substances.

Sources may include poisonous plants, chemicals, and spoiled feeds.

Poisoning affects body systems.

Nutritional Deficiency

Nutritional deficiency occurs when nutrient intake is insufficient.

It may lead to poor growth, weak immunity, and reproductive problems.

Nutritional deficiency reduces productivity.

Heat Stress

Heat stress occurs when cattle fail to regulate body temperature effectively.

Signs may include panting, reduced feed intake, and weakness.

Heat stress lowers productivity.

Dehydration

Dehydration occurs when water loss exceeds water intake.

Signs may include weakness, dry muzzle, and sunken eyes.

Dehydration affects body function.

Anemia

Anemia refers to reduced red blood cells or hemoglobin.

Causes may include blood loss, parasites, and nutritional deficiency.

Anemia causes weakness.

Abortion

Abortion refers to premature loss of fetus before full-term pregnancy.

Causes may include disease, stress, and nutritional problems.

Abortion causes reproductive loss.

Retained Placenta

Retained placenta occurs when fetal membranes fail to detach after calving.

Retained placenta is a postpartum disorder.

Dystocia

Dystocia refers to difficult calving.

Causes may include large fetus, poor fetal position, or narrow pelvis.

Dystocia = difficult birth.

Biosecurity

Biosecurity refers to practices preventing disease introduction and spread.

Examples include quarantine, sanitation, and visitor control.

Biosecurity prevents disease spread.

Quarantine

Quarantine refers to isolation of animals to prevent disease transmission.

Quarantine limits disease spread.

Vaccination

Vaccination stimulates immunity against diseases.

Vaccination prevents disease.

Deworming

Deworming controls internal parasites.

Deworming improves animal health.

Sanitation

Sanitation involves cleanliness of facilities and equipment.

Sanitation reduces disease risk.

Disinfection

Disinfection destroys harmful microorganisms using chemicals.

Disinfection controls pathogens.

Observation and Monitoring

Regular observation allows early disease detection.

Early detection improves treatment success.

Veterinary Care

Veterinary care includes diagnosis, treatment, vaccination, and disease prevention.

Veterinary care improves herd health.

Mortality

Mortality refers to animal death within the herd.

High mortality causes economic loss.

Good management lowers mortality.

Morbidity

Morbidity refers to disease occurrence within a population.

Morbidity measures disease incidence.

Zoonotic Diseases

Zoonotic diseases are transmissible from animals to humans.

Examples include anthrax and rabies.

Zoonotic diseases infect humans.

Economic Importance of Disease Control

Disease prevention and control improve productivity, growth, reproductive performance, and profitability.

Disease control reduces economic loss.

Importance of Proper Health Management

Proper health management improves survival, growth, feed efficiency, productivity, and profitability.

Proper health management improves herd performance.
