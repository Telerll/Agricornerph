Introduction

Economics is the study of how individuals and society use scarce or limited resources in producing goods and services to satisfy unlimited human wants and needs. These resources include land, labor, capital, machinery, technology, and managerial skills. Economics also explains how products and services are distributed and consumed within society.

In agriculture, economics is important because farmers and producers continuously make decisions regarding the use of limited resources such as farmland, seeds, fertilizers, labor, irrigation water, machinery, and capital. Since resources are limited, proper allocation is necessary to maximize production, reduce costs, and improve income.

Economics is commonly divided into two branches: macroeconomics and microeconomics.

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole. It deals with national income, inflation, unemployment, taxation, government expenditures, economic growth, and international trade. In the Philippine setting, macroeconomics helps explain issues such as rising prices of agricultural commodities, inflation, importation of rice, fuel price increases, and the overall condition of the agricultural sector.

Microeconomics focuses on individual economic units such as farmers, households, consumers, firms, and markets. It studies supply and demand, production decisions, pricing, costs, profits, and consumer behavior. For example, a farmer deciding whether to plant rice, corn, or vegetables is an example of microeconomic decision-making.

Positive Economics

Positive economics explains economic situations objectively using facts, data, and cause-and-effect relationships. It describes what currently exists or what may happen in the economy without giving opinions or judgments.

It focuses on statements that can be tested or proven true or false through observation and analysis.

Examples:
  • An increase in fertilizer prices increases farm production costs.
  • A decrease in rice supply may increase market prices.
  • Higher fuel prices affect transportation costs of agricultural products.

Positive economics is commonly used in economic analysis, forecasting, and policy evaluation.

Normative Economics

Normative economics explains what should happen in the economy based on opinions, beliefs, values, or recommendations. It is subjective because it involves personal judgments and policy preferences.

Examples:
  • The government should provide subsidies to farmers.
  • Rice prices should be stabilized to protect consumers.
  • Agricultural workers should receive higher wages.

Normative economics is often used in policy-making and development planning because it focuses on improving economic conditions and social welfare.

Economic Model

An economic model is considered the basic building block in economic analysis. It is a simplified representation or conceptual framework used by economists to explain how economic activities occur and how different economic variables are related to one another.

Economic models are based on assumptions that simplify complex real-world situations in order to make analysis easier and more understandable. These models help economists study production, consumption, pricing, supply and demand, market behavior, and resource allocation.

One example of an economic model is the perfectly competitive market model, where many buyers and sellers interact freely in the market with complete information and identical products.

Agricultural Economics

Agricultural economics is the application of economic principles to crop and animal production. It deals with the allocation of scarce resources among competing alternative uses in the production, processing, distribution, and consumption of food, feed, and fiber.

Agricultural economics studies how farmers, agribusiness firms, cooperatives, and government institutions make decisions regarding the use of land, labor, capital, technology, and other agricultural resources.

  • Farm management
  • Agricultural finance
  • Agricultural marketing
  • Agricultural policy
  • Cooperative management
  • Production economics

In the United States, agricultural economics was first taught at Harvard University and the University of Wisconsin in 1903. The Bureau of Agricultural Economics was later formed under the United States Department of Agriculture in 1921.

In the Philippines, the Bureau of Agricultural Economics was established on June 22, 1963 under the Office of the Secretary of Agriculture and Natural Resources.

Agricultural economics is important in the Philippines because agriculture remains a major source of food, employment, livelihood, and rural development. It helps improve farm productivity, resource utilization, income generation, and marketing efficiency within the agricultural sector.

Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)

The Production Possibilities Frontier or PPF is a useful economic tool used to illustrate the choices available to society and the limitations caused by scarce resources.

It shows the possible combinations of goods and services that can be produced using a given amount of resources and technology.

  • Scarcity
  • Choice
  • Opportunity cost
  • Trade-offs
  • Efficiency

The downward slope of the PPF indicates that producing more of one product requires sacrificing some quantity of another product. This illustrates opportunity cost and trade-offs.

Points inside the PPF indicate inefficient use of resources, while points outside the curve are unattainable or infeasible under current conditions and available resources.

In agriculture, if more farmland is devoted to rice production, less land may be available for corn or vegetable production.

Ceteris Paribus

Ceteris paribus is a Latin phrase meaning “other things being equal.” This assumption is used when analyzing the effect of one variable while keeping all other factors constant.

If the price of rice increases, quantity demanded decreases, ceteris paribus. This means factors such as income, population, and consumer preferences remain unchanged.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost refers to the value of the next best alternative sacrificed when making a decision.

A farmer choosing to plant onion instead of corn sacrifices the possible income from corn production. The foregone income becomes the opportunity cost.

Opportunity cost is important in economic decision-making because resources are limited and every choice involves trade-offs.

Diminishing Returns

The Law of Diminishing Returns states that as additional units of a variable input are added to a fixed input, the additional output eventually decreases.

In agriculture, this principle is commonly observed when too many workers, fertilizers, or other inputs are added beyond the optimum level.

Adding excessive laborers on a small farm may eventually reduce efficiency and productivity instead of increasing output.

Marginality

Marginality refers to additional or incremental changes resulting from one more unit of input or output.

  • Marginal cost
  • Marginal revenue
  • Marginal product
  • Marginal utility

Marginal analysis helps producers determine whether additional production will increase profits or losses.

Economic Resources

Economic resources are scarce resources used in the production of goods and services.

  • Land: agricultural land, forests, rivers, water resources, and minerals.
  • Labor: human effort, both physical and mental, used in production activities.
  • Capital: machinery, tools, equipment, irrigation systems, buildings, and other man-made resources.
  • Entrepreneurship: ability to organize resources, make decisions, innovate, and assume business risks.

Nature and Scope

Economics is considered a social science because it studies human behavior in relation to production, distribution, exchange, and consumption of goods and services.

The scope of economics includes agriculture, trade, banking, transportation, labor, industry, public finance, and international trade. Economics also studies relationships among producers, consumers, markets, and government institutions.

One of the major concerns of economics is solving the basic economic problems caused by scarcity.

  1. What to produce
  2. How much to produce
  3. How to produce
  4. When to produce
  5. For whom to produce

In agriculture, these questions are important because farmers must decide which crops to plant, how much fertilizer to apply, what technology to use, and who will consume the products.

Economics also aims to achieve several economic goals such as full employment, economic growth, efficiency, price stability, economic freedom, economic development, and economic security.

Foundation of Economics

The foundation of economics is based on self-interest, scarcity, and choice.

Self-interest

Self-interest motivates individuals and businesses to improve their welfare and satisfy their needs and wants. Farmers aim to maximize profits while consumers aim to maximize satisfaction.

Scarcity

Scarcity exists because resources are limited while human wants are unlimited. Agricultural land, water supply, machinery, labor, and capital are all limited resources.

Choice

Choice becomes necessary because individuals and societies must decide how limited resources will be allocated. Every decision involves trade-offs and opportunity costs.

Ideologies

Economic ideologies are systems or beliefs regarding how an economy should operate.

Capitalism

Capitalism is an economic system characterized by private ownership of property and businesses. It promotes competition, profit motive, and economic freedom with minimal government intervention.

Communism

Communism is a system where resources and means of production are collectively owned and controlled by the government.

Socialism

Socialism combines government ownership of important industries with limited private ownership. The government plays a major role in economic planning and resource distribution.

Fascism

Fascism is associated with strong centralized authority and government control over economic activities.

Feudalism

Feudalism is an old economic system where landowners control land while peasants provide labor in exchange for protection and livelihood.

Economic Theories

Economic theories are principles and explanations used to understand economic relationships and activities.

Classical Economics

Classical economists believed that markets naturally move toward equilibrium through the interaction of supply and demand.

Adam Smith

Adam Smith is considered the Father of Economics. In his book “The Wealth of Nations,” he introduced the concept of the invisible hand, which explains how individual self-interest can benefit society.

David Ricardo

David Ricardo developed the theory of comparative advantage, explaining how countries benefit from specialization and trade.

Thomas Malthus

Thomas Malthus studied population growth and warned that rapid population increase could exceed food production.

John Stuart Mill

John Stuart Mill contributed to political economy and social philosophy.

Neoclassical Economics

Neoclassical economics focuses on utility, marginal analysis, rational decision-making, and market equilibrium.

Alfred Marshall

Alfred Marshall emphasized demand, utility, and price determination.

John Maynard Keynes

Keynes emphasized the importance of government intervention during economic recessions and unemployment.

Post-Keynesian or Mainstream Economics

This combines neoclassical and Keynesian principles in analyzing economic activities.

Socialist Economics

Karl Marx criticized capitalism and promoted collective ownership of production resources.
