Animal Breeding

Animal Breeding

Animal breeding is the branch of animal science concerned with the planned mating and genetic improvement of animals to produce superior offspring.

It is the practical application of genetics in animal production.

Animal breeding is not simply mating animals. It is the deliberate and systematic process of selecting genetically superior males and females so that desirable traits are passed on to the next generation while undesirable traits are reduced or eliminated.

The main goal of animal breeding is genetic improvement.

Animal breeding = planned mating and genetic improvement of animals.

Importance of Animal Breeding

Animal breeding is important because it improves the productive, reproductive, and economic performance of farm animals.

Through proper breeding, producers can develop animals that are more efficient, more productive, healthier, and better adapted to their environment.

  • Growth rate
  • Fertility
  • Reproductive efficiency
  • Feed efficiency
  • Milk yield
  • Meat yield
  • Egg production
  • Wool production
  • Disease resistance
  • Adaptability
  • Longevity
  • Profitability
Animal breeding improves productivity, efficiency, and profitability.

Breeding

Breeding is the controlled mating of selected male and female animals to produce offspring with desirable inherited traits.

Breeding is based on the principle that offspring inherit genes from both parents.

Because of this, the choice of sire and dam directly affects the genetic quality and performance potential of the next generation.

Breeding = controlled mating for inherited improvement.

Animal Improvement

Animal improvement is the process of increasing the genetic merit and productive performance of animals across generations.

It is achieved through selection, culling, mating systems, performance testing, and genetic evaluation.

The goal is not only to produce better individual animals but also to improve the overall herd, flock, or population over time.

Animal improvement = long-term genetic progress.

Selection

Selection is the process of identifying and choosing superior animals to become parents of the next generation.

Selection is the most important tool in animal breeding because genetic improvement begins with choosing the right breeding stock.

Animals are selected based on traits that are economically important and heritable.

  • Performance
  • Productivity
  • Fertility
  • Health
  • Conformation
  • Adaptability
  • Pedigree
  • Progeny performance
Selection = choosing genetically superior animals.

Basis of Selection

Basis of selection refers to the criteria or information used in choosing breeding animals.

Selection should not rely on appearance alone. It may use records, ancestry, family performance, offspring performance, and genetic estimates.

  • Individual performance = based on the animal’s own record
  • Pedigree = based on ancestry
  • Family performance = based on relatives
  • Progeny performance = based on offspring
  • Estimated Breeding Value = based on predicted genetic merit
Basis of selection = criteria used in choosing breeders.

Culling

Culling is the deliberate removal of inferior, unproductive, diseased, or genetically undesirable animals from the breeding population.

Culling is the opposite of selection.

Selection adds superior animals to the breeding herd, while culling removes inferior animals that should not contribute genes to the next generation.

Culling = removal of inferior breeding stock.

Reasons for Culling

Animals are culled when they are no longer useful or when they carry traits that may reduce herd productivity.

  • Low productivity
  • Poor fertility
  • Poor mothering ability
  • Slow growth
  • Poor conformation
  • Disease
  • Genetic defects
  • Old age
  • Undesirable temperament
Culling removes poor performers and undesirable breeders.

Selection Differential

Selection differential is the difference between the average performance of selected parents and the average performance of the total population.

It measures how much better the selected parents are compared with the population average.

A higher selection differential usually means greater potential genetic improvement, provided the trait is heritable.

Selection differential = superiority of selected parents.

Selection Intensity

Selection intensity is the degree of strictness used in selecting breeding animals.

The fewer animals selected from a population, the greater the selection intensity.

High selection intensity can increase genetic progress, but it may also reduce genetic diversity if used carelessly.

Selection intensity = strictness of selection.

Selection Accuracy

Selection accuracy is the degree to which selection correctly identifies genetically superior animals.

Higher accuracy means the selected animals are more likely to truly possess superior genes, not just good performance caused by environment.

Accuracy improves when reliable records, progeny testing, EBV, and genetic information are used.

Selection accuracy = correctness of selection.

Breeding Systems

Breeding systems are planned methods of mating animals to achieve specific breeding objectives.

The choice of breeding system depends on the desired traits, available breeding stock, management level, and production goal.

Breeding systems may be used to preserve breed purity, increase uniformity, combine traits, or produce hybrid vigor.

Breeding systems = planned mating methods.

Purebreeding

Purebreeding is the mating of animals belonging to the same breed.

It is used to preserve breed identity and maintain predictable breed characteristics.

Purebreeding is common in seedstock production, where animals are bred mainly to produce breeding stock rather than market animals.

  • Preserves breed purity
  • Maintains breed identity
  • Retains predictable traits
  • Produces purebred breeding stock
Purebreeding = same breed mating.

Advantages of Purebreeding

Purebreeding is useful when the goal is to maintain a breed’s identity and produce animals with uniform, predictable traits.

  • Maintains breed purity
  • Preserves desirable breed traits
  • Produces uniform offspring
  • Useful for seedstock production
Purebreeding preserves breed identity.

Inbreeding

Inbreeding is the mating of animals that are more closely related than the average of the population.

Examples include sire to daughter, dam to son, and brother to sister mating.

Inbreeding increases homozygosity, meaning it increases the chance that alleles at a gene locus are the same.

  • Sire × daughter
  • Dam × son
  • Brother × sister
Inbreeding = mating of closely related animals.

Effects of Inbreeding

Inbreeding can increase uniformity and help fix desirable traits, but it can also expose harmful recessive genes.

Because recessive defects are more likely to appear when animals are homozygous, excessive inbreeding may reduce performance and health.

  • Increases uniformity
  • Fixes desirable traits
  • Exposes recessive genes
  • Increases homozygosity
  • May expose defects
  • May reduce fertility
  • May reduce vigor
  • May reduce survivability
Inbreeding increases homozygosity but may expose defects.

Inbreeding Depression

Inbreeding depression is the decline in biological performance caused by excessive inbreeding.

It usually affects traits related to reproduction, survival, and fitness.

Inbreeding depression is one reason why close inbreeding must be managed carefully in animal breeding programs.

  • Reduced fertility
  • Weak offspring
  • Low growth rate
  • Reduced survivability
  • Increased genetic defects
Inbreeding depression = reduced vigor due to inbreeding.

Linebreeding

Linebreeding is a mild and controlled form of inbreeding used to maintain a close genetic relationship to a superior ancestor.

It attempts to preserve the genes of an outstanding animal while avoiding the severe negative effects of close inbreeding.

Linebreeding is commonly used when a breeder wants to maintain the influence of a superior sire, dam, or family line.

Linebreeding = controlled inbreeding to preserve superior ancestry.

Outcrossing

Outcrossing is the mating of unrelated animals within the same breed.

It helps reduce inbreeding while still maintaining breed identity.

Outcrossing may introduce new genetic variation into a herd without changing the breed.

Outcrossing = unrelated same-breed mating.

Crossbreeding

Crossbreeding is the mating of animals from different breeds.

It is done to combine desirable traits from different breeds into one offspring.

Crossbreeding is widely used in livestock production because it can improve performance, fertility, adaptability, and survivability.

Crossbreeding = mating different breeds.

Advantages of Crossbreeding

Crossbreeding is useful because different breeds often have different strengths.

By combining breeds, producers can produce offspring that perform better than either breed alone in certain production conditions.

  • Combines desirable breed traits
  • Improves performance
  • Improves adaptability
  • Improves fertility
  • Improves survivability
Crossbreeding combines breed strengths.

Hybrid Vigor / Heterosis

Hybrid vigor, also called heterosis, is the superior performance of crossbred offspring compared with the average performance of their purebred parents.

It is one of the major advantages of crossbreeding.

Heterosis is often strongest for traits related to reproduction, survival, and general fitness.

Heterosis = crossbred superiority.

Effects of Heterosis

Heterosis can improve traits that are difficult to improve through selection alone, especially traits with low heritability.

  • Fertility
  • Growth rate
  • Vigor
  • Survivability
  • Disease resistance
  • Adaptability
Heterosis improves biological efficiency.

Grading Up

Grading up is the repeated mating of local or non-purebred females to purebred sires in order to improve the genetic quality of the herd over generations.

This system is commonly used to upgrade native or unimproved stock.

With each generation, the offspring become genetically closer to the purebred sire breed.

Grading up = improving native stock using purebred sires.

Backcrossing

Backcrossing is the mating of offspring back to one of the parent breeds.

It is used when breeders want to retain or recover certain traits from one parent breed.

Backcrossing may also be used in breed development and genetic studies.

Backcrossing = offspring × parent breed.

Pedigree

A pedigree is the ancestry record of an animal.

It shows the sire, dam, ancestors, and family lineage of the animal.

Pedigree is useful in breeding because it helps identify ancestry, avoid excessive inbreeding, and trace desirable or undesirable traits.

  • Sire
  • Dam
  • Ancestors
  • Family lineage
Pedigree = ancestry record.

Performance Record

A performance record is the documented productive or reproductive performance of an animal.

Performance records are important because they provide objective information for selection.

Without records, selection may rely only on appearance, which can be misleading.

  • Milk yield
  • Growth rate
  • Litter size
  • Egg production
  • Feed efficiency
Performance record = measured performance.

Progeny Testing

Progeny testing is evaluating the breeding value of an animal based on the performance of its offspring.

It is especially useful for traits that cannot be measured directly in the parent.

For example, milk production cannot be measured in a bull, but his breeding value for milk can be evaluated by the performance of his daughters.

Progeny testing = judge parent by offspring.

Breeding Value

Breeding value is the genetic worth of an animal as a parent.

It represents the value of genes the animal can pass to its offspring.

An animal with high breeding value is expected to produce offspring with superior performance for a specific trait.

Breeding value = genetic worth as parent.

Estimated Breeding Value

Estimated Breeding Value (EBV) is the predicted genetic merit of an animal based on available records and genetic information.

EBV helps breeders compare animals more accurately by estimating how much of their performance is due to genetics rather than environment.

EBV is a major tool in modern animal breeding and selection programs.

EBV = predicted genetic merit.

Contemporary Group

A contemporary group is a group of animals raised under the same environmental conditions for fair comparison.

Animals in the same contemporary group are similar in age, sex, management, feeding, and environment.

This helps separate genetic differences from environmental effects.

Contemporary group = fair comparison group.

Individual Selection

Individual selection is selecting animals based on their own performance.

This method works best for traits that are measurable in the animal and have moderate to high heritability.

Examples include growth rate, body weight, and some carcass traits.

Individual selection = based on own performance.

Family Selection

Family selection is selecting animals based on the performance of their relatives.

This method is useful when the trait has low heritability or when the trait cannot be measured directly in the individual.

Family selection may use records from siblings, parents, or other relatives.

Family selection = based on relatives.

Mass Selection

Mass selection is selection based mainly on the animal’s own phenotype or observable performance.

It is simple and practical but may be less accurate when environmental effects strongly influence the trait.

Mass selection is commonly used when records are limited.

Mass selection = phenotype-based selection.

Tandem Selection

Tandem selection is a selection method where one trait is improved at a time.

After the first trait reaches the desired level, selection shifts to another trait.

This method is simple but slow when many traits need improvement.

Tandem selection = one trait at a time.

Independent Culling Levels

Independent culling levels is a selection method where animals must meet minimum standards for several traits.

If an animal fails to meet the minimum standard for one important trait, it may be culled even if it performs well in other traits.

This method helps prevent selection of animals with serious weakness in one trait.

Independent culling levels = minimum standard for each trait.

Selection Index

A selection index combines several traits into one selection value.

Each trait may be weighted according to its economic importance and genetic value.

This is one of the best methods for selecting animals when several traits must be improved at the same time.

Selection index = multi-trait selection tool.

Artificial Insemination

Artificial insemination (AI) is the artificial placement of semen into the female reproductive tract.

AI allows wider use of superior males because semen from one excellent sire can be used to breed many females.

It can accelerate genetic improvement and reduce the need to maintain many breeding males.

AI = artificial breeding.

Embryo Transfer

Embryo transfer (ET) is the transfer of an embryo from a donor female to a recipient female.

This technique allows superior females to produce more offspring than they could naturally carry.

ET is useful for multiplying the genetics of elite females.

ET = embryo transfer.

Genomics

Genomics is the use of whole-genome information in breeding decisions.

It allows breeders to evaluate animals at the DNA level rather than relying only on visible performance or pedigree.

Genomics can increase selection accuracy and speed up genetic progress.

Genomics = genome-based breeding.

Marker-Assisted Selection

Marker-assisted selection (MAS) is selection using DNA markers linked to desirable traits.

A DNA marker is a known DNA sequence associated with a trait of interest.

MAS is useful for selecting traits that are difficult, expensive, or slow to measure directly.

Marker-assisted selection = DNA marker-based selection.
