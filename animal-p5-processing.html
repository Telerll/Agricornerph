Meat Processing and Preservation

Meat Processing

Meat processing is the conversion of fresh meat into products with improved shelf life, safety, convenience, and value.

It involves physical, chemical, and biological methods to improve meat for storage and consumption.

Meat processing adds value and extends usability.

Importance of Meat Processing

Meat processing is important to:

  • Preserve meat
  • Extend shelf life
  • Improve flavor
  • Improve convenience
  • Increase product variety
  • Reduce spoilage losses
Processing improves value, safety, and shelf life.

Meat Preservation

Meat preservation is the prevention or delay of spoilage in meat.

Its purpose is to:

  • Slow microbial growth
  • Reduce chemical deterioration
  • Extend storage life
Preservation prevents spoilage.

Causes of Meat Spoilage

Meat spoilage is caused by:

  • Microorganisms
  • Enzymes
  • Oxidation
  • Poor handling
  • High temperature
Spoilage reduces meat safety and quality.

Methods of Meat Preservation

Common preservation methods include:

  • Chilling
  • Freezing
  • Curing
  • Drying
  • Smoking
  • Salting
  • Canning
  • Fermentation
Preservation methods control spoilage.

Chilling

Chilling is lowering meat temperature above freezing, usually around 0–4°C.

It slows:

  • Bacterial growth
  • Enzyme activity
Chilling slows spoilage.

Freezing

Freezing is storing meat below 0°C to stop microbial growth and greatly slow spoilage.

Freezing preserves meat longer than chilling.

Curing

Curing is preserving meat using salt, nitrite or nitrate, and sugar.

It improves:

  • Flavor
  • Color
  • Shelf life
Curing preserves and flavors meat.

Importance of Curing

Curing helps:

  • Inhibit microbes
  • Preserve color
  • Improve taste
  • Extend shelf life
Curing improves both safety and quality.

Sodium Nitrite

Sodium nitrite is the key curing ingredient.

It is important because it:

  • Inhibits Clostridium botulinum
  • Prevents botulism
  • Helps preserve cured pink color
  • Improves cured flavor
Sodium nitrite is the key safety ingredient in curing.

Drying

Drying removes water from meat to reduce microbial growth.

Less water means less spoilage.

Drying preserves by removing moisture.

Smoking

Smoking exposes meat to smoke from burning wood.

It helps:

  • Preserve meat
  • Improve flavor
  • Improve color
Smoking preserves and flavors meat.

Salting

Salting preserves meat by drawing out water and inhibiting microbial growth.

Salt works through osmotic pressure, pulling water out of microbial cells and causing plasmolysis.

Salt preserves by dehydrating microbes.

Canning

Canning preserves meat by sealing it in containers and applying heat.

This destroys microorganisms and prevents recontamination.

Canning provides long shelf life.

Fermentation

Fermentation uses beneficial microorganisms to produce acids that preserve meat.

Common in:

  • Fermented sausages
Fermentation preserves through acid production.

Processed Meat Products

Common processed meat products include:

  • Ham
  • Bacon
  • Sausage
  • Tocino
  • Longganisa
  • Corned beef
  • Canned meat
Processing creates many meat products.

Comminution

Comminution is reducing meat into smaller particles by chopping, grinding, or mincing.

Used in:

  • Sausages
  • Patties
Comminution changes meat form.

Emulsification

Emulsification is the fine mixing of fat, water, and protein into a stable meat batter.

Used in:

  • Hotdog
  • Bologna
  • Luncheon meat

The key emulsifying protein is myosin, the same muscle protein involved in rigor mortis.

Myosin is the main emulsifying protein in meat products.

Binders and Fillers

In meat processing:

  • Binders improve water and fat retention. Example: soy protein.
  • Fillers increase bulk and reduce cost. Example: starch.
Binders improve function, fillers reduce cost.

Packaging

Packaging protects meat from:

  • Contamination
  • Moisture loss
  • Oxygen
  • Spoilage
Packaging protects meat quality.

Types of Meat Packaging

Common packaging systems include:

  • Vacuum packaging
  • Modified atmosphere packaging (MAP)
  • Canning
  • Plastic wrapping
Packaging extends shelf life.

Vacuum Packaging

Vacuum packaging removes air around meat before sealing.

This slows:

  • Oxidation
  • Microbial growth
Vacuum packaging extends shelf life by removing air.

Danger Zone

The temperature danger zone is 5°C to 60°C.

This is the range where bacteria multiply fastest.

Meat should move through this range as quickly as possible during processing.

5°C to 60°C is the bacterial danger zone.

Food Safety in Processing

Safe meat processing requires:

  • Sanitation
  • Temperature control
  • Clean equipment
  • Safe ingredients
  • Hygienic handling
Food safety is essential in processing.

Processing preserves and adds value.
