🍽 Digestive System of Farm Animals

Digestive System

Digestive system is the body system responsible for breaking down feed into nutrients that can be absorbed and used by the animal.

Functions of the Digestive System

Ingestion

Ingestion is the intake of food into the mouth.

Digestion

Digestion is the breakdown of food into simpler substances.

Absorption

Absorption is the movement of nutrients into the bloodstream.

Assimilation

Assimilation is the use of absorbed nutrients by body cells.

Egestion

Egestion is the elimination of undigested food materials.

Types of Digestive Systems

Monogastric Animals

Monogastric animals have a single-chambered stomach.

Examples: Swine and poultry.

Ruminant Animals

Ruminant animals have a four-compartment stomach that allows microbial fermentation.

Examples: Cattle, carabao, goat, and sheep.

Hindgut Fermenters

Hindgut fermenters are monogastric animals that ferment fiber in the large intestine, particularly in the cecum.

Examples: Horse and rabbit.

Hindgut fermenters have an enlarged cecum for microbial digestion.

General Parts of the Digestive System

Mouth

Mouth is the organ where ingestion and initial digestion occur.

Mastication

Mastication is the chewing of food.

Poultry do not chew because they do not have teeth.

Saliva

Saliva moistens food and begins digestion.

Esophagus

Esophagus is the tube that carries food from the mouth to the stomach.

Stomach

Stomach is the organ where chemical digestion occurs using acids and enzymes.

Small Intestine

Small intestine is the main site of digestion and nutrient absorption.

Large Intestine

Large intestine absorbs water and forms feces.

Anus

Anus is the opening where waste is expelled.

Ruminant Stomach Compartments

Rumen (Paunch)

Rumen is the largest compartment where microbial fermentation occurs.

It breaks down cellulose and produces volatile fatty acids (VFA).

Reticulum (Honeycomb)

Reticulum traps foreign materials and works with the rumen.

Omasum (Book Stomach)

Omasum absorbs water and nutrients.

Abomasum (True Stomach)

Abomasum performs enzymatic digestion using acids.

Rumen = fermentation
Reticulum = filter
Omasum = water absorption
Abomasum = true digestion

Rumination Process

Rumination

Rumination is the process of regurgitating, rechewing, and reswallowing feed.

Steps of Rumination

  • Regurgitation
  • Remastication
  • Resalivation
  • Redeglutition

Microbial Digestion

Microbial digestion is digestion by microorganisms in the rumen.

Products of Microbial Digestion

  • Volatile fatty acids (energy source)
  • Microbial protein
  • Vitamins such as B-complex

Poultry Digestive System

Crop

Crop temporarily stores food.

Proventriculus

Proventriculus is the glandular stomach that secretes digestive enzymes.

Gizzard

Gizzard is the muscular stomach that grinds food.

It uses grit or small stones and replaces mastication.

Cloaca

Cloaca is a common chamber where the digestive, urinary, and reproductive systems meet before waste is expelled.

It serves as the passageway for feces, urine, and eggs.

Beak → Esophagus → Crop → Proventriculus → Gizzard → Intestine → Cloaca

Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up digestion.

Importance in Animal Science

  • Determines feed efficiency
  • Important in feeding management
  • Affects growth and production
  • Serves as the basis of livestock nutrition
Ruminants can convert low-quality forage into high-quality protein because of microbial fermentation.

Comparison of Digestive Systems

Ruminants

  • Multi-chamber stomach
  • Digest fiber efficiently
  • Use microbial fermentation

Monogastrics

  • Single stomach
  • Use enzymes
  • Limited fiber digestion

Hindgut Fermenters

  • Fermentation in the cecum
  • Partial fiber digestion

Final Summary

Digestive system = ingestion → digestion → absorption

Monogastric = 1 stomach
Ruminant = 4 stomachs
Hindgut fermenter = cecum digestion

Poultry:
Crop = storage
Proventriculus = enzymes
Gizzard = grinding
Cloaca = exit chamber

Rumen = fermentation
