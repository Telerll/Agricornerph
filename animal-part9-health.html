Health Management

Health Management

Health management refers to the proper prevention, control, diagnosis, treatment, and management of diseases and health problems in goats and sheep to maintain productivity, welfare, and profitability.

Good health management improves growth, reproduction, survivability, feed efficiency, milk production, and meat production.

Poor health management may result in disease outbreaks, poor growth, infertility, weight loss, death, and economic losses.

Health management maintains herd and flock productivity.

Importance of Health Management

Health management is important because healthy animals grow faster, reproduce efficiently, resist diseases better, and produce more meat and milk.

Disease outbreaks may spread rapidly in poorly managed herds and flocks.

Prevention is better and cheaper than treatment.

Disease

Disease is an abnormal condition that affects the body and interferes with normal body function.

Diseases may be caused by bacteria, viruses, parasites, fungi, toxins, nutritional deficiencies, or poor management.

Disease interferes with normal body function.

Infectious Diseases

Infectious diseases are diseases caused by microorganisms that may spread from one animal to another.

Examples include bacterial diseases, viral diseases, and fungal diseases.

Infectious diseases are transmissible.

Non-Infectious Diseases

Non-infectious diseases are diseases not caused by infectious organisms.

Examples include nutritional deficiencies, poisoning, injuries, and metabolic disorders.

Non-infectious diseases are not transmissible.

Anthrax

Anthrax is a highly contagious bacterial disease caused by Bacillus anthracis.

Signs include sudden death, bleeding from body openings, and high mortality.

Anthrax is zoonotic and may infect humans.

Anthrax = Bacillus anthracis.

Mastitis

Mastitis is the inflammation of the udder usually caused by bacterial infection.

Signs include swollen udder, pain, abnormal milk, and reduced milk production.

Poor sanitation increases mastitis risk.

Mastitis = udder inflammation.

Foot Rot

Foot rot is a contagious bacterial infection affecting the feet and hooves.

Signs include lameness, foul odor, swollen hoof, and difficulty walking.

Wet and muddy environments favor foot rot development.

Foot rot causes lameness.

Pneumonia

Pneumonia is inflammation of the lungs.

It may be caused by bacteria, viruses, stress, and poor ventilation.

Signs include coughing, nasal discharge, fever, and difficulty breathing.

Pneumonia affects the lungs.

Rabies

Rabies is a fatal viral disease affecting the nervous system.

It is transmitted through bites of infected animals.

Signs include aggression, nervous signs, paralysis, and abnormal behavior.

Rabies affects the nervous system.

Foot-and-Mouth Disease (FMD)

Foot-and-Mouth Disease is a highly contagious viral disease affecting cloven-hoofed animals.

Signs include mouth blisters, excessive salivation, fever, and lameness.

FMD spreads rapidly among susceptible animals.

FMD = highly contagious viral disease.

Parasites

Parasites are organisms living in or on another organism and obtaining nourishment from it.

Parasites may be internal or external.

Parasites harm the host animal.

Internal Parasites

Internal parasites live inside the body.

Examples include roundworms, tapeworms, and liver flukes.

Effects include anemia, diarrhea, weakness, weight loss, and poor growth.

Internal parasites live inside the body.

External Parasites

External parasites live outside the body.

Examples include ticks, lice, mites, and fleas.

They may cause irritation, blood loss, and disease transmission.

External parasites live outside the body.

Liver Fluke

Liver fluke is a parasitic worm affecting the liver.

Signs include anemia, weakness, poor growth, and weight loss.

Liver fluke commonly occurs in wet areas.

Liver fluke affects the liver.

Mange

Mange is a skin disease caused by mites.

Signs include itching, hair loss, skin thickening, and crust formation.

Mange = mite infestation.

Bloat

Bloat is the excessive accumulation of gas in the rumen.

It may result from excessive intake of legumes or improper feeding management.

Signs include swollen abdomen, discomfort, and difficulty breathing.

Bloat = gas accumulation in rumen.

Enterotoxemia

Enterotoxemia is a toxic disease caused by Clostridium bacteria.

It is commonly associated with sudden diet changes or overeating concentrates.

Signs may include sudden death, diarrhea, and nervous signs.

Enterotoxemia is associated with overeating.

Ketosis

Ketosis is a metabolic disorder caused by energy deficiency.

It commonly occurs in pregnant or lactating animals.

Signs include weakness, reduced appetite, and weight loss.

Ketosis = energy deficiency disorder.

Disease Prevention

Disease prevention aims to stop disease occurrence before infection develops.

Prevention methods include vaccination, sanitation, biosecurity, proper nutrition, and quarantine.

Prevention is better than cure.

Vaccination

Vaccination is the administration of vaccines to stimulate immunity against disease.

Vaccination helps reduce disease outbreaks and mortality.

Vaccination stimulates immunity.

Vaccine

A vaccine is a biological preparation used to develop immunity.

Vaccines may contain weakened organisms, killed organisms, or toxoids.

Vaccine develops immunity.

Deworming

Deworming is the administration of anti-parasitic drugs to eliminate internal parasites.

Regular deworming improves growth, feed efficiency, and animal health.

Deworming controls internal parasites.

Quarantine

Quarantine is the isolation of newly acquired or sick animals.

It prevents disease spread and allows observation for symptoms.

Quarantine prevents disease transmission.

Isolation

Isolation is separating sick animals from healthy animals.

Isolation helps reduce disease transmission within the herd or flock.

Isolation separates diseased animals.

Sanitation

Sanitation refers to cleanliness and hygienic practices in animal facilities.

Practices include regular cleaning, manure disposal, disinfection, and clean water supply.

Sanitation reduces disease risk.

Disinfection

Disinfection is the destruction of harmful microorganisms using chemicals or disinfectants.

Disinfection kills harmful microorganisms.

Biosecurity

Biosecurity is the prevention of disease introduction and spread.

Practices include visitor control, disinfection, quarantine, and proper waste disposal.

Biosecurity prevents disease spread.

Observation

Daily observation helps detect disease early.

Animals should be checked for appetite, breathing, movement, feces, and behavior.

Early detection improves treatment success.

Signs of Healthy Animals

Healthy goats and sheep are usually active, alert, bright-eyed, smooth-coated, and have good appetite and normal breathing.

Healthy animals are active and alert.

Signs of Sick Animals

Sick animals may show loss of appetite, weakness, diarrhea, coughing, nasal discharge, rough hair coat, and abnormal behavior.

Sick animals show abnormal signs.

Stress

Stress is a condition caused by unfavorable environmental or management factors.

Stress factors include overcrowding, transport, rough handling, extreme heat, and poor nutrition.

Stress lowers immunity and productivity.

Stress reduces disease resistance.

Humane Handling

Humane handling means proper treatment of animals without unnecessary pain, fear, or injury.

Good handling practices reduce stress and improve animal welfare.

Humane handling improves welfare.

Proper Nutrition

Proper nutrition strengthens immunity, growth, reproduction, and disease resistance.

Malnutrition increases disease susceptibility.

Nutrition affects immunity.

Clean Water Supply

Clean water is important in maintaining health and preventing disease.

Contaminated water may spread parasites, bacteria, and viruses.

Clean water prevents disease.

Zoonotic Diseases

Zoonotic diseases are diseases transmissible from animals to humans.

Examples include anthrax and rabies.

Zoonotic diseases can infect humans.
