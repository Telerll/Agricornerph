🌱 Crop Science

Part 4- Propagation & Production Factors

A. Sexual Propagation

  • Most common method by which plants reproduce in nature
  • Most efficient and widely used method for cultivated crops
  • Seeds arise from the fusion of male and female gametes to form a single cell (zygote) within the ovule of a flower

Seed Germination

  • Series of events which take place when dry quiescent seeds imbibe water, resulting in an increase in metabolic activity and the initiation of a seedling from the embryo
  • Resumption of embryo growth

Epigeous germination – hypocotyl elongates and brings cotyledons above ground

Hypogeous germination – epicotyl emerges and the cotyledons remain below the soil surface

Seed Dormancy

Physiological or physical condition of a viable seed that prevents germination even in the presence of otherwise favorable germination conditions

Seed Quiescence

Condition in which seed cannot germinate because of unfavorable conditions

Types of Dormancy

  • Primary
    • Exogenous or coat-imposed dormancy – essential germination components not available
    • Endogenous dormancy – caused by environment during seed development and maturation
  • Secondary – imposed by temperature, light/darkness, abnormal amount of water, chemicals, and gases

Dormancy Technology

  • Ecodormancy – due to one or more unsuitable factors in the environment with non-specific effect
  • Paradormancy – due to physical factors or biochemical signals originating externally to the affected structure
  • Endodormancy – regulated by physiological factors inside the affected structure

Hastening Seed Germination / Breaking Dormancy

Scarification – treatment that removes the seed coat or alters it, making it more permeable to water and air.

  • Physical scarification – soaking in water (tap, hot, or boiling water) for a specific period of time
  • Mechanical scarification – piercing, rubbing on sandpaper, filing, grinding with abrasives
  • Chemical scarification – treatment with sulfuric acid and organic solvents

Stratification – placement of seeds between layers of moist sand, soil, or sawdust at high or low temperature so the action of water and temperature will soften the seed coat

Vernalization – seed treatment with cold temperature prior to germination

Embryo culture – aseptically removing the embryo from the seed and placing it in a sterilized culture medium to germinate

B. Asexual Propagation

Involves reproduction from vegetative parts of plants and is possible because the vegetative organs of many plants have the capacity for regeneration.

1. Propagation by Apomictic Embryos

Apomixis – the production of viable seeds without pollination.

  • Embryos contain the same genetic information as the mother plant and are in effect clones
  • Reproduction of embryo without meiosis and fertilization; embryos arise from vegetative cells within the ovule

Obligate Apomictic – reproduces only by asexual reproduction
Ex. Lanzones, Mangosteen

Facultative Apomictic – reproduces either sexually or asexually
Ex. Mango, Citrus

2. Separation and Division

Separation – involves separating naturally detachable organs from the mother plant

Division – specialized vegetative structures are cut into sections which may be separated and/or divided

  • Bulb – specialized underground organ; Ex. tulips, lilies
  • Bulbil – aerial plant formed on the axil of the leaves or flower stalk; Ex. agave
  • Corm – swollen base of a stem axis enclosed by dry scale leaves; Ex. banana, gladiolus, gabi
  • Cormel – miniature corm which develops between old and new corms
  • Crown – part of a plant at the soil surface from which new shoots are produced; Ex. aster, Shasta daisy
  • Offset / Offshoot – lateral shoot from base of the main stem; Ex. Pistia sp.
  • Pseudobulb – enlarged fleshy section of the stem; Ex. Cattleya sp.
  • Rhizome – main axis grows horizontally at or just below the ground surface; Ex. banana, bamboo, sugarcane
  • Runner – horizontal stem forming a new plant at one node; Ex. strawberry, black pepper
  • Slip – leafy shoot from axillary buds at the base or peduncle; Ex. pineapple, cabbage
  • Stolon – modified stem that grows horizontally; Ex. Bermuda grass
  • Sucker – adventitious shoot arising from underground stems; Ex. banana, pineapple
  • Tuber – modified stem structure developing below ground
  • Tuberous root – thickened root containing stored food; Ex. cassava, sweet potato

3. Cutting

A portion of a stem, root, or leaf is cut from the parent plant and induced to form roots and shoots.

  • Root cutting – Ex. breadfruit, apple
  • Stem cutting – hardwood, semi-hardwood, softwood, herbaceous; Ex. cassava, malunggay, coffee, rose
  • Leaf cutting – Ex. snake plant, begonia, African violet
  • Leaf-bud cutting – Ex. black pepper, vanilla

4. Layering

A propagation method by which adventitious roots are induced to form on a stem while it is still attached to the parent plant.

  • Simple layering
  • Air layering or marcotting
  • Compound or serpentine layering
  • Mound or stool layering
  • Trench layering

5. Grafting

Connecting parts of plants together in such a manner that they will unite and continue their growth as one plant.

  • Scion – upper portion of graft combination
  • Rootstock – lower portion which develops into the root system
  • Interstock – piece of stem inserted between scion and rootstock

Types of Graftage:

  • Approach Grafting / Inarching – Joining two living plants while both remain attached to their own roots until the graft heals.
  • Topworking – The process of changing an established tree’s variety by grafting new scions onto its upper main branches.
  • Splice Grafting – Joining a scion and rootstock of equal thickness using identical long, diagonal cuts.
  • Cleft Grafting – Inserting a wedge-shaped scion into a vertical split made in the center of a sawed-off rootstock.
  • Side Grafting – Inserting a scion into a notch on the side of a rootstock without cutting the main stem off first.
  • Bark Grafting – Slipping small scions between the bark and the wood of a thick, established rootstock.

6. Budding

An asexual propagation method like grafting, but the scion is reduced to only one bud and a small section of bark, with or without wood.

    Types of Budding

    • Patch Budding – used for thick bark; bark easily separates from wood.
    • Shield or T-budding – done on actively growing stems; bark readily peels.
    • Chip Budding – used when bark does not separate easily from wood.

    7. Inarching (Approach Grafting)

    An asexual propagation technique in which plants are made to unite while growing on their roots.

    8. Tissue Culture Techniques

    Can be started from a variety of plant parts which have cells capable of dividing.

    Ex. shoot-tip culture, meristem culture, endosperm culture

IX. Factors Affecting Crop Production

Crop production can be viewed from two properties:

  • at the crop level
  • at the systems level

Equation: Y = f [(G + E + (G × E))]

  • Y = yield
  • G = genotype
  • E = environment
  • G × E = interaction of genotype and environment

Genotype

  • Genetic design of a plant which dictates the ceiling of how much a variety / cultivar can yield
  • Genes controlling a character such as yield, plant height, taste, and color
  • Varies among and even within species
  • Sets the ultimate limit for plant variation

Environment

  • Any factor external to the plant that influences its growth and development
  • May be biotic or abiotic; examples are climate, soil, topography, pests, and diseases

G × E Interaction

  • A high-yielding variety grown under poor environment will have low yield
  • A low-yielding variety grown in optimum environment will still have low yield
  • An ideal genotype performs well over a wide range of environments
  • An optimum environment poses minimum constraints to crop growth and development

Practical Implications

  1. Develop management practices that can remove or avoid environmental constraints
  2. Continuously assess G × E interaction
  3. Need for continuous development of improved varieties

Production System

  • Inputs – seeds, fertilizers, pesticides, etc.
  • Output – yield
  • Environment – uncontrollable factors external to the system
  • System – component crops, processes, and activities

X. Environmental Factors

Includes climatic or above-ground factors and edaphic or soil factors. Also includes biotic factors.

A. Climatic Factors

Climate – seasonal pattern of a particular place occurring from year to year.

Weather – momentary state of the atmosphere.

Macroclimate – climatic environment one meter above the plant canopy.

Microclimate – climatic environment one meter below the canopy or within the leaf canopy.

Climatic Elements

  • Precipitation – rainfall, hail, snow, etc.
  • Temperature – influences every chemical, physiological, and biological process in plants
  • Wind – increases transpiration and may cause lodging, shattering, and pollen loss
  • Solar radiation or light – affects photosynthesis, development, and photoperiod response
  • Relative humidity – affects evapotranspiration
  • Gaseous environment – CO2 and air pollutants
  • Cloudiness – affects amount of radiation received by plants

Light-based Classification

  • Heliophytes – sun-loving
  • Sciophytes – shade-loving

Photoperiodic Response

  • Day neutral
  • Short day plant
  • Long day plant

Climatic Stresses

  • Typhoon and weather variations
  • Ozone destruction
  • Global warming
  • El Niño / La Niña
  • Acid rain
  • Lahar

B. Edaphic Factors

Refers to the soil as a factor in crop production.

Soil Properties

  • Soil Texture – proportion of sand, silt, and clay
  • Soil Structure – aggregation of soil particles
  • Bulk Density – dry weight per unit volume of soil
  • Soil pH – favorable range for most crops is pH 6 to 7
  • CEC – ability of soil to adsorb and exchange cations
  • Soil Organic Matter – improves physical, chemical, and biological properties
  • Soil Organisms – important in decomposition and soil aggregation

Other Factors

  • Biotic Factors – beneficial organisms, pollinators, decomposers, natural pest enemies, pests
  • Genetic Factors – genotype and genome
  • Human Factors – farmer’s preference, capability, and management

XI. Crop Improvement and Seed Selection

Plant Breeding

Science, art, and business of crop improvement for human benefit.

1. Creation of Variation

  • Domestication – conversion of wild plants into cultivated forms
  • Germplasm collection – gathering genetic materials of crops and relatives
  • Introduction – bringing plants to a new environment
  • Hybridization – crossing genetically different plants
  • Inbreeding / Inbred – mating related plants / resulting homozygous line
  • Topcross – cross between inbred and open-pollinated variety
  • Testcross – cross with tester to determine genotype
  • Backcross – hybrid crossed with one parent to recover traits
  • Polycross – random crossing among selected genotypes
  • Reciprocal cross – parents used alternately as male and female
  • Intraspecific cross – crossing within the same species
  • Wide or distant cross – crossing different species or distant relatives
  • Introgressive hybridization – repeated backcrossing to transfer genes
  • Heterosis / hybrid vigor – superiority of F1 over parents
  • Combining ability – ability to pass desirable traits to offspring
  • Inbreeding depression – loss of vigor due to inbreeding
  • Mutation – heritable genetic change
  • Chimera – plant with different genotypes in tissues
  • Polyploidization – increase in chromosome sets
  • Genetic engineering – direct manipulation of genes

2. Selection

  • Natural selection – selection driven by environmental survival
  • Artificial selection – selection controlled by humans
  • Stabilizing selection – favors average traits, reduces variation
  • Directional selection – favors one extreme trait
  • Diversifying selection – favors both extreme traits
  • Qualitative traits – traits controlled by few genes (discrete)
  • Quantitative traits – traits controlled by many genes (continuous)
  • Phenotypic variance – total observable variation (G + E + G×E)
  • Genotypic variance – variation due to genetic differences
  • Environmental variance – variation due to environmental factors
  • G × E interaction – genotype response differs across environments
  • Heritability – proportion of variation due to genetics
  • Selection intensity – proportion of individuals selected
  • Selection differential – difference between selected and population mean
  • Gain from selection – improvement achieved after selection

3. Evaluation

  • Observational yield test – initial testing of lines in one location
  • Preliminary yield test – early evaluation with check variety in few locations
  • General yield test – multi-location testing for performance comparison
  • Advanced yield test – final testing across many locations for stability
  • DUS-test – test for distinctness, uniformity, and stability of a variety

4. Multiplication

  • Seed production – process of producing seeds for planting
  • Isolation – separation to prevent unwanted cross-pollination
  • Seed processing – cleaning, drying, grading, and treating seeds
  • Hybrids – F1 offspring from crossing two different parents
  • Synthetics – mixture of selected genotypes maintained by open pollination
  • Composites – mixed population of genotypes maintained naturally
  • Inbreds – pure lines produced by repeated selfing
  • Multilines – mixture of similar lines differing in few genes
  • Isolines – lines genetically identical except for one gene
  • Open-pollinated variety – variety maintained by natural pollination
  • Landraces – locally adapted traditional varieties

Hybrid Seed Production

  • 3-line system – uses A (male sterile), B (maintainer), and R (restorer) lines to produce hybrid seeds
  • 2-line system – uses environment-sensitive male sterile line and a pollen parent
  • 1-line system – uses apomixis to produce uniform hybrid seeds without crossing

5. Distribution

  • Breeder seeds – original pure seeds produced by plant breeder
  • Foundation seeds – seeds produced from breeder seeds
  • Registered seeds – seeds produced from foundation seeds
  • Certified seeds – seeds produced for commercial farmer use
  • Good seeds – seeds from varieties not yet officially certified

6. Conservation of Germplasm

  • Seed storage in cold/refrigerated rooms
  • Maintained as living plants in field or through slow growth in vitro
  • Cryopreservation

XII. Sustainable Crop Production

Sustainable Agriculture

An integrated system of plant and animal production that over the long term will satisfy human needs, enhance environmental quality, sustain economic viability, and improve quality of life.

Sustainability

  • Ability of a system to maintain productivity despite major disturbance
  • Capacity to maintain output at a level approximately equal to or greater than its historical average

Characteristics of Sustainable Agriculture

  • Flexible – adaptable to different conditions and practices
  • Experiential – based on learning through experience
  • Participatory – involves active farmer and community involvement
  • Proactive – forward-looking and focused on long-term sustainability

Features / Attributes / Dimensions

  • Continuously evolving – dynamic and adaptable over time
  • Gender sensitive – recognizes roles and needs of all genders
  • Ecologically sound and friendly – protects and sustains the environment
  • Culturally appropriate – respects local traditions and practices
  • Economically viable – provides sustainable income and profit
  • Location specific – suited to local conditions and environment
  • Resource-based – uses and manages available local resources efficiently
  • Social equity – promotes fairness and equal access to resources
  • Holistic approach – considers the whole system and interactions
  • Enhances human values – promotes well-being and ethical responsibility

Focus for Action

  • Long-term sustainability rather than short-term benefits
  • Internal solutions to internal problems
  • Management solutions rather than merely technological solutions
  • Low rather than high external input
  • Systems approach rather than monoculture
  • Use of appropriate and indigenous technologies

Approaches / Systems

  • Biodynamic farming – farming based on natural and spiritual principles to enhance soil and crop health
  • Biological / ecological farming – minimizes chemical use and relies on natural processes
  • Natural farming – no tillage, no chemicals, minimal human intervention
  • Kyusei Nature Farming – uses beneficial microorganisms to improve soil and crop productivity
  • Permaculture – design system integrating plants, animals, and environment sustainably
  • Organic farming – avoids synthetic fertilizers and pesticides
  • Regenerative agriculture – restores and improves soil and ecosystem health
  • Precision farming / site-specific management – uses data and technology to manage crops efficiently per area
