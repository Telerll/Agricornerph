1. Accounting as a Language

Accounting is widely recognized as the “Language of Business.”

2. Basic Accounting Principles or Concepts

1. First Principle: Money as the Common Denominator

Using money as a unit of measurement allows different business transactions and activities to be expressed in numerical form so they can be compared, added, and analyzed.

2. Second Principle: The Business Entity Principle

A business organization is treated separately from the individuals connected with it, including its owners or members.

3. Third Principle: Value Equals Cost

Items of value owned by the business are known as assets. In accounting practice, these assets are usually recorded based on the amount paid to acquire them.

4. Fourth Principle: The Dual Aspect Principle

Properties and resources owned by the business are called assets.

The claims against these assets come from:

  1. Liabilities – obligations owed to creditors
  2. Owner’s equity / Members’ equity / Net worth / Capital – claims of the owners or members

5. Fifth Principle: The Accrual Principle

Net income represents changes in the owner’s equity resulting from business operations rather than changes in cash balance alone.

3. Application of the Basic Accounting Principles on the Balance Sheet

The balance sheet is a major accounting report used to record and present the effects of transactions on the financial condition of the business.

Its purpose is to show where the cooperative’s resources are invested and the sources from which these resources came. Since transactions continuously affect finances, the balance sheet presents the financial position of the cooperative at a particular date.

4. Fundamental Conventions of Accountants

a. Conventions

The principle of consistency states that once a company adopts a specific accounting method, the same method should continue to be applied to similar transactions and events.

b. Conservatism

The doctrine of conservatism suggests that when there are reasonable alternatives, accountants normally choose the option that reports lower asset values or lower owner’s equity.

c. Materiality

In accounting, insignificant matters are often disregarded when recording them would not provide meaningful value or benefit.

Development of Framework for Growth and Expansion

Growth and expansion may be achieved through three related approaches:

1. Horizontal and Vertical Integration

  • Horizontal integration involves combining organizations or groups engaged in similar types of business activities.
  • Vertical integration refers to expanding business operations into another stage or level of activity.

2. Diversification

Diversification means widening the scope of services by introducing additional service lines or activities.

3. Consolidation and Merger

Worldwide business trends commonly show continuous organizational expansion.

  • Consolidation refers to the combination of two or more associations to create a completely new association.
  • Merger refers to the joining of associations where one organization absorbs another without creating a new entity.

Strategies to Generate Volume Through Member Patronage

Cooperatives may apply the following strategies to increase member patronage and business volume:

  1. Developing a large base of qualified members
  2. Providing economical and efficient services to members
  3. Implementing effective practices that increase business volume

Pooling Method of Cooperative Marketing

Pooling is a marketing method in which members’ products are combined and sold collectively. Payments are later distributed based on the average proceeds after deducting total expenses.

Objectives of Pooling

  1. To spread marketing risks among participating members
  2. To help management prepare organized and realistic marketing plans
  3. To achieve savings in distribution costs

Adequate and Sound Financing

General Classification of Capital

1. Equity Capital

This refers to capital contributions provided by cooperative members.

2. Loan Capital

Loan capital consists of funds borrowed by the cooperative from outside lenders or creditors.

Common Methods of Raising Equity Capital

1. Sale of Shares of Stock

a. Common Stock

Common stock represents a type of share capital that may earn limited interest.

  • Stockholder – a person who owns shares in a cooperative corporation and has voting rights.
  • Stock Certificate – a document issued as proof of ownership of shares of stock.

b. Preferred Stock

Preferred stock is another option used to strengthen the capitalization of a cooperative and is often offered to supporters or individuals who may not qualify as regular stockholders.

2. Collection of Member’s Capital Contribution

Applicable to non-stock cooperative corporations.

3. Collection of Retains

Retains, also known as capital retains, are authorized deductions taken from sales proceeds, usually computed on a per-unit basis.

4. Deferment of Distribution of Patronage Refund

This method is used to retain additional capital within the cooperative.

Accumulation of Reserves

a. Common Kinds and Uses of Reserves

i. Valuation Reserves

These reserves are intended to cover bad debt losses and depreciation or obsolescence of fixed assets such as buildings, land, and equipment.

ii. Liquidity Reserves

Liquidity reserves are set aside to absorb operational losses and meet unexpected liabilities or contingencies.

iii. Capital Reserves

Capital reserves provide funds for working capital and investments in different forms of capital contributions.

Common Methods of Raising Loan Capital

  1. Borrowings – the most widely used way of obtaining funds from lenders
  2. Deferred Payments – payment arrangements made after goods have already been sold
  3. Trade Credit – obtaining goods with a promise of future payment
  4. Sale of Bonds and Debentures – long-term financing instruments used to raise capital
  5. Donations, Grants, Gifts, and Similar Support – financial assistance intended for cooperative growth, education, and training
