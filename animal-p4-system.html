Systems of Breeding

Systems of Breeding

Systems of breeding are planned mating methods used to improve animals and produce desired offspring.

They determine how males and females are mated to achieve breeding goals.

Breeding system = planned mating strategy.

Importance of Breeding Systems

Breeding systems are important because they help:

  • Improve desirable traits
  • Maintain breed quality
  • Increase productivity
  • Control inbreeding
  • Combine useful traits
Breeding systems determine long-term herd improvement.

Major Breeding Systems

The major systems of breeding are:

  • Inbreeding
  • Linebreeding
  • Outcrossing
  • Crossbreeding
  • Grading up
  • Purebreeding
Each breeding system has a different purpose.

Inbreeding

Inbreeding is the mating of closely related animals.

Examples:

  • Sire × daughter
  • Dam × son
  • Brother × sister

This increases the chance that offspring inherit similar genes from both parents.

The degree of inbreeding is measured by the Inbreeding Coefficient (F).

  • F = 0 means no inbreeding
  • F = 1 means complete inbreeding
Inbreeding increases genetic similarity.

Effects of Inbreeding

Inbreeding increases:

  • Homozygosity
  • Uniformity
  • Prepotency

It may also expose harmful recessive genes.

Inbreeding fixes traits but increases genetic risk.

Advantages of Inbreeding

  • Produces uniform animals
  • Fixes desirable traits
  • Reveals hidden recessive genes
  • Produces prepotent sires
Inbreeding helps stabilize traits.

Disadvantages of Inbreeding

  • Inbreeding depression
  • Reduced fertility
  • Lower vigor
  • Poorer survival
  • Increased genetic defects
Too much inbreeding reduces performance.

Inbreeding Depression

Inbreeding depression is the decline in performance caused by excessive inbreeding.

Traits most affected are usually:

  • Fertility
  • Fitness
  • Vigor
  • Survival

Common effects:

  • Low fertility
  • Weak offspring
  • Poor growth
  • Reduced survival
Inbreeding depression is the major danger of inbreeding.

Linebreeding

Linebreeding is a mild form of inbreeding used to maintain relationship to a superior ancestor.

It is less intense than close inbreeding.

Goal:

  • Retain desirable genes from an outstanding ancestor
Linebreeding preserves superior bloodlines.

Advantages of Linebreeding

  • Maintains desirable ancestry
  • Less risky than close inbreeding
  • Improves uniformity
  • Preserves elite bloodlines
  • Helps fix desirable traits
Linebreeding is controlled inbreeding.

Outcrossing

Outcrossing is mating unrelated animals within the same breed.

Both animals belong to the same breed but are not closely related.

Outcrossing reduces inbreeding within a breed.

Advantages of Outcrossing

  • Reduces inbreeding
  • Restores vigor
  • Maintains breed purity
  • Improves performance
Outcrossing improves vigor without changing breed.

Crossbreeding

Crossbreeding is mating animals of different breeds.

Purpose:

  • Combine strengths of two or more breeds
  • Improve performance
  • Exploit heterosis
Crossbreeding combines breed strengths.

Heterosis

Heterosis or hybrid vigor is the superior performance of crossbred offspring compared with the average of parent breeds.

Examples:

  • Better growth
  • Better fertility
  • Better survival
  • Better adaptability

Heterosis is the opposite of inbreeding depression.

Heterosis is the biggest advantage of crossbreeding.

Advantages of Crossbreeding

  • Improves vigor
  • Improves fertility
  • Improves growth
  • Improves adaptability
  • Combines breed strengths
Crossbreeding improves commercial performance.

Disadvantages of Crossbreeding

  • Less uniform offspring
  • Difficult replacement system
  • Less breed purity
  • Management becomes more complex
Crossbreeding improves performance but complicates breeding.

Grading Up

Grading up is repeated mating of non-purebred females with purebred sires of the same breed.

Purpose:

  • Improve local stock
  • Gradually approach purebred quality

A female is often considered purebred by cross after 5 to 6 generations.

Grading up improves native stock using purebred sires.

Effect of Grading Up

With repeated generations, offspring become genetically closer to the pure breed.

  • 1st generation = 50%
  • 2nd generation = 75%
  • 3rd generation = 87.5%
  • 4th generation = 93.75%
  • 5th generation = 96.88%
  • 6th generation = 98.44%
Grading up gradually upgrades the herd.

Purebreeding

Purebreeding is mating animals of the same breed to maintain breed purity.

Purpose:

  • Preserve breed identity
  • Maintain breed standards
Purebreeding preserves breed purity.

Breeding System Choice

Choice of breeding system depends on:

  • Breeding goal
  • Species
  • Herd size
  • Management level
  • Available breeding stock
  • Environment

Example:

  • Crossbreeding may combine high production of exotic breeds with heat tolerance of native breeds.
Breeding system must match production goals and environment.

Practical Use in Animal Production

In practice:

  • Purebreeding = breed preservation
  • Linebreeding = elite line maintenance
  • Crossbreeding = commercial production
  • Grading up = native herd improvement
Each system has a practical use.

Board Exam Master Concept

Breeding systems control:

  • Genetic variation
  • Uniformity
  • Vigor
  • Productivity
Breeding system determines herd structure and progress.

Board Exam Secret

  • Want uniformity? → Inbreed
  • Want vigor or performance? → Crossbreed
  • Want to fix a trait? → Linebreed
  • Want to upgrade native stock? → Grade Up
Choose breeding system based on breeding goal.
