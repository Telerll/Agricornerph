🌍 Part 10: Soil Management & Classification

1. Soil Management

Soil management is the proper use, maintenance, and improvement of soil to sustain productivity and prevent degradation.

It involves practices that maintain soil health while supporting crop production.

Goals of Soil Management

  • Maintain soil fertility
  • Prevent soil degradation
  • Ensure long-term productivity

2. Soil Fertility Management

Soil fertility management refers to practices that maintain or improve the nutrient status of the soil.

Methods

  • Application of fertilizers
  • Addition of organic matter such as manure and compost
  • Crop rotation
Soil fertility management ensures that plants receive essential nutrients for growth.

3. Soil Conservation

Soil conservation is the protection of soil from erosion, degradation, and loss of productivity.

Methods of Soil Conservation

  • Contour farming
  • Terracing
  • Strip cropping
  • Windbreaks
Soil conservation prevents loss of topsoil and helps maintain soil quality.

4. Soil Erosion

Soil erosion is the removal of topsoil by natural forces such as water and wind or by human activities.

Types of Soil Erosion

  • Water erosion
  • Wind erosion
Topsoil is the most fertile layer, so its loss reduces soil productivity.

5. Soil Degradation

Soil degradation is the decline in soil quality due to misuse or natural processes.

Causes of Soil Degradation

  • Over-cultivation
  • Deforestation
  • Pollution
Soil degradation results in reduced fertility and lower productivity.

6. Soil Salinity

Soil salinity is the accumulation of soluble salts in the soil.

Effects of Soil Salinity

  • Reduces plant growth
  • Interferes with water uptake by plants

7. Soil Acidity and Alkalinity

Soil Acidity

Soil acidity refers to soils with low pH.

It is common in areas with high rainfall.

Soil Alkalinity

Soil alkalinity refers to soils with high pH.

It is common in dry regions.

8. Soil Amendments

Soil amendments are materials added to soil to improve its physical or chemical properties.

Examples of Soil Amendments

  • Lime – reduces soil acidity
  • Organic matter – improves soil structure and fertility

9. Soil Classification

Soil classification is the grouping of soils based on their physical, chemical, and biological properties.

Importance of Soil Classification

  • Land use planning
  • Agricultural decision-making
  • Soil management

10. Soil Taxonomy

Soil taxonomy is a scientific system used to classify soils based on measurable characteristics such as horizons, texture, and composition.

11. Soil Orders

1. Entisols

Definition: Very young soils with little to no horizon development.

Key idea: Minimal soil formation; found in recently deposited or erosion-prone areas.

Suborders

  • Aquents – wet, saturated conditions
  • Fluvents – alluvial or river-deposited soils
  • Orthents – eroded or shallow soils
  • Psamments – sandy soils

2. Inceptisols

Definition: Young soils with weakly developed horizons.

Key idea: Early stage of soil profile development.

Suborders

  • Aquepts – wet soils
  • Udepts – humid climates
  • Ustepts – semi-arid climates
  • Ochrepts – weak horizon development

3. Alfisols

Definition: Moderately leached soils with clay accumulation (Bt horizon) and relatively high fertility.

Key idea: Productive soils, common in temperate regions.

Suborders

  • Aqualfs – wet conditions
  • Udalfs – humid climates
  • Ustalfs – seasonal dry climates
  • Xeralfs – Mediterranean climates

4. Ultisols

Definition: Strongly weathered soils with low base saturation and acidic condition.

Key idea: Less fertile than Alfisols and require management.

Suborders

  • Aquults – wet conditions
  • Udults – humid climates
  • Ustults – semi-arid climates
  • Xerults – Mediterranean climates

5. Oxisols

Definition: Highly weathered tropical soils rich in iron and aluminum oxides.

Key idea: Very old soils with low natural fertility.

Suborders

  • Aquox – wet tropical soils
  • Udox – humid tropics
  • Ustox – seasonal dry tropics
  • Perox – extremely weathered soils

6. Mollisols

Definition: Dark, organic-rich soils with high fertility.

Key idea: Common in grassland areas.

7. Vertisols

Definition: Clay-rich soils that shrink when dry and swell when wet.

Key idea: Forms deep cracks during dry periods.

8. Aridisols

Definition: Soils of arid regions with limited moisture.

Key idea: Low in organic matter and may accumulate salts.

9. Spodosols

Definition: Acidic soils with accumulation of organic matter, iron, and aluminum in the subsoil.

Key idea: Common in forest regions.

10. Histosols

Definition: Soils composed mainly of organic matter.

Key idea: Found in swamps and wetlands.

11. Andisols

Definition: Soils formed from volcanic ash.

Key idea: Usually have high water-holding capacity.

12. Gelisols

Definition: Soils with permafrost near the surface.

Key idea: Found in very cold regions.

12. Land Capability Classification

Land capability classification is a system that groups land based on its suitability for agricultural use.

Classes

  • Class I – most suitable for farming
  • Class VIII – not suitable for cultivation

13. Soil Survey

Soil survey is the systematic examination, description, classification, and mapping of soils.

Uses of Soil Survey

  • Land use planning
  • Agriculture
  • Engineering

14. Sustainable Soil Management

Sustainable soil management is the use of soil in a way that maintains its productivity and environmental quality over time.

Focus

  • Conservation
  • Productivity
  • Environmental protection

Final Summary

Soil Management = proper use and maintenance of soil
Soil Classification = grouping of soils based on characteristics
Soil Taxonomy = scientific system of soil classification
