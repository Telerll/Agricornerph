Microeconomics

Microeconomics is the branch of economics that focuses on individual economic units such as farmers, consumers, households, firms, traders, and markets. It studies how these economic units make decisions regarding the allocation of scarce resources.

In agriculture, microeconomics is important because it helps explain the behavior of farmers and consumers in relation to production, pricing, costs, profits, and consumption of agricultural products.

Filipino farmers must decide what crops to plant, how much fertilizer to use, what technology to adopt, how much labor to hire, when to sell products, and how to minimize cost while maximizing profit.

Production and Cost Theory of Production

Production refers to the process of transforming inputs or resources into outputs or finished goods and services. In agriculture, production involves combining land, labor, capital, technology, and management to produce crops, livestock, fishery products, and other agricultural commodities.

The Theory of Production studies the relationship between inputs and outputs. It explains how changes in inputs affect the quantity of output produced.

Production Function

A production function shows the technical relationship between inputs and outputs. It explains how much output can be produced using a given combination of inputs under existing technology.

Inputs

Inputs are resources used in production.

  • Fixed Inputs: resources that do not change in the short run even if production changes, such as farmland, farm buildings, irrigation systems, and machinery.
  • Variable Inputs: resources that change depending on production level, such as seeds, fertilizers, labor, feeds, and pesticides.

Outputs

Outputs are the products produced using inputs, such as palay, corn, vegetables, hogs, poultry, and fishery products.

Total Product (TP)

Total Product refers to the total quantity of output produced using available inputs.

A rice farm producing 200 cavans of palay represents total product.

Marginal Product (MP)

Marginal Product refers to the additional output produced when one additional unit of input is added while other inputs remain constant.

Hiring one additional worker that increases harvest from 200 to 220 cavans gives a marginal product of 20 cavans.

Average Product (AP)

Average Product refers to the average output produced per unit of input used in production. It measures productivity, especially labor productivity.

AP = Total Product ÷ Number of Inputs
If 10 farm workers produce 200 cavans of palay, AP = 200 ÷ 10 = 20 cavans per worker.

Law of Diminishing Returns

The Law of Diminishing Returns states that as additional units of a variable input are added to a fixed input, the additional output eventually decreases.

Adding too much fertilizer may eventually reduce crop quality and productivity instead of increasing output.

Stages of Production

Stage I

Stage I is characterized by increasing returns. Additional inputs result in increasing productivity and efficiency.

Stage II

Stage II is the rational stage of production. Marginal product begins to decline but remains positive. Producers usually operate in this stage because resources are used efficiently.

Stage III

Stage III occurs when marginal product becomes negative and total output decreases. Production becomes inefficient.

Length of Run

Short Run

In the short run, some inputs remain fixed while others are variable. For example, land area may remain fixed while labor and fertilizer may change.

Long Run

In the long run, all inputs become variable. Producers can expand farm size, purchase additional machinery, or adopt new technology.

Very Short Run

Very Short Run is a period so short that none of the inputs are variable.

Returns to Scale

Returns to Scale refers to the quantitative change in output resulting from a proportionate increase in all inputs used in production.

  • Constant Returns to Scale: output increases in the same proportion as input increases.
  • Increasing Returns to Scale: output increases by a greater proportion than input increases.
  • Decreasing Returns to Scale: output increases by a smaller proportion than input increases.

Factors of Production

  • Land: natural resources such as farmland, forests, rivers, minerals, and water resources.
  • Labor: human effort used in production, including farm workers, machine operators, and technicians.
  • Capital: tools, machinery, irrigation systems, buildings, and equipment used in production.
  • Entrepreneurship: ability to organize resources, manage operations, innovate, and assume risks.

Theory of Cost

Production involves costs because resources used in production have economic value. The Theory of Cost explains how production costs behave as output changes.

  • Fixed Cost: costs that remain constant regardless of production level, such as land rent, depreciation, insurance, and salaries of permanent workers.
  • Variable Cost: costs that change depending on production level, such as seeds, fertilizers, feeds, fuel, and labor.

Total Cost (TC)

Total Cost refers to the market value of all inputs used in production.

TC = TFC + TVC

Explicit Costs

Explicit costs are costs that require actual cash payment, such as wages, fertilizer expenses, fuel, feeds, pesticide costs, and machinery rental.

Implicit Costs

Implicit costs are costs that do not require direct cash payment. These are opportunity costs of resources owned by the producer, such as unpaid family labor, owner's management time, owned farmland, and owned machinery.

Marginal Cost (MC)

Marginal Cost refers to the additional cost incurred in producing one more unit of output.

Average Cost (AC)

Average Cost refers to cost per unit of output.

AC = Total Cost ÷ Quantity of Output

Price and Output Determination in a Perfectly Competitive Market

A perfectly competitive market is characterized by many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, free entry and exit, and perfect information. In agriculture, some products such as palay and corn are close to perfectly competitive markets because many farmers produce similar products.

Total Revenue (TR)

Total Revenue refers to total earnings from sales.

TR = Price × Quantity Sold

Marginal Revenue (MR)

Marginal Revenue refers to additional revenue earned from selling one more unit of product.

Profit

Profit refers to the difference between total revenue and total cost.

Profit = Total Revenue − Total Cost

Accounting Profit

Accounting Profit refers to total revenue minus explicit costs only.

Accounting Profit = Total Revenue − Explicit Costs

Economic Profit

Economic Profit refers to total revenue minus both explicit and implicit costs.

Economic Profit = Total Revenue − (Explicit Costs + Implicit Costs)

Demand

Demand refers to the quantity of goods consumers are willing and able to buy at different prices.

Law of Demand

The Law of Demand states that as price increases, quantity demanded decreases, ceteris paribus.

When rice prices increase, consumers may buy less rice or shift to substitute products.

Demand Curve

The demand curve slopes downward from left to right because of the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.

Supply

Supply refers to the quantity of goods producers are willing and able to sell at different prices.

Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied also increases, ceteris paribus.

Higher vegetable prices encourage farmers to increase production.

Supply Curve

The supply curve slopes upward because of the direct relationship between price and quantity supplied.

Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs when quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. At equilibrium, there is no shortage and no surplus.

Surplus

Surplus occurs when quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. This may cause prices to decrease.

Shortage

Shortage occurs when quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. This may cause prices to increase.

Elasticity

Elasticity measures responsiveness to changes in price, income, or related variables.

Price Elasticity of Demand

Price Elasticity of Demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to price changes.

  • Elastic Demand: quantity demanded changes significantly when price changes.
  • Inelastic Demand: quantity demanded changes only slightly when price changes.
Agricultural products such as rice are often relatively inelastic because they are basic necessities.

Consumption

Consumption refers to the use of goods and services to satisfy human wants and needs.

Utility

Utility refers to satisfaction derived from consuming goods and services.

Total Utility

Total Utility refers to total satisfaction obtained from consumption.

Marginal Utility

Marginal Utility refers to additional satisfaction gained from consuming one more unit of a product.

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

As more units of a product are consumed, additional satisfaction eventually decreases.

The first glass of water provides greater satisfaction than succeeding glasses.

Consumer Equilibrium

Consumer equilibrium occurs when consumers maximize satisfaction using limited income. Consumers allocate income in a way that equalizes marginal utility per peso spent across different products.

This helps consumers achieve maximum satisfaction within their budget limitations.
