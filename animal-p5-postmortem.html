Postmortem Changes in Meat

Postmortem Changes

Postmortem changes are the biochemical and physical changes that occur in muscle after animal death.

After slaughter, muscle is gradually converted into meat.

Postmortem change = muscle becomes meat.

Importance of Postmortem Changes

Postmortem changes affect:

  • Tenderness
  • Flavor
  • Color
  • Water-holding capacity
  • Shelf life
  • Meat quality
Meat quality depends heavily on postmortem changes.

Muscle to Meat Conversion

Before slaughter, muscle cells are alive.

After death:

  • Blood circulation stops
  • Oxygen supply stops
  • Energy production changes
  • Muscle metabolism continues briefly
Meat is formed after death through biochemical change.

Glycolysis After Death

After slaughter, muscle cells are dead as part of the animal, but they remain metabolically active for a short time.

Anaerobic glycolysis continues briefly using stored muscle glycogen.

Muscle is dead, but metabolism continues briefly.

Glycogen

Glycogen is stored carbohydrate in muscle.

After slaughter, glycogen is broken down into lactic acid.

Glycogen is the fuel for postmortem acid formation.

Lactic Acid Formation

After slaughter, muscle uses glycogen without oxygen.

This produces lactic acid.

Lactic acid causes the muscle pH to decline.

Lactic acid lowers meat pH.

pH Decline

Normal live muscle pH is about 7.0.

After slaughter, pH drops to around 5.4–5.8 due to lactic acid formation.

Normal pH decline is essential for good meat quality.

Ultimate pH

Ultimate pH is the final pH reached by meat after postmortem glycolysis and rigor development.

  • Normal ultimate pH = about 5.4–5.8
  • High ultimate pH = often associated with DFD meat
  • Rapid pH fall while carcass is still hot = often associated with PSE meat
Ultimate pH is a key meat quality indicator.

Rigor Mortis

Rigor mortis is the stiffening of muscles after death.

It occurs because ATP is depleted and muscle fibers remain locked.

Rigor mortis = postmortem muscle stiffening.

ATP

ATP is the energy molecule needed for muscle relaxation.

When ATP is depleted after death, muscles cannot relax.

ATP depletion is the direct cause of rigor mortis.

Actin, Myosin, and Actomyosin

Actin and myosin are muscle proteins involved in contraction.

During rigor mortis, actin and myosin bind tightly to form actomyosin.

This causes muscle stiffness.

Actomyosin formation causes rigor stiffness.

Stages of Rigor Mortis

Rigor mortis has three stages:

  • Pre-rigor
  • Rigor
  • Post-rigor
Meat changes through stages after slaughter.

Pre-Rigor Stage

Pre-rigor is the early stage after slaughter before full muscle stiffening.

Muscle is still soft and biochemical reactions are ongoing.

Pre-rigor = before stiffness.

Rigor Stage

Rigor stage is when muscles become stiff due to ATP depletion.

Rigor stage = muscle stiffness.

Post-Rigor Stage

Post-rigor is the stage after rigor mortis when meat gradually becomes tender.

This occurs due to natural enzyme activity.

Post-rigor = tenderization begins.

Meat Tenderization

Meat becomes tender after rigor due to enzyme breakdown of muscle proteins.

Important enzymes:

  • Calpains
  • Cathepsins
Tenderization improves eating quality.

Aging of Meat

Aging is the controlled storage of meat after slaughter to improve tenderness and flavor.

It allows natural enzymes to break down muscle proteins.

Aging is not rotting; it is controlled proteolysis.

Aging = controlled enzyme tenderization.

Cold Shortening

Cold shortening occurs when carcasses are chilled too quickly before rigor mortis is complete.

It causes strong muscle contraction and tough meat.

Cold shortening = tough meat from early chilling.

High Temperature Effect

If carcass temperature remains high while pH drops rapidly, meat quality may be affected.

This can contribute to poor color, poor texture, and poor water-holding capacity.

Temperature and pH must be controlled together.

PSE Meat

PSE meat means pale, soft, and exudative meat.

It occurs when pH drops rapidly while carcass temperature is still high.

Common in:

  • Pork

Characteristics:

  • Pale color
  • Soft texture
  • Watery surface
PSE = rapid pH drop + high temperature.

DFD Meat

DFD meat means dark, firm, and dry meat.

It occurs when muscle glycogen is low before slaughter, so little lactic acid is formed.

Common cause:

  • Long-term stress before slaughter

Characteristics:

  • Dark color
  • Firm texture
  • Dry surface
  • High final pH
DFD = low glycogen + high final pH.

Stress and Meat Quality

Animal stress before slaughter affects meat quality.

  • Short-term acute stress may cause rapid pH decline and PSE meat
  • Long-term chronic stress may reduce glycogen stores and cause DFD meat
Short stress = PSE. Long stress = DFD.

Water-Holding Capacity

Water-holding capacity is the ability of meat to retain water.

It affects:

  • Juiciness
  • Tenderness
  • Cooking loss
  • Processing yield
Better water-holding capacity = better meat quality.

Color Development

Meat color depends on:

  • Myoglobin
  • Oxygen exposure
  • pH
  • Freshness

Myoglobin is the main pigment responsible for meat color.

Hemoglobin is the pigment in blood.

Myoglobin is the main meat pigment.

Glycogen → Lactic Acid → pH Decline → Rigor Mortis → Aging / Tenderization

Controlled postmortem change improves meat quality.
