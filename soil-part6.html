💧 Part 6: Soil Water

Soil Water

  • Soil water is the water held in the pore spaces of the soil.
  • It is essential for plant growth and many biological and chemical processes in the soil.
  • It serves as a solvent and transport medium for nutrients.

Importance of Soil Water

  • Supplies water needed by plants for growth and development.
  • Helps dissolve nutrients so roots can absorb them.
  • Supports microbial activity and chemical reactions in the soil.
  • Regulates soil temperature.

Forms of Soil Water

Gravitational Water

  • Water that drains freely through the soil due to gravity.
  • Usually found in large pores after heavy rain or irrigation.
  • Not readily available to plants for long because it moves downward quickly.

Capillary Water

  • Water held in the small pores by capillary forces.
  • This is the main form of water available to plants.

Hygroscopic Water

  • Thin film of water tightly held around soil particles.
  • Not available to plants because it is held too strongly.

Field Capacity

  • Field capacity is the amount of water left in the soil after excess gravitational water has drained away.
  • It represents the upper limit of water available to plants.

Permanent Wilting Point

  • The soil moisture level at which plants can no longer obtain enough water and remain permanently wilted.
  • Below this point, water is unavailable for plant use.

Available Water

  • Available water is the water held between field capacity and permanent wilting point.
  • This is the portion of soil water that plants can use.
Available Water = Field Capacity − Permanent Wilting Point

Infiltration

  • Infiltration is the process by which water enters the soil surface.
  • It is affected by soil texture, structure, compaction, organic matter, and surface cover.

Percolation

  • Percolation is the downward movement of water through the soil profile.
  • It is important for groundwater recharge and drainage.

Capillary Rise

  • Capillary rise is the upward movement of water through small pores due to capillary action.
  • It can help bring water closer to plant roots from lower layers.

Factors Affecting Soil Water

  • Texture – clay soils hold more water than sandy soils.
  • Structure – good structure improves water movement and storage.
  • Organic Matter – increases water holding capacity.
  • Depth of Soil – deeper soils can store more water.
  • Compaction – reduces infiltration and water movement.

Soil Moisture Management

  • Proper irrigation helps maintain adequate soil moisture.
  • Mulching reduces evaporation losses.
  • Good drainage prevents waterlogging.
  • Organic matter improves moisture retention.

Waterlogging

  • Waterlogging happens when the soil becomes saturated for a long period.
  • It reduces soil aeration and limits root respiration.
  • Can lead to poor growth and root damage.
