⚡ Part 7: Soil Chemical Properties

1. Soil Chemical Properties

  • Soil chemical properties refer to the chemical composition and reactions that occur in the soil.
  • These properties influence nutrient availability, soil fertility, and plant growth.

2. Soil pH

Soil pH is a measure of the acidity or alkalinity of soil.

pH Scale

  • Less than 7 = Acidic
  • Equal to 7 = Neutral
  • Greater than 7 = Alkaline

Importance of Soil pH

  • Affects nutrient availability
  • Influences microbial activity
  • Determines crop suitability
Most crops grow best at pH 6 to 7.

3. Soil Acidity

Types of Soil Acidity

  • Active acidity – acidity in the soil solution
  • Exchangeable acidity – acidity held on soil particles
  • Residual acidity – acidity present in soil minerals and organic matter
Soil acidity is important in determining lime requirement.

4. Soil Alkalinity

  • Soil alkalinity occurs when soil has high pH.
  • It is common in dry regions.

Problems Caused by Soil Alkalinity

  • Nutrient deficiency
  • Poor plant growth

5. Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)

CEC is the ability of soil to hold and exchange positively charged ions called cations.

Common Cations

  • Calcium (Ca²⁺)
  • Magnesium (Mg²⁺)
  • Potassium (K⁺)
  • Sodium (Na⁺)
High CEC means better nutrient holding capacity.

6. Base Saturation

Base saturation is the percentage of the soil exchange sites occupied by basic cations.

  • High base saturation = more fertile soil
  • Low base saturation = more acidic soil

7. Soil Colloids

Soil colloids are very small soil particles that hold nutrients and water.

Types of Soil Colloids

  • Clay minerals
  • Organic colloids (humus)
Soil colloids are important for nutrient retention.

8. Soil Organic Matter (SOM)

Soil organic matter is made up of decomposed plant and animal residues.

Functions of Soil Organic Matter

  • Improves soil structure
  • Increases water holding capacity
  • Supplies nutrients
  • Enhances microbial activity

9. Nutrient Availability

Nutrient availability depends on several soil conditions.

  • Soil pH
  • Soil moisture
  • Microbial activity

10. Essential Plant Nutrients

Macronutrients

  • Nitrogen (N)
  • Phosphorus (P)
  • Potassium (K)

Secondary Nutrients

  • Calcium (Ca)
  • Magnesium (Mg)
  • Sulfur (S)

Micronutrients

  • Iron (Fe)
  • Zinc (Zn)
  • Copper (Cu)
  • Manganese (Mn)
  • Boron (B)
  • Molybdenum (Mo)

11. Nutrient Deficiency

Nutrient deficiency happens when essential nutrients are lacking in the soil.

Causes

  • Poor soil fertility
  • Imbalanced fertilization
  • Improper soil pH

Results

  • Stunted growth
  • Yellowing of leaves

12. Soil Reaction

Soil reaction refers to the overall chemical condition of the soil.

  • Includes soil acidity
  • Includes soil alkalinity

13. Soil Salinity

Soil salinity is the accumulation of soluble salts in the soil.

Effects of Soil Salinity

  • Reduces water uptake by plants
  • Causes plant stress

14. Soil Sodicity

Soil sodicity is the condition where soil has high sodium content.

Effects of Soil Sodicity

  • Poor soil structure
  • Reduced infiltration

15. Buffering Capacity

Buffering capacity is the ability of soil to resist changes in pH.

  • Usually high in clay soils
  • Usually high in organic soils

16. Soil Chemical Reactions

Important soil chemical reactions affect nutrient availability and soil condition.

  • Oxidation
  • Reduction
  • Hydrolysis

17. Fertilizer Application

Fertilizer application is the addition of nutrients to the soil.

Types of Fertilizers

  • Organic fertilizers
  • Inorganic fertilizers

18. Liming

Liming is the application of lime to reduce soil acidity.

Liming raises soil pH.

19. Soil Testing

Soil testing is done to determine soil pH and nutrient levels.

Uses of Soil Testing

  • Fertilizer recommendations
  • Soil management planning

Final Summary

pH controls nutrient availability.
CEC determines nutrient holding capacity.
Organic matter improves soil condition.
Salinity and sodicity are soil problems.
Liming reduces soil acidity.
