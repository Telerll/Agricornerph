🦟 Mouthparts of Insects

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

1. Definition of Mouthparts

Mouthparts are specialized external structures located on the head of insects that are used for feeding.

These mouthparts are modified according to the insect’s feeding habit and type of food.

  • Used for feeding and food manipulation
  • Modified depending on feeding habits
  • Important in insect identification
  • Closely related to plant damage and pest management

2. Basic Parts of Insect Mouthparts

1. Labrum

The labrum is the upper lip of the insect mouth. It is a flap-like movable structure located above the mouth opening.

Functions of the Labrum

  • Covers the mouth opening
  • Helps hold food during feeding
  • Guides food toward the mouth

2. Mandibles

Mandibles are paired jaw-like structures used mainly for chewing and cutting food materials.

  • Strongly sclerotized
  • Usually unsegmented
  • Used for biting, cutting, grinding, and crushing food

Mandibles are well developed in chewing insects such as grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars, and termites.

3. Maxillae

Maxillae are paired segmented appendages located behind the mandibles.

Functions of Maxillae

  • Hold and manipulate food
  • Push food into the mouth
  • Assist in mastication
  • Contain sensory palps for tasting

4. Labium

The labium serves as the lower lip of insects. It is formed from fused appendages.

Functions of the Labium

  • Supports food during feeding
  • Helps manipulate food
  • Contains sensory structures

5. Hypopharynx

The hypopharynx is a tongue-like structure found inside the insect mouth.

  • Bears the opening of salivary ducts
  • Mixes saliva with food
  • Assists swallowing

3. Generalized Chewing Mouthparts

The chewing type is considered the primitive or generalized arrangement of insect mouthparts.

Characteristics

  • Strong mandibles present
  • Adapted for solid food feeding
  • Capable of biting and grinding food

Examples

  • Grasshoppers
  • Cockroaches
  • Beetles
  • Termites
  • Caterpillars

Chewing insects are important agricultural pests because they directly consume plant tissues.

4. Types of Insect Mouthparts

Through evolution, insect mouthparts became highly modified according to different feeding habits.

5. Chewing Type

Chewing mouthparts are adapted for biting, grinding, and chewing solid food materials.

Characteristics

  • Strong mandibles
  • Well-developed maxillae
  • Adapted for solid food feeding

Examples

  • Grasshoppers
  • Beetles
  • Termites
  • Caterpillars

Damage Caused

  • Defoliation
  • Leaf holes
  • Stem boring
  • Root feeding

6. Piercing-Sucking Type

Piercing-sucking mouthparts are adapted for piercing tissues and sucking liquid food such as plant sap or blood.

Characteristics

  • Needle-like stylets present
  • Labium forms a beak or rostrum
  • Mandibles and maxillae modified into stylets

Examples

  • Aphids
  • Leafhoppers
  • True bugs
  • Mosquitoes

Damage Caused

  • Sap loss
  • Wilting
  • Leaf curling
  • Yellowing
  • Disease transmission

Many viral plant diseases are transmitted by piercing-sucking insects.

7. Siphoning Type

Siphoning mouthparts are adapted for sucking nectar and other liquid food.

Characteristics

  • Long coiled proboscis
  • Mandibles absent
  • Adapted for liquid feeding

Examples

  • Butterflies
  • Moths

8. Sponging Type

Sponging mouthparts are adapted for absorbing liquid or semi-liquid food.

Characteristics

  • Soft sponge-like labium
  • Food is dissolved before ingestion

Examples

  • Housefly

9. Chewing-Lapping Type

This mouthpart combines chewing and lapping functions.

Characteristics

  • Mandibles used for chewing
  • Tongue-like structure used for lapping nectar

Examples

  • Honeybee

10. Rasping-Sucking Type

Rasping-sucking mouthparts scrape plant surfaces and suck plant sap.

Characteristics

  • Asymmetrical mouthparts
  • Scrapes tissues before sucking sap

Examples

  • Thrips

Damage Caused

  • Silvery leaves
  • Curling
  • Distortion of flowers and leaves

11. Economic Importance of Mouthparts

Studying insect mouthparts helps determine feeding habits, type of crop injury, and proper pest management strategies.

  • Helps identify insects
  • Predicts type of plant damage
  • Helps select proper insecticides
  • Important in pest management

12. Summary

Insect mouthparts are highly specialized feeding structures adapted to different food sources and feeding behaviors.

Major mouthpart types include chewing, piercing-sucking, siphoning, sponging, chewing-lapping, and rasping-sucking. Their structure determines how insects feed and the type of damage they cause.
