Non-Parasitic or Abiotic Diseases

Non-parasitic diseases are plant disorders caused by non-living environmental factors rather than pathogens.

  • Also called abiotic diseases or physiological disorders.
  • They are not contagious.
  • They do not spread from plant to plant.
  • No fungus, bacterium, virus, or nematode is involved.

Characteristics of Abiotic Diseases

Non-Contagious

The disorder does not spread from one plant to another because no living pathogen is involved.

Uniform Appearance

Symptoms often appear uniformly over a field or area because the cause is usually environmental.

Sudden Occurrence

Damage may appear suddenly after stress such as frost, heat, drought, flooding, or chemical injury.

No Pathogen Present

No infectious organism can be isolated as the cause of the problem.

1. Injury Caused by Low Temperature

Low temperature damages plant tissues and interferes with normal plant metabolism.

Freezing Injury

Freezing injury occurs when temperature falls below 0°C.

  • Ice crystals form inside or between plant cells.
  • Water expands during freezing.
  • Cells rupture and die.
  • Symptoms include water-soaked tissues, blackening, wilting, and tissue collapse.

Chilling Injury

Chilling injury occurs at temperatures above freezing but still too low for tropical plants, usually below 12.5°C.

  • Common in bananas, tomatoes, mangoes, and cucumbers.
  • Produces pitting and water-soaked symptoms.
  • May cause poor ripening and internal browning.
  • Reduces storage quality and marketability.

2. Injury Caused by High Temperature

Excessive heat damages tissues, disrupts physiological processes, and may reduce crop quality.

Sunscald

Sunscald is caused by prolonged exposure to strong sunlight and high temperature.

  • Produces bleached or blistered tissues.
  • Common on fruits and leaves exposed directly to sunlight.
  • May reduce market value of fruits and vegetables.

Heat Necrosis of Potato

Heat necrosis occurs when potatoes are grown in hot, dry, light soils. It results in yellow or brown discoloration in the vascular system.

3. Stress Caused by Lack of Oxygen

Plant roots need oxygen for respiration. When oxygen becomes too low, normal respiration cannot proceed properly.

  • Oxygen below 2% may cause anaerobic conditions.
  • Common in waterlogged soils and poorly drained fields.
  • Symptoms include wilting, yellowing, root death, and stunted growth.

Blackheart Disease of Potato

Blackheart disease of potato is caused by oxygen deficiency inside potato tubers, resulting in blackened internal tissues.

4. Too Much or Too Little Light

Excessive Light

Too much light may scorch or scald leaf tissues, especially when combined with high temperature.

Insufficient Light

Too little light reduces chlorophyll formation and produces weak, elongated, pale plants.

Lack of light may cause etiolation, where plants become pale, weak, and abnormally elongated.

5. Injury Caused by Adverse Meteorological Conditions

Extreme weather conditions may physically injure plants and make them more vulnerable to disease.

  • Strong winds may break stems and tear leaves.
  • Heavy rains may cause flooding, lodging, and soil erosion.
  • Lightning may damage plant tissues.
  • Storms may predispose plants to secondary infections.

6. Injury Caused by Air Pollutants

Air pollutants damage plant tissues, interfere with photosynthesis, and reduce plant growth and yield.

Ethylene

Ethylene is an essential plant hormone but may also come from vehicle emissions and industrial activities. Excess ethylene may cause premature ripening, leaf drop, and flower drop.

Nitrogen Oxides

Nitrogen oxides are produced from automobile emissions and fuel combustion. They may cause leaf injury and reduced photosynthesis.

Peroxyacetyl Nitrates (PANs)

PANs are formed when nitrogen oxides undergo photochemical reactions with gaseous hydrocarbons. They may cause bronzing, glazing, and tissue injury on leaves.

Ozone

Ozone is a component of smog. It may cause chlorosis, necrosis, and reduced crop yield.

Particulates

Particulates include dusts, ashes, soot, lime, and cement particles. They may cover leaf surfaces, block sunlight, and reduce photosynthesis.

  • Chlorine (Cl₂)
  • Sulfur dioxide (SO₂)
  • Fluorides
  • Hydrogen chloride

7. Mineral Deficiencies and Mineral Excesses

Plants need balanced nutrients for normal growth. Deficiency or excess of minerals may cause abnormal growth and physiological disorders.

Nitrogen Deficiency

Causes yellowing of older leaves and poor vegetative growth.

Potassium Deficiency

Causes leaf scorching, weak stems, and poor resistance to stress.

Iron Deficiency

Causes interveinal chlorosis, especially in young leaves.

Nutrient Toxicity

Excess minerals may poison plants and cause leaf burn, poor root growth, or reduced yield.

8. Unfavorable Soil pH

Soil pH affects nutrient availability and root growth.

  • Very acidic soils may cause aluminum toxicity and nutrient deficiency.
  • Very alkaline soils may cause iron and zinc deficiency.

9. Excessive Pesticide Levels

Excessive or improper pesticide use may injure crops.

  • Overapplication may cause leaf burn.
  • Wrong concentration may suppress plant growth.
  • Wrong timing may damage sensitive plant stages.
  • Improper mixing may cause phytotoxicity.

10. Improper Agricultural Practices

Poor farming practices can stress plants and reduce productivity.

  • Improper irrigation
  • Overfertilization
  • Deep planting
  • Mechanical injury
  • Poor spacing

11. Lack or Excess of Soil Moisture

Drought Stress

Drought stress is caused by insufficient water. It may result in wilting, dry leaves, reduced yield, and plant death.

Waterlogging

Waterlogging is caused by excessive soil moisture. It reduces oxygen in the soil, damages roots, and may cause yellowing or root death.

12. Naturally Occurring Toxic Chemicals

Some soils naturally contain substances that are toxic to plants.

  • Excess salts
  • Heavy metals
  • Toxic minerals

General Management of Abiotic Diseases

  • Use proper irrigation to avoid drought and waterlogging.
  • Maintain balanced fertilization.
  • Improve drainage and soil aeration.
  • Use pesticides only at recommended rates.
  • Correct soil pH and mineral toxicities.
  • Use shade, mulch, or windbreaks when needed.
