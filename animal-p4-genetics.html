Introduction to Animal Genetics

Animal Genetics

Animal genetics is the branch of animal science that studies heredity and variation in animals.

It explains:

  • How traits are passed from parents to offspring
  • Why animals resemble their parents
  • Why animals differ from one another
Genetics explains inheritance and variation.

Importance of Animal Genetics

Animal genetics is important because it helps improve:

  • Growth rate
  • Milk production
  • Egg production
  • Fertility
  • Disease resistance
  • Feed efficiency
  • Carcass quality
Genetics improves animal productivity and profitability.

Heredity

Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring.

Examples of inherited traits:

  • Coat color
  • Body size
  • Horned or polled condition
  • Milk yield potential
  • Growth potential
Heredity explains why offspring resemble parents.

Variation

Variation is the difference in traits among animals of the same species.

Examples:

  • Some animals grow faster
  • Some animals produce more milk
  • Some animals resist disease better
Variation is the basis of selection.

Genetics and Breeding

Genetics provides the scientific basis of animal breeding.

Breeding uses genetics to:

  • Select superior animals
  • Improve future generations
  • Increase production efficiency
Breeding is applied genetics.

Nature and Nurture

Animal performance is influenced by:

  • Genetics or nature
  • Environment or nurture

Genetics determines potential, but environment affects expression.

Phenotype = Genotype + Environment (P = G + E)

Genes set the limit, environment determines performance.

Genotype

Genotype is the genetic makeup of an animal.

It refers to the genes the animal carries.

Genotype = genetic potential.

Phenotype

Phenotype is the observable appearance or performance of an animal.

It includes:

  • Body size
  • Coat color
  • Milk yield
  • Growth rate
Phenotype = observed trait.

Genotype vs Phenotype

Genotype is what the animal is genetically.

Phenotype is what the animal shows physically or in performance.

Example:

  • Genotype = genetic potential for high milk yield
  • Phenotype = actual milk produced
Genotype is inherited, phenotype is expressed.

Environment

Environment affects how genes are expressed.

Examples:

  • Nutrition
  • Housing
  • Management
  • Health
  • Climate
Poor environment can prevent good genes from being expressed.

Trait

Trait is any measurable or observable characteristic of an animal.

Examples:

  • Body weight
  • Fertility
  • Coat color
  • Milk yield
Traits are the units evaluated in breeding.

Qualitative Traits

Qualitative traits are traits controlled by one or a few genes and are usually expressed in distinct categories.

Examples:

  • Coat color
  • Horned vs polled
  • Blood type
Qualitative traits are simple and discrete.

Quantitative Traits

Quantitative traits are traits controlled by many genes and influenced by environment.

Examples:

  • Growth rate
  • Milk yield
  • Egg production
  • Body weight
Quantitative traits are complex, measurable, and economically important.

Importance of Quantitative Traits

Most economically important traits are quantitative.

These include:

  • Growth
  • Fertility
  • Milk production
  • Feed efficiency
Quantitative traits are the main focus in animal breeding.

Heritability

Heritability (h²) is the proportion of total variation in a trait that is due to genetics.

It tells how much of a trait can be passed from parent to offspring.

  • High heritability = faster genetic progress
  • Low heritability = slower genetic progress

Examples:

  • Carcass traits = usually high
  • Fertility traits = usually low
Heritability determines response to selection.

Genetics vs Genomics

Genetics studies individual genes and heredity.

Genomics studies the entire genome of the animal.

Genetics studies specific genes, while genomics studies all genes together.

Genetic Improvement

Genetic improvement is the process of increasing the frequency of desirable genes in a population.

This is done through:

  • Selection
  • Mating
  • Breeding systems
Genetic improvement is the goal of breeding.

Genetic Progress

Genetic progress is the improvement achieved in animal performance through breeding over generations.

It is usually:

  • Gradual
  • Cumulative
  • Permanent
  • Transmittable
Genetic progress is slow but lasting.

Genetics in Animal Production

Genetics affects:

  • Efficiency
  • Production
  • Reproduction
  • Health
  • Profitability
Better genetics = better animals.

Board Exam Master Concept

Animal genetics explains:

  • Why animals resemble parents
  • Why animals differ
  • How breeders improve livestock
Genetics is the foundation of animal breeding.
