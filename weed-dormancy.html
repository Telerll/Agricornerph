Introduction to Seed Dormancy

Seed dormancy is one of the most important survival mechanisms of weeds. It allows weed seeds to remain alive for extended periods and germinate only when environmental conditions become favorable.

Dormancy explains why weeds continue to appear even after repeated weed control measures and why weed infestations can persist for many years.

Definition:
Seed dormancy is a condition in which a viable seed fails to germinate despite the presence of favorable environmental conditions.
Board Tip:
Dormant seeds are alive. Dormancy does not mean seed death.

Importance of Dormancy in Weeds

Dormancy provides several advantages to weeds and contributes greatly to their persistence.

  • Allows survival during drought
  • Allows survival during flooding
  • Allows survival during extreme temperatures
  • Helps weeds escape control measures
  • Maintains soil seed banks
  • Ensures long-term survival of weed populations
Dormancy is one of the major reasons weeds continue to appear year after year despite repeated control efforts.

I. Causes of Seed Dormancy

Dormancy may result from internal or external factors affecting the seed.

A. Seed Coat Dormancy (Physical Dormancy)

Physical dormancy is caused by a hard or impermeable seed coat.

The seed coat prevents water absorption and oxygen entry, thereby preventing germination.

Examples

  • Many leguminous weeds
  • Hard-coated weed seeds
Board Tip:
Hard Seed Coat = Physical Dormancy

B. Embryo Dormancy (Physiological Dormancy)

Embryo dormancy occurs when the embryo is immature or physiologically inactive.

Even if environmental conditions are favorable, germination does not occur until physiological changes take place.

Importance

Allows seeds to delay germination until favorable conditions become available.

C. Chemical Dormancy

Chemical dormancy occurs because of germination inhibitors present in the seed or surrounding tissues.

Examples of Chemical Inhibitors

  • Abscisic Acid (ABA)
  • Phenolic Compounds

These substances suppress germination until conditions become favorable.

Board Tip:
ABA (Abscisic Acid) is a major germination inhibitor.

D. Environmental Dormancy

Environmental dormancy occurs when external environmental conditions are unfavorable.

Examples

  • Lack of moisture
  • Low oxygen supply
  • Unsuitable temperatures
  • Absence of light
Board Tip:
Environmental Dormancy is often referred to as Enforced Dormancy.

II. Types of Dormancy

A. Innate Dormancy

Innate dormancy is present when seeds are shed from the parent plant.

This type of dormancy is genetically controlled.

Example

Freshly harvested seeds that fail to germinate immediately.

B. Induced Dormancy

Induced dormancy develops after seed dispersal due to environmental conditions.

This helps seeds survive unfavorable environments.

C. Enforced Dormancy

Enforced dormancy occurs because environmental conditions are unsuitable for germination.

Examples include lack of water, oxygen deficiency, or unfavorable temperature.

Board Tip:
When favorable conditions return, enforced dormancy may be removed.

III. Dormancy and the Soil Seed Bank

Definition of Seed Bank

The seed bank is the total reserve of viable and dormant weed seeds present in the soil.

Relationship Between Dormancy and Seed Bank

Dormancy allows weed seeds to remain viable in the soil for many years.

Because of dormancy, seeds can accumulate in the soil and germinate over time.

Important Concept:
Dormancy + Seed Bank = Long-Term Weed Survival

Importance

This relationship explains why weed infestations continue despite repeated weed control efforts.

IV. Advantages of Dormancy to Weeds

1. Survival During Unfavorable Conditions

Dormant seeds remain viable during periods unsuitable for growth.

2. Prevention of Simultaneous Germination

Not all seeds germinate at the same time.

This ensures that some seeds survive even if emerging seedlings are destroyed.

3. Escape from Control Measures

Dormant seeds may survive cultivation, herbicide application, and other weed management practices.

4. Maintenance of Weed Populations

Dormancy provides continuous replenishment of weed populations.

V. Breaking Seed Dormancy

Certain environmental or artificial treatments can terminate dormancy and stimulate germination.

A. Scarification

Scarification is the mechanical or chemical weakening of the seed coat.

Importance

Allows water and oxygen to enter the seed.

Methods

  • Abrasion
  • Soil cultivation
  • Acid treatment
  • Mechanical scratching
Board Tip:
Scarification is used to overcome hard seed coats.

B. Stratification

Stratification is the exposure of seeds to specific temperature conditions for a period of time.

Types

  • Cold Stratification
  • Warm Stratification

Importance

Promotes embryo development and overcomes physiological dormancy.

Board Tip:
Stratification is commonly associated with physiological dormancy.

C. Weathering

Weathering is the natural breakdown of seed coats through environmental exposure.

Examples

  • Rainfall
  • Temperature fluctuations
  • Microbial activity

D. Passage Through Animal Digestive Tract

Digestive processes may weaken the seed coat and stimulate germination.

VI. Dormancy vs Quiescence

This is one of the most common ALE board examination traps.

Dormancy Quiescence
Seed fails to germinate despite favorable conditions Seed fails to germinate because conditions are unfavorable
Internal cause External cause
Requires dormancy-breaking mechanisms Requires favorable environmental conditions
Seed is dormant Seed is ready but waiting
Board Tip:
Dormancy = Internal Problem
Quiescence = External Problem

VII. Agricultural Importance

Understanding dormancy helps determine:

  • Timing of cultivation
  • Timing of herbicide application
  • Weed emergence patterns
  • Seed bank management
  • Long-term weed control strategies

VIII. Dormancy and Weed Management

Effective weed management must consider dormancy because not all seeds germinate at once.

Some seeds remain hidden in the soil and may germinate years later.

Repeated control measures are often necessary because dormant seeds continue to emerge over time.

Dormancy is one of the major reasons why long-term weed management programs are necessary.

Summary

Seed dormancy is a condition in which a viable seed fails to germinate despite favorable environmental conditions. It is one of the most important survival mechanisms of weeds because it allows seeds to remain viable during unfavorable periods and contributes to the persistence of the soil seed bank. Dormancy may result from hard seed coats, physiological factors, chemical inhibitors, or environmental influences. Processes such as scarification and stratification help break dormancy. Understanding seed dormancy is essential for predicting weed emergence, managing weed seed banks, and designing effective long-term weed control programs.
