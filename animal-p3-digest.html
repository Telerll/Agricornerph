Digestion and Absorption of Nutrients

Digestion

Digestion is the process of breaking down feed into simpler substances that can be absorbed and used by the body.

The goal of digestion is to convert feed into usable nutrients.

Digestion makes nutrients available for absorption.

Absorption

Absorption is the process by which digested nutrients pass through the intestinal wall into the blood or lymph.

Digestion breaks nutrients down.
Absorption moves nutrients into circulation.

Importance of Digestion and Absorption

Digestion and absorption are essential because they allow animals to:

  • Obtain nutrients
  • Produce energy
  • Grow
  • Repair tissues
  • Reproduce
  • Maintain body functions
No digestion = no nutrient use
No absorption = no benefit from feed

Steps in Digestion

The process of digestion includes:

  1. Ingestion
  2. Mechanical digestion
  3. Chemical digestion
  4. Microbial digestion
  5. Absorption
  6. Assimilation

Ingestion

Ingestion is the taking in of feed and water.

This is the first step in digestion.

Mechanical Digestion

Mechanical digestion is the physical breakdown of feed into smaller particles.

Examples:

  • Chewing in mammals
  • Grinding in the gizzard of poultry
Mechanical digestion increases surface area for enzyme action.

Chemical Digestion

Chemical digestion is the breakdown of feed by enzymes and digestive juices.

Examples:

  • Saliva
  • Stomach acid
  • Pancreatic enzymes
  • Intestinal enzymes
Chemical digestion converts complex nutrients into absorbable forms.

Important Digestive Enzymes

  • Amylase – breaks down starch
  • Pepsin – breaks down protein
  • Lipase – breaks down fats
Enzymes are the chemical tools of digestion.

Microbial Digestion

Microbial digestion is the breakdown of feed by microorganisms.

Important in:

  • Ruminants — rumen
  • Horses — cecum
  • Rabbits — cecum

Microbes digest:

  • Fiber
  • Cellulose
  • Some starches

Microbes produce:

  • Volatile fatty acids (VFAs)
  • Microbial protein
  • B vitamins
Microbial digestion allows animals to use fibrous feeds.

Absorption Sites

Most absorption occurs in the small intestine, especially:

  • Duodenum
  • Jejunum
  • Ileum

Main products absorbed:

  • Glucose
  • Amino acids
  • Fatty acids
  • Vitamins
  • Minerals
  • Water

Villi and Microvilli

The small intestine is lined with:

  • Villi – finger-like projections
  • Microvilli – microscopic projections on villi
Villi and microvilli increase surface area and improve absorption.

Assimilation

Assimilation is the use of absorbed nutrients by body cells.

Absorbed nutrients are used for:

  • Energy
  • Growth
  • Repair
  • Reproduction
  • Production
Absorption brings nutrients in.
Assimilation puts nutrients to work.

Monogastric Digestion

Monogastric animals have one simple stomach.

Examples:

  • Swine
  • Poultry
  • Dogs

Characteristics:

  • Rely mainly on enzymatic digestion
  • Have limited fiber digestion
  • Efficiently digest starch and concentrates
Monogastrics digest concentrates well.

Ruminant Digestion

Ruminants have a four-compartment stomach.

Compartments:

  • Rumen
  • Reticulum
  • Omasum
  • Abomasum

Examples:

  • Cattle
  • Sheep
  • Goats

Characteristics:

  • Extensive microbial digestion
  • Efficient fiber utilization
  • Can use roughages effectively

Abomasum = True Stomach

The abomasum is the true stomach because it is the only compartment that produces acid and digestive enzymes.

Abomasum = monogastric equivalent in ruminants.

Hindgut Fermentation

Some animals are hindgut fermenters.

Examples:

  • Horses
  • Rabbits

Fermentation occurs mainly in the:

  • Cecum
  • Large intestine
Hindgut fermenters digest fiber after the small intestine.

Coprophagy in Rabbits

Coprophagy is the re-eating of soft feces or cecotropes in rabbits.

Purpose:

  • Recover microbial protein
  • Recover B vitamins
  • Improve nutrient use
Rabbits re-digest nutrients produced in the cecum.

Nutrient Absorption Pathways

After absorption:

  • Sugars and amino acids → blood
  • Fatty acids → lymph
  • Water → blood
  • Minerals → blood
Absorbed nutrients are transported to body tissues.

Factors Affecting Digestion and Absorption

Efficiency depends on:

  • Feed quality
  • Digestibility
  • Species
  • Age
  • Health
  • Gut condition
  • Microbial population
Better digestion = better nutrient use.

Board Exam Master Concept

Feeding is not enough.

The animal must:

  • Digest feed
  • Absorb nutrients
  • Use nutrients
Feed value depends on digestibility and absorption.
