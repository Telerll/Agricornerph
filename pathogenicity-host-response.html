VIII. Mechanisms of Pathogenicity and Host Response

Definition

Plant pathogens cause disease by interfering with the normal physiology of the affected plant. This results in abnormal growth, reduced productivity, tissue damage, and sometimes death of the plant.

A. Malfunctioning of the Normal Physiology

1. Interference with the Uptake of Water and Inorganic Elements from the Soil

  • Root-rotting microorganisms destroy root tissues.
  • Colonization of xylem vessels blocks water movement.
  • Gall-forming nematodes crush or deform xylem tissues.
  • Formation of tyloses may block water-conducting vessels.
  • Fungi and bacteria in xylem interfere with water translocation.

2. Interference with Translocation of Organic Compounds

  • Pathogens may damage phloem tissues.
  • This disrupts movement of food materials inside the plant.
  • Common effects include yellowing, poor grain filling, and small tubers.

3. Reduction of the Plant’s Photosynthetic Capacity

Diseases reduce the effective leaf surface available for photosynthesis.

  • Leaf spots
  • Blight
  • Mosaic
  • Mottle
  • Canker
  • Scab
  • Dieback
  • Wilt

4. Increased Transpiration

  • Powdery mildew, downy mildew, and rust pathogens damage the cuticle and epidermis.
  • This increases leaf permeability.
  • Excessive water loss may lead to wilting.

5. Changes in Growth of the Suscept

Pathogens may disturb growth hormones and feedback mechanisms, causing abnormal plant growth.

6. Changes in the Reproduction of the Host Plant

Some pathogens affect reproductive organs.

  • Phyllody – flowers are transformed into green leafy structures.

7. Death of Cells and Tissues

Necrotic diseases caused by fungi, bacteria, viruses, and other pathogens result in disintegration of cell walls and protoplasm.

B. Hormones Associated with Plant Diseases

1. Auxin (IAA) and Cytokinins

  • Required for cell division and differentiation.
  • May increase when plants are infected.
  • Can contribute to abnormal growth and gall formation.

2. Gibberellins

  • Stimulate stem elongation.
  • May cause abnormal elongation when affected by pathogens.

3. Ethylene

  • Natural aging and ripening hormone.
  • Excess ethylene may cause epinasty, premature senescence, and shedding of leaves.

C. Enzymes Associated in Plant Diseases

Enzymes are protein molecules that catalyze reactions inside cells. Pathogens produce enzymes that degrade plant cell walls and tissues.

Pectic Enzymes

  • Assist in penetration of the host.
  • Break down cellulose and hemicellulose portions of the cell wall.
  • Cause weakening and maceration of plant tissues.
  • Facilitate intercellular and intracellular invasion.
  • Provide nutrients to pathogens in infected tissues.
  • May cause vascular plugging and occlusion in wilt diseases.

D. Toxins in Plant Diseases

Toxins are chemicals produced by pathogens that act directly on living host protoplasts, seriously damaging or killing cells and causing necrosis.

Characteristics of Toxins

  • Extremely toxic even in low concentrations.
  • Affect membrane permeability.
  • Inactivate or inhibit enzymes.
  • May induce deficiency of essential growth factors.

Host-Specific Toxins

Host-specific toxins are toxic only to hosts of the pathogen producing the toxin. They play an important role in disease establishment.

Examples

  • HS toxin – causes eye spot of sugarcane.
  • HV toxin / Victorin – causes foot or root rot and leaf blight of oats.
  • T toxin – causes southern corn leaf blight.
  • HC toxin – causes leaf spot of corn.
  • PC toxin – causes milo disease of sorghum.

E. Non-Host Specific Fungal Toxins

Non-host specific fungal toxins produce all or part of the disease syndrome on both host and non-host plants. They are not selective.

Examples

  • Fusaric acid
  • Phytonivein
  • Phytolycopersin
  • Lycomarasmin
  • Ophiobolin
  • Helminthosporal
  • Fusicoccin
  • Pyricularin
