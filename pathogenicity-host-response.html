Mechanisms of Pathogenicity and Host Response

Mechanisms of pathogenicity refer to the different ways plant pathogens cause disease and damage plant tissues.

Host response refers to the reactions and defense mechanisms of the plant against pathogen attack.

Plant pathogens interfere with normal physiological processes, causing abnormal growth, tissue destruction, reduced yield, and sometimes plant death.

A. Malfunctioning of the Normal Physiology

Pathogens cause disease by disrupting normal physiological activities of plants.

1. Interference with the Uptake of Water and Inorganic Elements from the Soil

Pathogens interfere with absorption and movement of water and mineral nutrients from the soil to the plant.

When roots or xylem vessels are damaged, plants cannot absorb enough water and nutrients, leading to wilting and deficiency symptoms.

Root-Rotting Microorganisms

These organisms destroy root tissues and reduce water absorption.

  • Pythium
  • Phytophthora
  • Fusarium

Colonization of Xylem Vessels

Some fungi and bacteria invade xylem vessels and block water movement.

  • Wilting
  • Water stress
  • Plant death

Gall-Forming Nematodes

Nematodes such as Meloidogyne crush or deform xylem tissues.

  • Poor water movement
  • Root swelling
  • Stunted growth

Formation of Tyloses

Tyloses are enlarged xylem parenchyma cells that block vessels.

  • Reduced water conduction
  • Wilting symptoms

EPS and Vessel Blockage

Extracellular polysaccharides or EPS produced by bacteria may clog vessels.

  • Vascular blockage
  • Wilting
  • Reduced nutrient transport

2. Interference with Translocation of Organic Compounds

Pathogens may interfere with movement of sugars and food materials through the phloem.

This disrupts food distribution inside the plant.

  • Yellowing
  • Reduced growth
  • Poor grain filling
  • Small tubers or fruits
  • Reduced yield

Example

Viruses may cause phloem necrosis, preventing normal food transport.

3. Reduction of the Plant’s Photosynthetic Capacity

Plant diseases reduce the plant’s ability to perform photosynthesis. When leaves are damaged, chlorophyll decreases and less food is produced.

Common Causes

  • Leaf spots
  • Blights
  • Mosaic
  • Mottle
  • Canker
  • Scab
  • Dieback
  • Wilt

Effects

  • Reduced food production
  • Poor growth
  • Reduced yield
  • Weak plants

4. Increased Transpiration

Some pathogens destroy the protective epidermis and cuticle, increasing water loss through transpiration.

Examples

  • Powdery mildews
  • Downy mildews
  • Rust fungi

Effects

  • Excessive water loss
  • Wilting
  • Drying of leaves
  • Reduced plant vigor

5. Changes in Growth of the Suscept

Pathogens alter normal growth regulation of plants.

Plant growth is controlled by hormones and metabolic processes. Pathogens disturb these processes and cause abnormal growth.

  • Stunting
  • Excessive growth
  • Gall formation
  • Abnormal branching
  • Dwarfing

6. Changes in the Reproduction of the Host Plant

Some pathogens alter flower and reproductive organ development.

Example: Phyllody

Phyllody is the transformation of floral parts into green leafy structures.

It is usually caused by mycoplasma-like organisms or phytoplasmas.

Effects

  • Sterility
  • Reduced seed formation
  • Poor fruit production

7. Death of Cells and Tissues

Many pathogens produce toxins, enzymes, and other harmful substances that kill plant cells.

  • Necrosis
  • Tissue disintegration
  • Rotting
  • Cankers
  • Leaf blights

B. Hormones Associated with Plant Diseases

Plant pathogens may alter plant hormone balance, resulting in abnormal growth and disease symptoms.

1. Auxin or IAA and Cytokinins

Functions

  • Cell division
  • Cell elongation
  • Differentiation

Disease Effects

Pathogen infection may increase auxin levels, causing gall formation, tumors, and abnormal growth.

2. Gibberellins

Functions

  • Stem elongation
  • Cell division

Disease Effects

Some pathogens produce gibberellins causing excessive elongation, weak stems, and abnormal growth.

Example: Bakanae disease of rice.

3. Ethylene

Functions

  • Fruit ripening
  • Aging processes

Excess Ethylene Causes

  • Epinasty
  • Premature senescence
  • Leaf shedding
  • Abnormal ripening

C. Enzymes Associated in Plant Diseases

Definition

Enzymes are protein molecules that catalyze chemical reactions. Pathogens produce enzymes that destroy plant tissues.

Functions of Pathogenic Enzymes

  • Break down cell walls
  • Assist penetration
  • Destroy tissues
  • Release nutrients for pathogens

Pectic Enzymes

Functions

  • Degrade pectin in cell walls
  • Cause tissue softening
  • Cause maceration
  • Facilitate pathogen invasion

Effects

  • Soft rot
  • Tissue collapse
  • Wilting

D. Toxins in Plant Diseases

Definition

Toxins are poisonous chemicals produced by pathogens that damage or kill plant cells.

Characteristics of Toxins

  • Extremely toxic even in low concentration
  • Affect cell membranes
  • Inactivate enzymes
  • Cause nutrient deficiency

Host-Specific Toxins

Host-specific toxins affect only specific host plants.

Characteristics

  • Determine pathogen virulence
  • Important in disease establishment

Examples

  • HS toxin – causes eye spot of sugarcane
  • HV toxin or Victorin – causes oat blight
  • T toxin – causes southern corn leaf blight

Non-Host Specific Fungal Toxins

These toxins affect both host and non-host plants.

Characteristics

  • Non-selective
  • Produce general disease symptoms

Examples

  • Fusaric acid
  • Phytolycopersin
  • Lycomarasmin
  • Helminthosporal
  • Fusicoccin
  • Pyricularin

Mechanisms of Disease Resistance

Definition

Disease resistance is the ability of plants to inhibit or suppress pathogen growth and development.

  • Pre-formed resistance
  • Induced resistance

A. Pre-formed Resistance

Pre-formed resistance refers to defense mechanisms already present before pathogen attack.

1. External Physical Barriers

Trichomes

Hair-like structures that hinder pathogen entry.

Cuticle

A waxy layer acting as both a physical and chemical barrier.

Stomata

Natural openings that regulate gas exchange and may influence pathogen entry.

Root Cap and Mucilage

Structures that protect root tips from infection.

Thick Seed Coat and Epidermis

These prevent penetration by many pathogens.

Waxy Layers

Waxy surfaces reduce water retention and limit infection droplet formation.

2. Internal Physical Barriers

  • Suberized tissues
  • Lignified tissues
  • Cellulosic walls
  • Middle lamella
  • Gum, resin, and tannin deposition
  • Silica deposition

These structures strengthen tissues against pathogen invasion.

3. Defense Through Lack of Essential Factors

Some plants resist pathogens because they lack essential factors needed by the pathogen.

  • Lack of recognition sites
  • Lack of receptors
  • Lack of essential nutrients for pathogens

4. Pre-formed Chemical Factors

Phenolics

Toxic compounds that inhibit pathogens.

Tannins

Compounds that suppress pathogen enzymes.

Saponins

Compounds that alter fungal cell membrane permeability.

Examples: Avenacin and Tomatine.

B. Induced Resistance

Induced resistance refers to defense mechanisms activated only after pathogen attack.

1. Active Defense Mechanisms

Plants respond actively to pathogen invasion through biochemical and structural changes.

2. Mechanical Barriers to Pathogenesis

Plants may form corky layers around infected tissues to isolate pathogens from healthy tissues.

3. Hypersensitivity

Hypersensitivity is the rapid localized death of host cells around infection sites.

Importance

  • Restricts pathogen spread
  • Traps pathogens inside dead tissue
  • Prevents further disease development

Phytoalexins

During hypersensitive reactions, plants may produce antimicrobial compounds called phytoalexins.

4. Plant Immunization

Plants do not naturally produce antibodies like animals. However, genetic engineering can introduce resistance genes.

Plantibodies

Transgenic plants may produce antibodies called plantibodies against pathogens.

Example: Resistance against viral coat proteins.

5. Other Post-Infectional Toxic Substances

Plants accumulate toxic compounds around infection sites.

Examples

  • Phenolic glucosides
  • Polyphenols
  • Flavonoids
  • Anthocyanins
  • Aromatic amino acids
  • Coumarin derivatives

Functions

  • Inhibit pathogen growth
  • Increase toxicity around infected tissues
  • Strengthen resistance mechanisms
