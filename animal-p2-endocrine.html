🧬 Endocrine System of Farm Animals

Endocrine System

Endocrine system regulates body functions through hormones released into the bloodstream.

Its effects are usually slower than the nervous system, but they are longer-lasting.

Hormones

Hormones are chemical messengers produced by glands and transported through the blood to target cells or organs.

Hormones = chemical messengers that control body processes.

Hormone Production Flow

Stimulus → Hypothalamus → Pituitary → Endocrine Gland → Hormone → Target Organ → Response

The control of many hormone activities begins in the hypothalamus.

Hypothalamus

Hypothalamus is the part of the brain that controls the pituitary gland.

GnRH

Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone (GnRH) is released by the hypothalamus and stimulates the pituitary gland to release FSH and LH.

Hypothalamus = master control center of endocrine activity.

Pituitary Gland

Pituitary gland is known as the master gland because it controls many other endocrine glands.

Growth Hormone (GH)

Growth hormone promotes tissue growth, bone growth, and muscle development.

It is important in meat production because it supports animal growth.

Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH)

FSH stimulates follicle development in females and sperm production in males.

Luteinizing Hormone (LH)

LH triggers ovulation in females and stimulates testosterone production in males.

Pituitary gland = master gland for growth and reproduction.

Thyroid Gland

Thyroid gland regulates metabolism and energy use in the body.

Thyroxine (T₄)

Thyroxine controls metabolic rate and supports growth and development.

High T₄ = faster metabolism
Low T₄ = slower metabolism

Parathyroid Gland

Parathyroid gland controls calcium balance in the body.

Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)

PTH increases calcium level in the blood.

It is important for bones, muscle contraction, and nerve function.

PTH = increases blood calcium.

Adrenal Glands

Adrenal glands are endocrine glands involved in stress response and fluid balance.

Adrenal Medulla

Adrenal medulla produces adrenaline for immediate stress response.

Adrenaline

Adrenaline increases heart rate, alertness, and energy availability during stress.

Adrenal Cortex

Adrenal cortex produces hormones involved in long-term stress response and electrolyte balance.

Cortisol

Cortisol is a long-term stress hormone.

  • Reduces immunity
  • Reduces growth
  • Reduces feed efficiency

Aldosterone

Aldosterone regulates water and electrolyte balance.

It promotes sodium retention and helps the body manage dehydration and heat stress.

Adrenal glands = stress response + fluid balance.

Pancreas

Pancreas has an endocrine function that helps regulate blood sugar.

Insulin

Insulin lowers blood sugar by helping cells take up glucose.

Glucagon

Glucagon raises blood sugar by stimulating glucose release into the blood.

Insulin lowers blood sugar.
Glucagon raises blood sugar.

Gonads

Gonads are reproductive glands that produce sex hormones.

Testes

Testes produce testosterone.

Testosterone

Testosterone develops male characteristics and supports muscle development.

Ovaries

Ovaries produce estrogen and progesterone.

Estrogen

Estrogen develops female characteristics and helps regulate estrus.

Progesterone

Progesterone maintains pregnancy.

Progesterone is commonly used as an indicator of pregnancy status in ruminants.

Milk Production Hormones

Prolactin

Prolactin stimulates milk synthesis or milk production.

Oxytocin

Oxytocin causes milk ejection or milk let-down.

Oxytocin is produced in the hypothalamus and released by the posterior pituitary.

Prolactin = makes milk
Oxytocin = releases milk

Hormone Types and Mechanism

Protein Hormones

Protein hormones are amino-acid based hormones that act quickly.

They usually act through receptors on the cell membrane.

Steroid Hormones

Steroid hormones are cholesterol-based hormones that are slower but longer-lasting.

They can enter the cell and affect DNA activity in the nucleus.

Lock-and-Key Mechanism

Lock-and-key mechanism means a hormone affects only cells with the correct receptor.

Protein hormones = fast, membrane action
Steroid hormones = slow, nuclear action

Feedback Mechanism

Negative Feedback

Negative feedback is a control mechanism where high hormone levels reduce further hormone production.

Negative feedback prevents hormone imbalance.

Stress Response

Stress → Sympathetic System → Adrenal Glands → Cortisol → Body Response

Effects of Stress in Livestock

  • Reduced growth
  • Lower immunity
  • Lower feed efficiency
  • Reduced weight gain

Livestock Production Application

Growth Promotants

Growth promotants are substances used to improve growth performance and production efficiency.

Beta-Agonists

Beta-agonists, such as ractopamine, mimic some effects of adrenaline and shift nutrients toward muscle growth instead of fat deposition.

Ionophores

Ionophores improve feed efficiency by modifying rumen fermentation.

Growth promotants are related to production efficiency, but proper use depends on regulations and animal management practices.

Specific Animal Facts

Poultry

Poultry do not have a typical estrus cycle like mammals.

Their reproductive activity is strongly affected by photoperiod or light exposure.

Photoperiod influences the pineal gland and hypothalamus.

Ruminants

Progesterone level is an important indicator of pregnancy status in milk or blood testing.

Final Master Summary

Hypothalamus = control center
Pituitary = master gland

Prolactin = milk production
Oxytocin = milk release

Cortisol = stress → lower production
Aldosterone = water balance

GH = growth
Testosterone = muscle and male traits
Progesterone = pregnancy

Protein hormones = fast
Steroid hormones = slow

Negative feedback = control system
