Breeding and Management Practices

Breeding and Management Practices

Breeding and management practices are the proper methods, systems, and procedures used in raising, reproducing, feeding, handling, housing, and caring for goats and sheep to improve productivity, health, reproduction, and profitability.

Good breeding and management improve fertility, growth rate, feed efficiency, survivability, disease resistance, and overall herd or flock performance.

Poor management may lead to disease outbreaks, low reproductive performance, weak offspring, slow growth, and economic losses.

Proper breeding and management improve productivity and profitability.

Breeding

Breeding is the mating of male and female animals for reproduction and genetic improvement.

The objective of breeding is to produce offspring with desirable inherited traits such as fast growth, good body conformation, adaptability, fertility, disease resistance, and good production performance.

Breeding may be done through natural mating, controlled mating, or artificial insemination.

Breeding = mating for reproduction and improvement.

Buck

A buck is the mature male goat used for breeding.

The buck contributes half of the genetic makeup of the offspring. Because of this, selecting a superior buck is very important in improving herd genetics.

A good buck should be healthy, active, masculine, fertile, and free from defects and disease.

Buck = mature male goat.

Ram

A ram is the mature male sheep used for breeding.

Rams are important in flock improvement because one superior ram may sire many lambs.

A good ram should have sound feet and legs, good body conformation, strong reproductive ability, and freedom from disease.

Ram = mature male sheep.

Doe

A doe is the mature female goat.

The doe is responsible for pregnancy, kidding, milk production, and care of the kids.

A good doe should possess fertility, good mothering ability, healthy udder structure, and good body condition.

Doe = mature female goat.

Ewe

A ewe is the mature female sheep.

The ewe carries and nourishes the lamb during pregnancy and produces milk after lambing.

Good ewe characteristics include fertility, strong maternal instinct, healthy udder, and disease resistance.

Ewe = mature female sheep.

Kid

A kid is a young goat.

The term may refer to both male and female young goats before maturity.

Kid = young goat.

Lamb

A lamb is a young sheep.

The term usually refers to sheep from birth until approximately one year of age.

Lamb = young sheep.

Puberty

Puberty is the stage when goats or sheep become sexually mature and capable of reproduction.

At puberty, males begin producing viable sperm while females begin estrous cycles and ovulation.

Puberty is influenced by breed, genetics, nutrition, body weight, and environmental conditions.

Puberty = sexual maturity.

Estrous Cycle

The estrous cycle is the reproductive cycle occurring between one heat period and the next.

In goats and sheep, the estrous cycle usually averages around 17–21 days.

The estrous cycle includes proestrus, estrus, metestrus, and diestrus.

Estrous cycle = reproductive cycle between heat periods.

Estrus or Heat

Estrus or heat is the period when the female becomes sexually receptive and willing to mate.

Common signs include restlessness, tail wagging, swollen vulva, mucus discharge, mounting behavior, and seeking the male.

Breeding is commonly done during estrus to achieve higher conception rate.

Estrus = heat period or sexual receptivity.

Ovulation

Ovulation is the release of the egg cell from the ovary.

Ovulation usually occurs near the end of estrus.

Fertilization happens when sperm unites with the ovum after ovulation.

Ovulation = release of egg from ovary.

Gestation Period

The gestation period is the length of pregnancy from conception until birth.

  • Goat = about 150 days
  • Sheep = about 147–150 days

Proper feeding and management during gestation are important for healthy offspring.

Gestation period = length of pregnancy.

Kidding

Kidding is the process of giving birth in goats.

Signs before kidding include enlarged udder, mucus discharge, restlessness, relaxation of pelvic ligaments, and isolation behavior.

Proper kidding management reduces kid mortality and improves doe recovery.

Kidding = parturition in goats.

Lambing

Lambing is the process of giving birth in sheep.

The offspring produced is called a lamb.

Proper lambing management is important to improve lamb survival and ewe recovery.

Lambing = parturition in sheep.

Flushing

Flushing is the practice of increasing the nutrition of females before breeding to improve ovulation and conception rate.

Flushing helps increase fertility, improve conception, and increase litter size.

This practice is commonly done before the breeding season.

Flushing = increased feeding before breeding.

Selection of Breeding Stock

Selection of breeding stock is the process of choosing superior animals for reproduction.

Selection should consider fertility, growth rate, adaptability, health, body conformation, and production performance.

Only healthy and productive animals should be retained for breeding.

Breeding stock selection improves future generations.

Culling

Culling is the removal of unproductive, infertile, weak, diseased, or undesirable animals from the herd or flock.

Animals may be culled because of infertility, low production, defects, disease, or poor mothering ability.

Culling improves herd quality and productivity.

Culling = removal of inferior animals.

Management

Management refers to the proper care, feeding, housing, handling, sanitation, and health maintenance of animals.

Good management improves production efficiency, animal welfare, and profitability.

Management = proper care and handling of animals.

Identification

Identification is the process of recognizing and distinguishing animals individually.

Identification is important for breeding records, health monitoring, and ownership purposes.

  • Ear tagging
  • Tattooing
  • Branding
  • Ear notching
Identification = individual animal recognition.

Record Keeping

Record keeping is the documentation of important information about animals.

Records may include breeding dates, vaccination schedules, kidding or lambing records, deworming schedules, and production records.

Good record keeping improves herd management decisions.

Record keeping = documentation of herd information.

Castration

Castration is the removal or destruction of the testes of male animals.

It is done to prevent unwanted breeding, reduce aggression, improve manageability, and improve meat quality.

A castrated male goat is called a wether.

Castration = removal of testes.

Dehorning

Dehorning is the removal of horns.

It reduces injury, fighting, and damage to facilities and improves handling safety.

Dehorning = horn removal.

Docking

Docking is the shortening or removal of the tail, especially in sheep.

Docking helps improve sanitation and reduce fly strike.

Docking = tail removal or shortening.

Weaning

Weaning is the separation of young animals from their mother and transition to solid feed.

Weaning encourages independent feeding and improves growth management.

Weaning = separation from mother.

Housing Management

Proper housing protects goats and sheep from rain, heat, predators, disease, and extreme weather conditions.

Good housing should be clean, dry, elevated, properly drained, and well ventilated.

Proper housing improves health and productivity.

Sanitation

Sanitation refers to cleanliness and hygienic practices in animal facilities.

Good sanitation reduces disease occurrence, parasite infestation, and contamination.

Practices include regular cleaning, proper manure disposal, disinfection, and clean water supply.

Sanitation reduces disease occurrence.

Biosecurity

Biosecurity is the prevention of disease introduction and spread within the herd or flock.

Biosecurity practices include quarantine, disinfection, visitor control, isolation of sick animals, and vaccination.

Biosecurity = disease prevention system.

Stress Management

Stress management involves reducing environmental and handling stress in animals.

Stress may reduce fertility, growth, immunity, and productivity.

Common stress factors include overcrowding, poor nutrition, rough handling, transport, and extreme heat.

Stress lowers animal performance.

Humane Handling

Humane handling means treating animals properly without unnecessary pain, fear, or injury.

Good handling practices include calm movement, proper restraint, and avoiding beating or overcrowding.

Humane handling improves welfare and reduces stress.
