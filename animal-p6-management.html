Poultry Housing and Management

Poultry Housing and Management

Poultry housing and management refers to the proper sheltering, environmental control, and daily care of poultry to ensure efficient production, bird comfort, health, and welfare.

It includes housing systems, house design, brooding, ventilation, lighting, litter management, sanitation, biosecurity, stocking density, and waste management.

Poultry housing and management ensure bird comfort, health, and efficient production.

Importance of Poultry Housing

  • Protects birds from heat, rain, wind, and predators
  • Reduces disease exposure
  • Improves comfort and welfare
  • Promotes efficient growth and egg production
  • Improves feed efficiency
  • Supports easy management
Housing protects birds and improves production efficiency.

Objectives of Poultry Housing

  • Provide shelter
  • Protect birds
  • Reduce stress
  • Improve feed conversion
  • Support growth and laying
  • Maintain hygiene
  • Simplify management
Poultry housing is designed for protection, comfort, and productivity.

Poultry House Orientation

Poultry house orientation refers to the direction in which the poultry house is constructed.

Proper orientation reduces heat stress and improves ventilation.

In tropical areas, poultry houses are commonly oriented east to west to minimize direct sunlight exposure on side walls.

Proper house orientation reduces heat load.

Poultry House Design

A good poultry house should be:

  • Well ventilated
  • Dry
  • Easy to clean
  • Predator proof
  • Spacious
  • Comfortable
  • Well drained
Good poultry house design improves efficiency and bird welfare.

Housing Systems

Housing systems are the methods used to shelter and manage poultry.

  • Free range = birds roam freely outdoors
  • Semi-intensive = confinement with controlled outdoor access
  • Intensive = birds kept under full confinement
Housing systems differ in confinement and management intensity.

Deep Litter System

Deep litter system is a floor-based intensive system using bedding material.

Common litter materials include rice hulls, wood shavings, and chopped straw.

Deep litter uses bedding on the floor.

Cage System

Cage system houses birds in cages, mainly for layers.

Cage system is used mainly for layer production.

Slat System

Slat system uses raised slatted floors allowing manure to fall below.

Slat system improves cleanliness and manure handling.

Floor Space Requirement

Floor space requirement is the area needed per bird for comfort and efficiency.

Insufficient space causes stress, poor growth, poor ventilation, and disease problems.

Proper floor space prevents overcrowding.

Feeder Space

Feeder space is the feeding area available per bird.

Adequate feeder space prevents competition and uneven growth.

Proper feeder space improves feeding efficiency.

Drinker Space

Drinker space is the watering access available per bird.

Adequate water access is essential for growth, health, and egg production.

Adequate drinker space ensures water availability.

Brooding

Brooding is the care of chicks during early life when they cannot regulate body temperature.

Brooding provides warmth, protection, feed access, and water access.

Brooding is essential for chick survival.

Brooder

A brooder is the heat source used for chicks.

Common brooders include electric, gas, and charcoal brooders.

Brooder provides artificial heat.

Brooding Guard

A brooding guard is a circular barrier placed around chicks to keep them near heat, feed, and water.

Brooding guard prevents chilling and crowding.

Brooding Temperature

Brooding begins at about 32–35°C in week 1.

Temperature is reduced gradually each week as chicks grow feathers and improve heat regulation.

Brooding temperature decreases gradually.

Signs of Proper Brooding

  • Chicks are active
  • Chicks are evenly distributed
  • Chicks are eating normally
  • Chicks are drinking normally
Chick behavior shows brooding comfort.

Ventilation

Ventilation is the movement of fresh air through the poultry house.

It removes heat, moisture, gases, and dust.

Ventilation maintains air quality.

Types of Ventilation

  • Natural ventilation = uses natural airflow through windows, curtains, and open sides
  • Artificial ventilation = uses fans and mechanical systems
Ventilation may be natural or mechanical.

Ammonia Control

Ammonia control is important because ammonia from manure causes respiratory irritation, poor growth, and eye damage.

Ammonia control is critical for bird health.

Lighting

Lighting controls activity, feeding, growth, and egg production.

A lighting program is the planned duration of light exposure used to regulate growth and laying.

Lighting affects behavior and productivity.

Litter Management

Litter management is the maintenance of bedding material to keep it dry, clean, and absorbent.

It includes proper litter depth, regular turning, and removal of wet or caked litter.

Litter management maintains comfort and hygiene.

Litter Depth and Turning

Litter depth is the thickness of bedding placed on the floor.

Litter turning is the regular stirring of litter to prevent caking and moisture buildup.

Proper litter depth and turning keep litter dry.

Wet Litter

Wet litter causes ammonia buildup, footpad lesions, disease, and poor air quality.

Wet litter is harmful to health and performance.

Sanitation

Sanitation means maintaining cleanliness in the poultry house.

It includes cleaning feeders and drinkers, manure removal, washing equipment, and disinfection.

Sanitation reduces contamination and disease.

Disinfection

Disinfection is the use of chemicals to kill harmful microorganisms.

Disinfection reduces pathogen load.

Downtime

Downtime is the rest period between flocks for cleaning and disinfection.

Downtime breaks disease cycles.

All-In All-Out System

All-in all-out system means placing and removing birds as one group.

All-in all-out improves disease control.

Biosecurity

Biosecurity is the prevention of disease entry and spread.

Common measures include footbaths, restricted entry, disinfection, quarantine, visitor control, and rodent control.

Biosecurity prevents disease introduction.

Traffic Control and Zoning

Traffic control limits movement of people, vehicles, and equipment.

Zoning separates clean and dirty areas on the farm.

Traffic control and zoning limit contamination.

Pest and Rodent Control

Pest control reduces flies, beetles, and insects.

Rodent control prevents rats and mice from spreading disease and consuming feed.

Pests and rodents spread disease and waste feed.

Dead Bird Disposal

Dead birds must be disposed properly through burial, burning, or composting.

Proper disposal prevents disease spread.

Waste Management

Waste management is the proper handling of manure and waste materials.

Waste management improves sanitation and environmental safety.

Stocking Density

Stocking density is the number of birds per unit area.

Overcrowding causes stress, poor growth, poor ventilation, disease, and feather pecking.

Stocking density affects welfare and production.

Board Exam Master Concept

Poultry housing and management focus on comfort, environment, hygiene, ventilation, and disease prevention.

Proper housing and management improve poultry productivity.
