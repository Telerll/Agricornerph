Ration Formulation

Ration Formulation

Ration formulation is the process of combining feeds in the correct proportions to meet the nutrient needs of an animal.

The goal is to provide:

  • Adequate nutrients
  • Proper balance
  • Efficient feeding
  • Economical production
Ration formulation = balancing nutrients for animal needs.

Ration

Ration is the total amount of feed given to an animal in 24 hours.

It must supply nutrients for:

  • Maintenance
  • Growth
  • Reproduction
  • Production
A ration is the animal’s daily feed allowance.

Importance of Ration Formulation

Ration formulation is important because it helps:

  • Meet nutrient requirements
  • Reduce feed cost
  • Improve productivity
  • Avoid deficiencies
  • Prevent waste
Proper ration formulation improves efficiency and profit.

Factors Considered in Ration Formulation

Rations are formulated based on:

  • Species
  • Age
  • Body weight
  • Production stage
  • Health status
  • Nutrient requirements
  • Feed availability
  • Feed cost
Ration formulation must match the animal’s needs.

Characteristics of a Good Ration

A good ration must be:

  • Balanced
  • Digestible
  • Palatable
  • Economical
  • Safe
  • Practical
A good ration is nutritionally correct and economically efficient.

Types of Rations

Maintenance Ration

Maintenance ration supplies nutrients only for basic survival.

Production Ration

Production ration supplies nutrients for milk, eggs, meat, work, or growth.

Balanced Ration

Balanced ration contains all nutrients in proper amounts and proportions.

Balanced ration is the goal in feeding.

Nutrients Considered in Ration Formulation

A ration must provide:

  • Water
  • Energy
  • Protein
  • Minerals
  • Vitamins
All nutrients must be balanced, not just one.

Steps in Ration Formulation

Basic steps:

  1. Identify animal type
  2. Determine nutrient requirements
  3. Identify available feed ingredients
  4. Determine nutrient composition of feeds
  5. Combine feeds in proper proportions
  6. Evaluate cost and practicality
Ration formulation is both nutritional and economic.

Pearson Square Method

Pearson Square Method is a simple way to formulate rations using two feed ingredients.

Commonly used for:

  • Balancing crude protein
  • Simple feed mixing
Pearson Square is a basic board exam calculation tool.

Pearson Square Steps

  1. Place desired nutrient level in the center
  2. Place feed nutrient values at the corners
  3. Subtract diagonally
  4. Use differences as proportions
Pearson Square is used for simple two-ingredient formulation.

Example of Pearson Square

Goal: formulate a 16% crude protein ration using:

  • Corn = 8% CP
  • Soybean meal = 44% CP

Pearson Square determines the proportion of each ingredient needed to reach 16% CP.

This is a common board exam computation.

Board Exam Calculation Shortcuts

The Middle Rule

In Pearson Square, the target nutrient level must always fall between the two feed values.

Example:

  • Corn = 8% CP
  • Soybean meal = 44% CP
  • Target = 16% CP — workable

Impossible examples:

  • Target = 50% CP
  • Target = 5% CP
The desired value must always be between the two ingredients.

Parts to Percent Rule

After diagonal subtraction, the answers are called parts, not percentages.

Ingredient % = ingredient parts ÷ total parts × 100
Always convert parts into percent of the final ration.

Pearson Square Limitation

Pearson Square is only for:

  • Two ingredients
  • One nutrient, usually crude protein
Pearson Square = simple but foundational.

Advanced Formulation Methods

More advanced ration methods include:

  • Trial and error

  • Least-cost formulation
  • Computer formulation
Pearson Square is the foundation of feed calculations.

Least-Cost Ration

Least-cost ration is a ration that meets nutrient needs at the lowest possible cost.

Goal:

  • Meet requirements
  • Reduce cost
  • Maintain performance
Best ration is not the cheapest feed, but the cheapest balanced feed.

Importance of Feed Cost

Feed is the largest cost in livestock production.

Feed usually accounts for 60–70% of total production cost.

Ration formulation helps:

  • Control cost
  • Reduce waste
  • Improve profitability

Feeding Systems

Ad Libitum Feeding

Ad libitum feeding means feed is offered free-choice, allowing the animal to eat as much as it wants.

Common in:

  • Broilers
  • Layers
  • Growing pigs
  • Dairy cattle

Advantages:

  • Supports rapid growth
  • Supports high production
  • Reduces competition for feed

Disadvantages:

  • May increase feed waste
  • May lead to overconsumption
Ad libitum = all the animal can eat.

Restricted Feeding

Restricted feeding means feed intake is controlled or limited.

Common in:

  • Breeding stock
  • Gestating sows
  • Dry cows
  • Replacement pullets

Advantages:

  • Prevents obesity
  • Improves feed efficiency
  • Controls body condition

Disadvantages:

  • Slower growth
  • Possible competition if poorly managed
Restricted feeding = controlled intake.

Board Exam Feeding Rule

Use:

  • Ad libitum for growth and production
  • Restricted feeding for breeding and body condition control
Feeding system depends on production goal.

Practical Feeding Considerations

A good ration must also consider:

  • Storage
  • Spoilage risk
  • Animal preference
The best ration on paper must still work in practice.
