Health Management in Beef Cattle

This lesson covers cattle health management, disease prevention, signs of healthy and sick animals, common diseases, parasites, biosecurity, vaccination, sanitation, and board exam recall points.

Health Management in Beef Cattle

Health Management

Health management refers to the prevention, control, and treatment of diseases and disorders affecting beef cattle.

Proper health management improves productivity, growth, reproduction, feed efficiency, and profitability.

Healthy cattle perform better and produce higher-quality meat.

Good health management improves cattle productivity.

Objectives of Health Management

The objectives of health management include:

  • Disease prevention
  • Disease control
  • Maintenance of animal health
  • Reduction of mortality
  • Improvement of productivity
Disease prevention is a major goal of health management.

Animal Health

Animal health refers to the normal physical and physiological condition of cattle.

Healthy cattle usually show:

  • Good appetite
  • Normal movement
  • Alertness
  • Normal body temperature
  • Smooth hair coat
Healthy animals show normal behavior and appetite.

Signs of Healthy Cattle

  • Bright eyes
  • Moist muzzle
  • Smooth hair coat
  • Active movement
  • Normal feeding behavior
  • Regular rumination
Healthy cattle are active and alert.

Signs of Sick Cattle

  • Loss of appetite
  • Weakness
  • Coughing
  • Diarrhea
  • Nasal discharge
  • Rough hair coat
  • Abnormal temperature
Sick cattle often show poor appetite and weakness.

Disease

Disease refers to abnormal conditions affecting normal body function.

Diseases may be caused by:

  • Bacteria
  • Viruses
  • Parasites
  • Fungi
  • Nutritional deficiencies
Disease disrupts normal body functions.

Infectious Diseases

Infectious diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms and can spread between animals.

Infectious diseases are transmissible.

Non-Infectious Diseases

Non-infectious diseases are not caused by infectious organisms.

Examples include nutritional deficiency, poisoning, and metabolic disorders.

Non-infectious diseases are not transmissible.

Bacterial Diseases

Bacterial diseases are caused by bacteria.

  • Anthrax
  • Blackleg
  • Mastitis
Bacteria cause infectious diseases.

Viral Diseases

Viral diseases are caused by viruses.

  • Foot-and-mouth disease
  • Rabies
Viruses cause highly contagious diseases.

Parasitic Diseases

Parasitic diseases are caused by internal or external parasites.

  • Liver fluke
  • Roundworms
  • Ticks
  • Mange mites
Parasites reduce productivity.

Fungal Diseases

Fungal diseases are caused by fungi.

Example: ringworm.

Fungi may infect skin and tissues.

Anthrax

Anthrax is a highly fatal bacterial disease caused by Bacillus anthracis.

Signs may include sudden death, bleeding from body openings, and fever.

Anthrax is zoonotic.

Anthrax = Bacillus anthracis.

Blackleg

Blackleg is a bacterial disease affecting young cattle.

Signs may include lameness, swelling of muscles, and fever.

Blackleg affects muscles.

Mastitis

Mastitis is inflammation of the udder.

It is commonly caused by bacterial infection and poor sanitation.

Mastitis affects the udder.

Foot-and-Mouth Disease

Foot-and-mouth disease or FMD is a highly contagious viral disease.

Signs include blisters in the mouth, lameness, and excessive salivation.

FMD is highly contagious.

Rabies

Rabies is a fatal viral disease affecting the nervous system.

It is zoonotic and transmissible to humans.

Rabies affects the nervous system.

Liver Fluke

Liver fluke is an internal parasite affecting the liver.

Effects may include poor growth, weakness, and reduced productivity.

Liver fluke damages the liver.

Mange

Mange is a skin disease caused by mites.

Signs include itching, hair loss, and skin irritation.

Mange is caused by mites.

Tick Infestation

Ticks are external parasites that suck blood and transmit diseases.

Ticks reduce animal performance.

Roundworms

Roundworms are internal parasites affecting digestion and growth.

Roundworms reduce feed efficiency.

Bloat

Bloat refers to excessive gas accumulation in the rumen.

Signs may include swollen abdomen and difficulty breathing.

Bloat = gas accumulation in rumen.

Ketosis

Ketosis is a metabolic disorder caused by energy deficiency.

Ketosis is related to energy imbalance.

Milk Fever

Milk fever is caused by calcium deficiency.

Signs may include weakness and inability to stand.

Milk fever is associated with low calcium.

Diarrhea

Diarrhea involves frequent loose feces and may cause dehydration.

Severe diarrhea may cause dehydration.

Pneumonia

Pneumonia is inflammation of the lungs.

Signs may include coughing, fever, and difficulty breathing.

Pneumonia affects the respiratory system.

Lameness

Lameness refers to difficulty in walking.

Causes may include foot rot, injuries, and hoof problems.

Lameness affects movement.

Foot Rot

Foot rot is an infectious hoof disease.

Signs may include swelling, foul odor, and lameness.

Foot rot affects the hooves.

Biosecurity

Biosecurity involves practices preventing disease introduction and spread.

Examples include quarantine, sanitation, and visitor control.

Biosecurity prevents disease spread.

Quarantine

Quarantine refers to isolation of animals to prevent disease transmission.

Quarantine limits disease spread.

Vaccination

Vaccination stimulates immunity against specific diseases.

Vaccination prevents disease.

Deworming

Deworming controls internal parasites.

Deworming improves animal health.

Sanitation

Sanitation involves maintaining cleanliness of facilities and equipment.

Sanitation reduces disease risk.

Disinfection

Disinfection destroys harmful microorganisms using chemicals.

Disinfection controls pathogens.

Proper Housing

Good housing should provide:

  • Ventilation
  • Dryness
  • Comfort
  • Protection
Proper housing improves animal health.

Ventilation

Ventilation removes excess heat, moisture, and harmful gases.

Ventilation improves air quality.

Nutrition and Health

Good nutrition strengthens immunity and improves disease resistance.

Proper nutrition improves resistance to disease.

Stress

Stress may reduce immunity and productivity.

Causes include heat, overcrowding, transportation, and poor nutrition.

Stress weakens animals.

Heat Stress

Heat stress occurs when cattle cannot regulate body temperature effectively.

Signs include panting, reduced feed intake, and weakness.

Heat stress reduces productivity.

Observation and Monitoring

Regular observation helps detect diseases early.

Early detection improves treatment success.

Record Keeping

Health records may include:

  • Vaccination dates
  • Treatments
  • Disease history
  • Deworming schedule
Record keeping improves herd management.

Veterinary Care

Veterinarians assist in diagnosis, treatment, vaccination, and disease prevention.

Veterinary care improves herd health.

Zoonotic Diseases

Zoonotic diseases are transmissible from animals to humans.

Examples include anthrax and rabies.

Zoonotic diseases infect humans.

Mortality

Mortality refers to animal death within the herd.

Good health management reduces mortality.

Proper management lowers mortality rate.

Importance of Health Management

Proper health management improves growth, reproduction, feed efficiency, survival, and profitability.

Health management improves productivity and profitability.

Board Exam Trap Terms

  • Anthrax = Bacillus anthracis
  • Mastitis = udder inflammation
  • FMD = highly contagious viral disease
  • Rabies = nervous system disease
  • Liver fluke = liver parasite
  • Mange = mite infestation
  • Bloat = gas accumulation in rumen
  • Milk fever = calcium deficiency
  • Quarantine = isolation
  • Vaccination = immunity stimulation
  • Biosecurity = disease prevention
These are common board recall terms in cattle health management.
