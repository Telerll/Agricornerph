Introduction

Herbicides are among the most widely used weed control tools in modern agriculture. They provide an efficient, economical, and effective means of controlling weeds in agricultural and non-agricultural environments.

The proper use of herbicides can significantly reduce weed competition, improve crop productivity, and lower labor requirements. However, improper use may result in crop injury, environmental contamination, and herbicide resistance.

Understanding herbicide classification, formulation, application, selectivity, and mode of action is essential for effective weed management and is heavily emphasized in the AgLE Board Examination.

Definition:
A herbicide is a chemical substance used to kill, suppress, or control unwanted plants (weeds).

Importance of Herbicides

  • Reduce crop-weed competition
  • Increase crop yield
  • Reduce labor costs
  • Improve farm efficiency
  • Facilitate large-scale crop production
Board Tip:
Herbicides are chemicals specifically designed to control weeds.

I. Classification Based on Selectivity

A. Selective Herbicides

Selective herbicides kill certain weed species without seriously injuring the crop.

Characteristics

  • Target specific weeds
  • Safe to crops when properly applied
  • Widely used in crop production

Examples

  • 2,4-D
  • MCPA
  • Butachlor
  • Propanil

Uses

  • Rice
  • Corn
  • Wheat
  • Other cereal crops
Board Tip:
2,4-D = Selective Herbicide

B. Non-Selective Herbicides

Non-selective herbicides kill or injure most vegetation.

Characteristics

  • Broad-spectrum activity
  • Used for total vegetation control
  • Effective on many weed species

Examples

  • Glyphosate
  • Paraquat
  • Glufosinate
Board Tip:
Glyphosate = Non-Selective Herbicide

II. Classification Based on Movement Within the Plant

A. Contact Herbicides

Contact herbicides kill only the plant parts directly contacted by the spray.

Characteristics

  • Rapid action
  • Limited translocation
  • Most effective on annual weeds

Examples

  • Paraquat
  • Diquat

Advantages

  • Fast weed kill
  • Quick visible results

Disadvantages

  • Poor control of perennial weeds
Board Tip:
Contact Herbicide = Kills only sprayed tissues.

B. Systemic Herbicides

Systemic herbicides are absorbed and translocated throughout the plant.

Characteristics

  • Move through xylem and phloem
  • Kill roots and shoots
  • Effective against perennial weeds

Examples

  • Glyphosate
  • 2,4-D
Board Tip:
Systemic Herbicide = Moves throughout the plant.

III. Classification Based on Time of Application

A. Pre-Plant Herbicides

Applied before crop planting.

Example: Glyphosate during land preparation.

B. Pre-Emergence Herbicides

Applied after planting but before weed emergence.

Examples

  • Butachlor
  • Pendimethalin
Board Tip:
Pre-emergence = Before weed emergence.

C. Post-Emergence Herbicides

Applied after weeds have emerged.

Examples

  • 2,4-D
  • Glyphosate
  • Paraquat
Board Tip:
Post-emergence = After weed emergence.

IV. Classification Based on Site of Application

A. Soil-Applied Herbicides

Applied directly to the soil and usually affect germinating weed seeds.

Examples

  • Pendimethalin
  • Butachlor

B. Foliar-Applied Herbicides

Applied directly to plant leaves.

Examples

  • Glyphosate
  • Paraquat
  • 2,4-D

V. Herbicide Formulations

A herbicide formulation is the commercial preparation in which the active ingredient is mixed with other substances to improve handling, storage, and application.

A. EC (Emulsifiable Concentrate)

Liquid formulation that forms an emulsion when mixed with water.

B. WP (Wettable Powder)

Powder formulation mixed with water before application.

C. SC (Suspension Concentrate)

Finely ground particles suspended in liquid.

D. G (Granular Formulation)

Applied directly to soil without dilution.

Board Tip:
EC = Emulsifiable Concentrate
WP = Wettable Powder
SC = Suspension Concentrate
G = Granular

VI. Herbicide Application Methods

A. Broadcast Application

Herbicide is applied uniformly over the entire field.

B. Band Application

Herbicide is applied in narrow strips along crop rows.

C. Spot Treatment

Applied only to weed-infested areas.

D. Directed Spraying

Spray is directed away from crop plants.

E. Rope-Wick Application

Herbicide is transferred by contact through saturated ropes or wicks.

VII. Herbicide Mode of Action

Mode of action refers to the specific biological process disrupted by a herbicide.

Importance

  • Improves herbicide selection
  • Prevents resistance
  • Improves weed control efficiency

A. Photosynthesis Inhibitors

Prevent food production in plants.

B. Growth Regulators

Cause abnormal growth and development.

Example: 2,4-D

C. Amino Acid Synthesis Inhibitors

Prevent protein production.

Example: Glyphosate

D. Cell Membrane Disruptors

Destroy plant cell membranes.

Example: Paraquat

Board Tip:
Glyphosate inhibits amino acid synthesis.
2,4-D acts as a growth regulator.
Paraquat disrupts cell membranes.

VIII. Herbicide Resistance

Definition

Herbicide resistance is the inherited ability of a weed population to survive herbicide applications that previously controlled it.

Causes

  • Repeated use of the same herbicide
  • Overreliance on chemical control
  • Lack of herbicide rotation

Management

  • Herbicide rotation
  • Tank mixing
  • Integrated Weed Management
  • Crop rotation
Board Tip:
Repeated use of one herbicide promotes resistance.

IX. Advantages of Herbicides

  • Effective weed control
  • Reduced labor
  • Fast application
  • Suitable for large farms
  • Improved crop yield

X. Disadvantages of Herbicides

  • Crop injury
  • Environmental contamination
  • Herbicide resistance
  • Health concerns
  • Cost of chemicals

ALE Quick Facts

  • Herbicide = chemical used to control weeds.
  • Selective herbicides target specific weeds.
  • Non-selective herbicides kill most plants.
  • Glyphosate is systemic and non-selective.
  • Paraquat is contact and non-selective.
  • 2,4-D is selective and systemic.
  • Pre-emergence herbicides act before weed emergence.
  • Post-emergence herbicides act after weed emergence.
  • Herbicide resistance results from repeated herbicide use.

Summary

Herbicides are chemical substances used to kill, suppress, or control weeds. They may be classified according to selectivity, movement within the plant, time of application, site of application, formulation, and mode of action. Common herbicides include glyphosate, paraquat, and 2,4-D. Understanding herbicide classification and proper application is essential for effective weed management and resistance prevention. Herbicides remain an important component of Integrated Weed Management and are among the most heavily tested topics in Weed Science and the AgLE Board Examination.
