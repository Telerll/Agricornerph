Plant Disease Diagnosis

Plant disease diagnosis is the process of identifying a plant disease by observing symptoms, signs, environmental conditions, and the possible causal organism involved.

  • Correct diagnosis helps determine the proper control method.
  • Symptoms alone may not be reliable because different pathogens may produce similar symptoms.
  • The presence of signs increases the accuracy of diagnosis.
  • Secondary invaders may be mistaken as the primary pathogen.

Symptoms of Plant Diseases

Symptoms are the visible reactions or expressions of the host plant to infection or disease.

Primary vs. Secondary Symptoms

  • Primary symptoms are direct and immediate effects on infected tissues.
  • Secondary symptoms are indirect effects appearing on distant or uninfected plant parts.

Localized vs. Systemic Symptoms

  • Localized symptoms are limited to a small area, such as leaf spots, cankers, and galls.
  • Systemic symptoms affect the whole plant system, such as mosaic, mottling, and wilting.

Histological vs. Morphological Symptoms

  • Histological symptoms are internal changes seen under the microscope.
  • Morphological symptoms are external visible abnormalities.

General Classification of Symptoms

Plesionecrotic Symptoms

These are symptoms that appear before the actual death of plant cells.

  • Yellowing
  • Wilting
  • Silvering

Necrotic Symptoms

These involve the death of protoplasts, cells, or tissues.

  • Spot
  • Blight
  • Scorch
  • Canker
  • Die-back

Hypoplastic Symptoms

These involve underdevelopment, inhibition, or failure of normal plant growth.

  • Stunting
  • Chlorosis
  • Mosaic
  • Curling
  • Rosetting

Hyperplastic Symptoms

These involve excessive multiplication, enlargement, or overdevelopment of plant organs.

  • Gall formation
  • Fasciation
  • Scab
  • Premature greening
  • Hypertrophy means increase in cell size.
  • Hyperplasia means increase in number of cells.

Important Plant Disease Symptoms

Abscission

Premature falling of leaves, flowers, or fruits.

Blast

Sudden death of young buds, inflorescence, or young fruits.

Bleeding

Flow of plant sap from wounds.

Blight

Extensive and usually sudden death of host tissue.

Blotch

Large irregular necrotic spots on leaves or fruits.

Callus

Overgrowth of tissue formed in response to injury.

Canker

Sunken necrotic area with cracked borders on leaves, fruits, stems, or branches.

Chlorosis

Yellowing caused by factors other than light, such as infection or nutrient problems.

Curling

Abnormal bending or curling of leaves.

Damping-Off

Rotting of seedlings before or after emergence.

Die-Back

Drying backward from the tip of twigs or branches.

Etiolation

Yellowing of normally green tissues caused by inadequate light.

Fasciation

Abnormal clustering or flattening of roots, flowers, fruits, or twigs.

Flecks

Extremely tiny spots on leaves, fruits, or stems.

Gumming or Gummosis

Oozing of viscous gum from wounds in the bark.

Leak

Exudation of host juices from soft-rotted portions.

Mosaic

Sharply defined variegated patterns of green and yellow areas on leaves.

Mottling

Less defined variegation with more diffused boundaries.

Mummification

Conversion of infected fruit into a hard, dry, shriveled mummy.

Phyllody

Transformation of floral parts into leaf-like structures.

Pitting

Depressions or pits on the surface of fruits, tubers, or fleshy organs.

Rosetting

Shortened internodes causing leaves to crowd into a rosette.

Rotting

Disintegration and decomposition of host tissue.

Russeting

Superficial brownish roughening of fruit or tuber skin.

Sarcody

Abnormal swelling of bark above wounds due to accumulated food materials.

Savoying

Cupping, pocketing, curling, or puckering of leaf parts.

Scab

Raised, rough, ulcer-like lesions caused by overgrowth of surface tissues.

Shot-Hole

Perforated leaf appearance when dead lesion areas drop out.

Spot

Localized necrotic lesion, usually circular, angular, or irregular.

Streak or Stripe

Long narrow necrotic lesions on leaves or stems.

Vein Clearing

Leaf veins become translucent or pale while the rest of the leaf remains normal.

Virescence or Greening

Development of chlorophyll in tissues or organs where it is normally absent.

Wilting

Drooping caused by lack of water or infection affecting water movement.

Signs of Plant Diseases

Signs are the actual structures or products of the pathogen found on infected plant parts.

  • Signs represent the pathogen itself.
  • They may not always be visible because formation depends on environmental conditions.
  • Many signs are best seen under the microscope.

General Categories of Signs

Vegetative Structures

  • Felt – densely woven mat of mycelium.
  • Haustorium – absorbing organ of a fungus.
  • Mycelium – mass of fungal threads or hyphae.
  • Pathogen cells – masses of bacterial cells.
  • Plasmodium – naked mass of protoplasm.
  • Rhizomorph – cord-like strand of fungal hyphae.
  • Sclerotium – hard resting body made of fungal hyphae.

Reproductive Structures

  • Acervulus – mat of hyphae forming eruptive lesions.
  • Apothecium – open cup-like fruiting body.
  • Ascus – sac-like structure containing ascospores.
  • Basidium – club-shaped structure producing basidiospores.
  • Conidiophore – branch that produces conidia.
  • Mildew – cobwebby or powdery growth on leaves.
  • Mold – woolly or furry mycelial growth.
  • Mushroom – umbrella-shaped fruiting body.
  • Pycnidium – hollow fruiting body containing conidia.
  • Sporangium – structure bearing sporangiospores.
  • Spore – reproductive propagule of fungi.
  • Stroma – compact mass of fungal hyphae.

Disease Products

  • Odor – characteristic smell associated with infection.
  • Ooze – viscous mass made of plant juices and pathogen cells.

Signs According to Pathogen

  • Fungal diseases: mycelia, spores, fruiting bodies.
  • Bacterial diseases: bacterial cells and bacterial ooze.
  • Nematode diseases: eggs, juveniles, adult nematodes.
  • Virus diseases: virus particles and inclusion bodies.
  • Viroid diseases: RNA fragments.
  • Parasitic flowering plants: seeds and the plant itself.

Koch’s Rules of Proof of Pathogenicity

Koch’s postulates are rules used to prove that a suspected organism is the actual cause of a disease.

  • The suspected pathogen must always be present in diseased plants.
  • The organism must be isolated and grown in pure culture.
  • The pure culture must produce the same disease when inoculated into a healthy plant.
  • The organism must be re-isolated and proven identical to the original pathogen.

Koch’s rules may need modification when dealing with obligate parasites, viruses, nematodes, abiotic factors, or non-living agents.
