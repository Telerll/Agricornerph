Definition of Disease Cycle

The disease cycle refers to the sequence of events involved in the development, spread, reproduction, and survival of a plant pathogen within the host plant and the environment.

It explains how a disease starts, develops, spreads, survives, and repeats from one season or plant generation to another.

  • Production of inoculum
  • Dissemination or spread
  • Inoculation
  • Penetration
  • Infection
  • Colonization
  • Symptom development
  • Survival

Importance of Disease Cycle

Understanding the disease cycle helps explain when and how plant diseases occur. It also helps identify the best point where disease control can interrupt the cycle.

  • Helps predict disease outbreaks
  • Guides timing of control measures
  • Helps reduce crop losses
  • Improves disease forecasting
  • Improves timing of fungicide or pesticide application

1. Production of Inoculum

Definition

Inoculum refers to any part of a pathogen capable of causing infection. It is the infectious material that initiates disease.

Examples of Inoculum

Fungi

  • Spores
  • Mycelia
  • Sclerotia
  • Rhizomorphs

Bacteria

  • Bacterial cells

Viruses

  • Virus particles

Nematodes

  • Eggs
  • Larvae or juveniles
  • Adults

Parasitic Higher Plants

  • Seeds

Sources of Inoculum

  • Infected living plants
  • Plant debris
  • Infested soil
  • Infected seeds and vegetative propagating materials
  • Contaminated tools, containers, storage areas, and equipment
  • Insects, nematodes, and other living agents that carry inocula
  • Alternate hosts and weeds

Survival of Pathogens

Pathogens survive unfavorable environmental conditions through special structures or survival strategies.

Common Survival Mechanisms

  • Surviving as saprophytes in dead plant debris or soil
  • Forming thick-walled resistant structures
  • Surviving in weeds and alternate hosts
  • Surviving inside insect vectors
  • Surviving in seeds or planting materials

Examples by Pathogen Group

Fungi

  • Sclerotia
  • Chlamydospores
  • Dormant mycelia

Bacteria

  • Plant debris
  • Seeds
  • Biofilms

Viruses

  • Vectors
  • Perennial infected hosts
  • Infected planting materials

Nematodes

  • Dormant eggs
  • Cysts

2. Dissemination of Inocula

Definition

Dissemination is the spread or movement of inoculum from one place to another. It allows pathogens to reach healthy host plants.

After symptoms have advanced, pathogen structures may be formed on the colonized surface of the host. These structures can serve as inoculum and may be spread by wind, rain, insects, humans, and other agents.

Dissemination by Wind

Wind is one of the major means of spreading airborne pathogens, especially fungal spores.

Stages of Wind Dissemination

  • Take-off: inoculum gets into the air.
  • Flight: inoculum moves with the wind.
  • Deposition: inoculum settles from the atmosphere.

Viruses, viroids, mycoplasmas, nematodes, and bacteria are generally not commonly disseminated by wind, except under special conditions such as soil particles or occasional movement.

Dissemination by Rain

Rain spreads fungal spores and bacterial cells mainly through rain splash and water movement. This usually spreads pathogens over short distances.

Dissemination by Insects

Insects can transmit pathogens from diseased plants to healthy plants while feeding.

  • Viruses
  • Bacteria
  • Fungi
  • Mollicutes

Dissemination by Seeds and Planting Materials

Infected or infested planting materials can carry pathogens externally or internally.

  • Viruses
  • Viroids
  • Mycoplasmas
  • Bacteria
  • Fungal spores
  • Nematodes

Dissemination by Humans

Humans are major long-distance disseminators of plant pathogens.

  • Transport of infected seeds and planting materials
  • Contaminated tools and machinery
  • Shipping crates and agricultural equipment
  • Movement of infected products

3. Inoculation

Definition

Inoculation is the contact of a pathogen with the host. It occurs when inoculum is deposited onto or into an infection court.

Infection Court

The infection court is the susceptible part of the plant where infection may occur.

  • Natural openings
  • Wounds
  • Intact plant surfaces
  • Root surfaces

Types of Inoculum

Fungi

  • Asexual or sexual spores
  • Mycelial fragments
  • Sclerotial bodies
  • Rhizomorphs
  • Dormant mycelia in seeds

Nematodes

  • Eggs
  • Larvae or juveniles
  • Adults

Bacteria

  • Bacterial cells
  • Phytoplasma cells
  • Protozoan cells

Others

  • Virus particles
  • Viroid entities
  • Seeds of parasitic flowering plants

4. Penetration or Ingress

Definition

Penetration or ingress is the entrance of the pathogen into the host plant. It is completed when the pathogen has passed through the initial cell wall or has entered the intercellular spaces.

Entry Points

1. Natural Openings

  • Stomata
  • Lenticels
  • Hydathodes
  • Nectarthodes

2. Intact Host Surface

Some pathogens, especially fungi, can penetrate directly through the intact epidermis.

3. Wounds

Wounds caused by insects, tools, wind, pruning, or mechanical injury can serve as entry points.

Types of Penetration

1. Passive Penetration

The pathogen does not actively participate in penetration. For example, bacteria may be carried by a film of water through stomata into host tissues.

2. Active Penetration

The pathogen directly participates in penetration. For example, fungal spores germinate, form germ tubes and appressoria, then penetrate the intact host surface using infection hyphae or penetration pegs.

5. Infection

Definition

Infection occurs when the pathogen becomes established in the plant tissues after penetration and begins obtaining nutrients from the host.

Latent Infection

Latent infection is the state in which the host is infected with the pathogen but does not show visible symptoms.

Latent infection is important because infected plants may appear healthy while already carrying the pathogen.

6. Colonization or Invasion

Definition

Colonization or invasion is the growth or movement of the pathogen through host tissues.

Active and Passive Colonization

Active Colonization

The pathogen actively grows or moves from cell to cell.

Passive Colonization

The pathogen is carried through the plant system, such as through the transpiration stream.

Effects of Colonization

Pathogens may produce harmful chemicals that damage plant tissues.

  • Enzymes
  • Toxins
  • Hormones

Colonization by Viruses, Viroids, and Mollicutes

These pathogens are intracellular parasites. They colonize cells and may spread through epidermal cells, palisade cells, spongy mesophyll, and vascular tissues.

Colonization by Bacteria

  • Intracellular colonization in xylem and phloem vessels
  • Intercellular colonization between plant cells
  • May cause gall formation, vascular wilting, and soft rots

Colonization by Nematodes

  • Sedentary endoparasites
  • Migratory endoparasites

Colonization by Phytoplasma

Phytoplasmas multiply by binary fission and budding, producing more cells in the phloem tissues.

Colonization by Fungi

  • Colonization on the host surface
  • External colonization with haustoria inside host cells
  • Intercellular colonization without haustoria
  • Intercellular colonization with intracellular haustoria
  • Intracellular colonization

7. Incubation

Definition

The incubation period is the time from inoculation to the appearance of visible symptoms.

It may also refer to the time from the first response of the plant to the formation of visible symptoms.

Importance of Incubation Period

  • Helps in disease diagnosis
  • Helps in disease forecasting
  • Guides timing of control measures

Factors Affecting Incubation Period

  • Temperature
  • Humidity
  • Host susceptibility
  • Pathogen virulence
  • Plant age

8. Symptom Development

Definition

Symptom development is the stage when visible signs of disease appear on the plant as a result of pathogen activity.

Common Symptoms

  • Chlorosis
  • Wilting
  • Leaf spots
  • Blights
  • Rotting
  • Galls
  • Cankers
  • Mosaic
  • Necrosis

9. Secondary Inoculum and Secondary Cycles

After symptom development, pathogens may produce secondary inoculum. This secondary inoculum can initiate new infections within the same growing season.

Primary Disease Cycle

The primary cycle is initiated by primary inoculum that survived from previous seasons.

Secondary Disease Cycle

Secondary cycles occur when new inoculum produced during the current season causes repeated infections.

Monocyclic Diseases

Monocyclic diseases usually have one major disease cycle per growing season.

Polycyclic Diseases

Polycyclic diseases have repeated cycles in one season and can cause rapid epidemics.

10. Survival Stage

Definition

Survival is the ability of a pathogen to remain alive during unfavorable conditions or when no susceptible host is available.

Some pathogen structures may not land on a susceptible plant, and environmental conditions may not favor continued growth and development. In such cases, the pathogen must survive until conditions become favorable.

Disease Cycle and Disease Management

Understanding each stage of the disease cycle helps identify where the cycle can be interrupted.

Control Strategies

During Dissemination

  • Quarantine
  • Sanitation
  • Control movement of infected materials

During Inoculation

  • Remove infected plants
  • Use clean seeds and planting materials
  • Disinfect tools and equipment

During Penetration

  • Protect wounds
  • Avoid unnecessary injury to plants
  • Apply protective fungicides when needed

During Colonization

  • Use resistant varieties
  • Apply systemic fungicides where appropriate
  • Improve plant health and nutrition

During Survival

  • Crop rotation
  • Field sanitation
  • Destroy crop residues
  • Remove volunteer plants and weeds
