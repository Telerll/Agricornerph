🌿 Part 8: Allelopathy and Weed-Crop Interactions

Comprehensive Weed Science Reviewer

Introduction

After understanding crop-weed competition, it is important to recognize that weeds can affect crops not only through competition for resources but also through chemical interactions.

Some weeds release chemical substances into the environment that can influence the germination, growth, development, and survival of neighboring plants. This phenomenon is known as allelopathy.

Allelopathy plays a significant role in plant interactions and contributes to the success and persistence of many weed species.

Definition:
Allelopathy is the phenomenon in which one plant releases chemical substances that influence the growth, development, survival, or reproduction of another plant.

Origin of the Term

  • Allelon = one another
  • Pathos = suffering
Board Tip:
Allelopathy = Chemical Interaction
Competition = Resource Competition

I. Allelochemicals

Allelochemicals are chemical substances released by plants that affect neighboring plants.

Sources of Allelochemicals

  • Roots — release root exudates into the soil.
  • Leaves — chemicals may be washed by rain into the soil.
  • Stems — chemicals may be released during decomposition.
  • Flowers — some flowering parts contain inhibitory compounds.
  • Seeds — some seeds contain germination inhibitors.
  • Plant residues — decomposing plant materials release allelochemicals.
Board Tip:
Most allelochemicals enter the soil through root exudates and plant residues.

II. Mechanisms of Allelopathy

A. Inhibition of Seed Germination

  • Delayed germination
  • Reduced germination percentage
  • Abnormal seedling development
  • Failure of germination

B. Inhibition of Root Growth

  • Shorter roots
  • Thinner roots
  • Poorly branched roots
  • Reduced nutrient and water uptake

C. Inhibition of Shoot Growth

  • Reduced plant height
  • Smaller leaves
  • Reduced biomass
  • Stunted growth

D. Interference with Photosynthesis

  • Reduced chlorophyll formation
  • Reduced photosynthesis
  • Reduced plant vigor

E. Interference with Nutrient Uptake

  • Nutrient deficiencies
  • Poor plant growth
  • Reduced yield

III. Types of Allelopathic Effects

A. Inhibitory Allelopathy

Chemical substances suppress plant growth and development.

  • Reduced germination
  • Reduced root growth
  • Reduced shoot growth
  • Yield reduction

B. Stimulatory Allelopathy

Chemical substances promote growth and development.

  • Increased germination
  • Enhanced root growth
  • Improved seedling vigor
Board Tip:
Most ALE questions focus on inhibitory allelopathy.

IV. Allelopathy in Weeds

A. Cogon Grass — Imperata cylindrica

Cogon grass releases chemicals that suppress neighboring vegetation.

B. Purple Nutsedge — Cyperus rotundus

Purple nutsedge is known for both strong competition and allelopathic effects.

C. Parthenium Weed — Parthenium hysterophorus

Parthenium is one of the most notorious allelopathic weeds worldwide.

D. Johnson Grass — Sorghum halepense

Johnson grass produces compounds capable of suppressing neighboring plants.

Board Tip:
Parthenium is frequently cited as a strongly allelopathic weed.

V. Allelopathy in Crops

A. Rice

Rice plants may suppress certain weeds through root exudates.

B. Sorghum

Sorghum produces a powerful allelochemical called sorgoleone.

Board Tip:
Sorghum = Sorgoleone

C. Sunflower

Sunflower residues may suppress weed emergence.

D. Rye

Rye is widely used as a cover crop because of its allelopathic properties.

VI. Allelopathy and Sustainable Weed Management

Advantages

  • Reduces herbicide dependence
  • Environmentally friendly
  • Supports sustainable weed management
  • Improves crop productivity by reducing weed pressure

Applications

  • Cover cropping
  • Mulching
  • Crop residue management
  • Crop rotation

VII. Crop-Weed Competition vs Allelopathy

Competition Allelopathy
Competition for resources Chemical interaction
Light, water, nutrients, and space Allelochemicals
Physical struggle Biochemical effect
Resource limitation Chemical inhibition
Board Tip:
Competition = Resource Struggle
Allelopathy = Chemical Suppression

VIII. Advantages of Allelopathy to Weeds

  • Reduces competition from neighboring plants
  • Improves resource availability
  • Increases survival
  • Improves reproductive success
  • Allows dominance in plant communities

IX. Factors Affecting Allelopathic Activity

  • Soil type
  • Soil moisture
  • Temperature
  • Microbial activity
  • Concentration of allelochemicals

X. Agricultural Importance of Allelopathy

  • Improves weed management programs
  • Supports sustainable agriculture systems
  • Reduces herbicide dependence
  • Improves cover crop selection
  • Promotes environmentally friendly farming practices

ALE Quick Facts

  • Allelopathy = chemical interaction between plants.
  • Allelochemicals are responsible for allelopathy.
  • Root exudates commonly contain allelochemicals.
  • Plant residues are major sources of allelochemicals.
  • Allelopathy may inhibit germination.
  • Allelopathy may suppress root growth.
  • Sorghum produces sorgoleone.
  • Competition differs from allelopathy.
  • Most allelopathic effects are inhibitory.

Summary

Allelopathy is the phenomenon in which one plant affects another through the release of chemical substances known as allelochemicals. These chemicals may inhibit seed germination, root growth, shoot development, nutrient uptake, and photosynthesis. Many weeds such as cogon grass, purple nutsedge, parthenium, and Johnson grass exhibit allelopathic effects, while crops such as sorghum, rice, sunflower, and rye also possess allelopathic properties. Allelopathy differs from crop-weed competition because it involves chemical interaction rather than competition for environmental resources. Understanding allelopathy is important for sustainable weed management and effective weed control strategies.
