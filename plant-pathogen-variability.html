🧬 Variability in Plant Pathogens

Crop Protection Reviewer

A. General Concepts

Plant pathogenic microorganisms are not genetically fixed organisms. Like all living things, they continuously change and evolve over time. These changes are called variability.

Variability is important in plant pathology because it affects:

  • Disease severity
  • Ability of pathogens to infect plants
  • Development of new pathogen races
  • Breakdown of resistant plant varieties
  • Effectiveness of disease control measures

The faster microorganisms reproduce, the greater the chance of genetic variation.

Why Pathogens Change Rapidly

1. Short Generation Time

Bacteria can reproduce within minutes, while fungi can produce millions of spores quickly. Because of rapid reproduction:

  • More mutations occur
  • New strains develop faster
  • Adaptation becomes easier

2. Large Population Size

A huge number of pathogen individuals increases the probability of:

  • Mutation
  • Recombination
  • Adaptation to environmental changes

3. Environmental Pressure

Environmental factors force pathogens to adapt.

Examples:

  • Resistant plant varieties
  • Fungicides
  • Antibiotics
  • Climate conditions

This pressure may produce:

  • More aggressive races
  • Fungicide-resistant strains
  • Wider host range pathogens

Terms Used Below the Species Level

1. Biotype

A biotype is a group of organisms within a species that possess identical inheritable traits.

Characteristics

  • Genetically similar
  • Morphologically alike
  • May differ physiologically or behaviorally

Importance

Biotypes help explain:

  • Differences in pathogenicity
  • Resistance to chemicals
  • Adaptation to hosts

2. Pathovar

Pathovar refers to bacterial strains distinguished mainly by the host they infect.

Characteristics

  • Same bacterial species
  • Different host specificity
  • Produce different diseases

Examples:

  • Xanthomonas campestris pv. oryzae infects rice.
  • Xanthomonas campestris pv. vesicatoria infects tomato and pepper.

3. Pathogenic Race

A pathogenic race is a subgroup within a pathogen species capable of attacking specific plant varieties.

Development of Pathogenic Races

Pathogenic races develop through:

  • Mutation
  • Hybridization
  • Selection pressure
  • Sexual recombination

Factors Favoring Development

  • Presence of resistant varieties
  • Narrow host range
  • Sexual reproduction
  • Obligate parasitism

Importance

A resistant variety may become susceptible if a new pathogen race develops.

4. Forma Specialis (f.sp.)

Forma specialis is a special form of a pathogen classified according to host specificity.

Examples:

  • Puccinia graminis f.sp. tritici attacks wheat.
  • Puccinia graminis f.sp. avenae attacks oats.

B. Mechanisms of Variation

Variation occurs naturally through several biological processes.

Sources of Variation in Fungi

1. Heterokaryosis

Heterokaryosis is the presence of genetically different nuclei within the same fungal mycelium.

Importance

  • Produces genetic diversity
  • Creates new fungal strains
  • Alters pathogenicity

Effect

  • Increased virulence
  • Better adaptability
  • Improved survival ability

2. Parasexual Process

A process similar to sexual reproduction but occurring without meiosis.

Steps

  1. Fusion of fungal cells
  2. Exchange of nuclei
  3. Genetic recombination
  4. Formation of new strains

Importance

Produces variation even in fungi lacking true sexual reproduction.

3. Mutation

Mutation is a sudden hereditary change in genetic material.

Causes

  • Radiation
  • Chemicals
  • Environmental stress
  • Natural replication errors

Effects

  • Increase in virulence
  • Decrease in pathogenicity
  • Development of fungicide resistance
  • Alteration of host range

4. Cytoplasmic Variation

Genetic changes may occur through hereditary factors found in the cytoplasm rather than the nucleus.

Importance

  • Affects metabolism
  • Influences virulence
  • May alter resistance

5. Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction creates new gene combinations through recombination.

Importance

  • Increases diversity
  • Produces new races
  • Improves adaptability

Sources of Variation in Bacteria

1. Mutation

Random changes in bacterial DNA.

Importance

  • May produce antibiotic resistance
  • May increase virulence
  • May improve survival ability

2. Transformation

Bacteria absorb DNA fragments from their surroundings and incorporate them into their own cells.

A non-pathogenic bacterium may become pathogenic after acquiring virulence genes.

3. Transduction

Transfer of genetic material through bacteriophages or viruses that infect bacteria.

Importance

  • Spreads virulence genes
  • Spreads resistance genes

4. Conjugation

Direct transfer of genetic material between bacterial cells.

Characteristics

  • Requires cell-to-cell contact
  • Often involves plasmids

Importance

  • Rapid spread of resistance genes
  • Rapid spread of pathogenic traits

Sources of Variation in Nematodes

Nematodes develop variation through mutation, hybridization, and selection pressure.

Importance

New nematode races may overcome resistant crop varieties.

Sources of Variation in Other Pathogens

1. Hybridization

Fusion of genetic materials from different organisms.

2. Recombination

Exchange of genetic material between viruses or other pathogens.

3. Mutation

Common in viruses and viroids because of rapid replication.

Effects of Variation

1. Growth Rate

Some pathogen strains grow faster than others.

2. Longevity

Certain pathogens survive longer in soil or plant debris.

3. Host Range

Some pathogens infect more plant species.

4. Environmental Adaptation

Pathogens may tolerate:

  • Higher temperatures
  • Low moisture
  • Chemical treatments

C. Genetics of Host-Parasite Interactions

Development of infectious plant diseases depends on the interaction between the host plant and the pathogen.

  • The plant may be resistant or susceptible.
  • The pathogen may be virulent or avirulent.

Co-evolution of Hosts and Pathogens

Plants and pathogens evolve together continuously in nature.

If the pathogen becomes too aggressive:

The host plant may die rapidly.

If the plant becomes completely resistant:

The pathogen may disappear.

Therefore, a balance develops between:

  • Host resistance
  • Pathogen virulence

Gene-for-Gene Concept

The gene-for-gene concept explains that for every resistance gene in the host plant, there is a corresponding virulence gene in the pathogen.

If the pathogen lacks the matching virulence gene, the plant remains resistant.

If the pathogen possesses the virulence gene, infection occurs.

Importance of Gene-for-Gene Concept

The concept helps explain:

  • Breakdown of resistant varieties
  • Development of new pathogen races
  • Disease resistance breeding
  • Host specificity

Importance of Studying Variability in Plant Pathogens

Understanding pathogen variability helps in:

  • Breeding resistant varieties
  • Developing durable resistance
  • Improving disease management
  • Predicting emergence of new races
  • Preventing resistance breakdown
  • Designing effective control measures
