🌿 Part 5: Seed Germination and Seedling Growth

Comprehensive Weed Science Reviewer

Introduction

After weed seeds are produced and disseminated, the next critical stage in the weed life cycle is germination and seedling growth.

Seed germination marks the beginning of a new weed plant and plays a major role in weed establishment and survival.

Understanding germination and seedling development helps farmers predict weed emergence and apply weed control measures at the most effective time.

Definition:
Seed germination is the resumption of active growth of the embryo resulting in the development of a seedling.

Importance of Seed Germination

  • Begins the weed life cycle
  • Allows establishment of weed populations
  • Determines weed emergence patterns
  • Influences weed management decisions
  • Affects crop-weed competition

I. Structure of a Seed

1. Seed Coat (Testa)

The seed coat is the protective outer covering of the seed.

It protects the embryo from physical injury, pathogens, and unfavorable environmental conditions.

2. Embryo

The embryo is the young developing plant inside the seed.

It eventually develops into roots, stems, and leaves.

Board Tip:
Embryo = Future Plant

3. Food Reserve

Food reserves provide nutrients needed during germination and early seedling growth.

Food may be stored in:

  • Endosperm
  • Cotyledons

II. Germination Process

Step 1: Imbibition

The dry seed absorbs water and swells.

This is the first step of germination.

Step 2: Enzyme Activation

Water activates enzymes that initiate metabolic activities.

Step 3: Food Mobilization

Stored food reserves are converted into usable nutrients.

Step 4: Radicle Emergence

The radicle or embryonic root emerges first.

Board Tip:
Radicle emerges first during germination.

Step 5: Plumule Emergence

The plumule develops into the shoot system.

Step 6: Seedling Establishment

The young seedling begins independent growth.

III. Requirements for Germination

A. Water

Water is the most important factor initiating germination.

Functions of Water

  • Softens the seed coat
  • Activates enzymes
  • Supports cell expansion
  • Initiates metabolic processes

B. Oxygen

Oxygen is required for respiration.

Respiration provides energy needed for growth.

Board Tip:
Waterlogged soils may reduce oxygen availability and inhibit germination.

C. Temperature

Temperature affects enzyme activity and metabolic processes.

Temperature Categories

  • Minimum Temperature
  • Optimum Temperature
  • Maximum Temperature

D. Light

Some weed seeds require light while others germinate better in darkness.

Positive Photoblastic Seeds

Require light for germination.

Negative Photoblastic Seeds

Require darkness for germination.

Board Tip:
Many small weed seeds require light for germination.

IV. Factors Affecting Germination

Internal Factors

  • Seed viability
  • Seed dormancy
  • Seed age
  • Genetic characteristics

External Factors

  • Moisture
  • Temperature
  • Oxygen
  • Light
  • Soil conditions

V. Seed Dormancy

Definition

Dormancy is a condition in which viable seeds fail to germinate despite favorable environmental conditions.

Importance

Dormancy allows weeds to survive unfavorable periods and contributes greatly to weed persistence.

Dormancy is one of the major reasons weeds continue to appear year after year.

Types of Dormancy

A. Innate Dormancy

Present when seeds are shed from the parent plant.

B. Induced Dormancy

Develops after seed dispersal due to environmental conditions.

C. Enforced Dormancy

Occurs because environmental conditions are unsuitable for germination.

Board Tip:
Dormancy is a survival mechanism, not seed death.

VI. Seedling Growth

Definition

Seedling growth begins immediately after successful germination.

Stages of Seedling Growth

1. Emergence

The seedling appears above the soil surface.

2. Establishment

Roots and shoots continue developing.

3. Early Vegetative Growth

Rapid growth and development occur.

Importance

Young seedlings are generally the most vulnerable stage of weed development.

Board Tip:
Young seedlings are easier to control than mature weeds.

VII. Why Weed Seedlings Are Successful

1. Rapid Growth

Many weeds grow faster than cultivated crops.

2. Efficient Resource Use

Weeds effectively utilize water, nutrients, and sunlight.

3. Adaptability

Weed seedlings can grow under diverse environmental conditions.

4. High Survival Potential

Many weed seedlings successfully establish and reproduce.

VIII. Agricultural Importance

Understanding germination helps determine:

  • Timing of cultivation
  • Timing of herbicide application
  • Weed emergence patterns
  • Critical control periods
  • Integrated weed management strategies

IX. Weed Emergence and Management

Most weed control measures are most effective during germination and early seedling stages.

Reasons

  • Less competition with crops
  • Easier weed control
  • Lower management cost
  • Higher control efficiency
Board Tip:
Early weed control is generally more effective than late weed control.

ALE Quick Facts

  • Germination = resumption of embryo growth
  • Radicle emerges first
  • Water initiates imbibition
  • Oxygen is required for respiration
  • Temperature affects enzyme activity
  • Light influences some weed seeds
  • Dormancy allows weed survival
  • Young seedlings are easier to control
  • Germination begins the weed life cycle

Summary

Seed germination is the process by which a viable seed resumes active growth and develops into a seedling. Germination requires water, oxygen, suitable temperature, and sometimes light. The process begins with imbibition and proceeds through enzyme activation, food mobilization, radicle emergence, plumule emergence, and seedling establishment. Dormancy allows weed seeds to survive unfavorable conditions and contributes greatly to weed persistence. Understanding seed germination and seedling growth is essential for predicting weed emergence and implementing effective weed management strategies.
