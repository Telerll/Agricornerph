🌿 Part 7: Crop-Weed Competition

Comprehensive Weed Science Reviewer

Introduction

Crop-weed competition occurs when crops and weeds require the same limited resources from the environment.

Because crops and weeds occupy the same area, competition develops whenever resources become insufficient to satisfy the needs of both plants.

Definition:
Crop-weed competition is the struggle between crops and weeds for limited environmental resources necessary for growth and development.
Board Tip:
The most important harmful effect of weeds in agriculture is competition with crops.

I. Resources for Which Crops and Weeds Compete

A. Competition for Light

Light is required for photosynthesis. Weeds often grow taller and faster than crops, allowing them to shade crop plants.

Effects

  • Reduced photosynthesis
  • Reduced plant vigor
  • Reduced crop growth
  • Reduced yield
Board Tip:
Tall weeds are strong competitors for sunlight.

B. Competition for Water

Water is essential for photosynthesis, nutrient transport, cell expansion, and metabolic activities.

Many weeds possess extensive root systems that absorb large amounts of soil moisture.

Effects

  • Moisture stress
  • Wilting
  • Reduced crop growth
  • Yield reduction
Board Tip:
Competition for water is especially severe during drought.

C. Competition for Nutrients

Crops and weeds compete for essential plant nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.

Effects

  • Nutrient deficiency
  • Stunted crop growth
  • Reduced productivity
  • Lower yield
Board Tip:
Nitrogen is often one of the most competitive nutrients.

D. Competition for Space

Plants require adequate space for root growth, shoot development, and canopy expansion.

Effects

  • Restricted root growth
  • Reduced canopy development
  • Poor crop stand performance

E. Competition for Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide is needed for photosynthesis. However, competition for carbon dioxide is generally less important than competition for light, water, nutrients, and space.

II. Factors Affecting Crop-Weed Competition

A. Weed Density

Weed density refers to the number of weeds per unit area.

Higher weed populations usually result in greater competition and higher yield loss.

Board Tip:
Yield loss generally increases as weed density increases.

B. Weed Species

Different weed species vary in competitiveness. Some weeds are more aggressive than others because of rapid growth, deep roots, high nutrient uptake, or tall canopy formation.

Highly Competitive Weeds

  • Cogon grass
  • Johnson grass
  • Purple nutsedge
  • Barnyard grass
Board Tip:
Not all weeds cause the same degree of damage.

C. Time of Weed Emergence

Time of weed emergence is one of the most important factors affecting crop-weed competition.

Weeds that emerge earlier or at the same time as the crop usually cause greater yield loss.

Board Tip:
Early weed competition is more damaging than late weed competition.

D. Duration of Competition

The longer weeds remain with crops, the greater the damage.

Effects

  • Increased crop stress
  • Reduced crop growth
  • Lower yield

E. Crop Species

Some crops compete better than others depending on canopy structure, growth rate, root system, and planting density.

Competitive Crops

  • Rice
  • Corn
  • Sorghum

Less Competitive Crops

  • Onion
  • Carrot
  • Garlic
Board Tip:
Crop competitiveness varies among species.

F. Environmental Conditions

Competition becomes more severe when environmental conditions are unfavorable for crop growth.

Examples

  • Limited water
  • Low soil fertility
  • Poor weather conditions
  • Poor soil structure

III. Effects of Crop-Weed Competition

A. Reduced Crop Yield

Yield reduction is the most important consequence of crop-weed competition.

Yield loss may range from minor reductions to complete crop failure depending on the severity and duration of competition.

B. Reduced Crop Quality

  • Smaller grains
  • Poor fruit quality
  • Reduced market value
  • Contaminated produce

C. Delayed Crop Maturity

Competition may slow crop development and delay maturity.

D. Increased Production Costs

  • Additional herbicide cost
  • Additional cultivation
  • Additional labor
  • Increased management expenses

E. Harvesting Difficulties

Dense weed growth may interfere with harvesting operations and reduce harvesting efficiency.

IV. Critical Period of Weed Competition

Definition

The critical period of weed competition is the period during crop growth when weeds must be controlled to prevent unacceptable yield losses.

Important Concept:
Critical Period = Period when weeds must be controlled.

Why It Matters

Controlling weeds before or during the critical period provides maximum yield protection.

General Rule

The first few weeks after crop emergence are usually the most critical because young crops are poor competitors.

Examples

  • Rice: Approximately 15–45 days after planting, depending on production system.
  • Corn: Approximately 2–6 weeks after emergence.
Board Tip:
Young crops are poor competitors, so early weed control is very important.

V. Crop-Weed Competition and Yield Loss

Yield loss depends on several interacting factors.

  • Weed density
  • Weed species
  • Time of weed emergence
  • Duration of competition
  • Crop species
  • Environmental conditions
General Principle:
Greater competition usually results in greater yield loss.

VI. Crop-Weed Competition and Integrated Weed Management

Knowledge of crop-weed competition helps determine the best timing and method of weed control.

It Helps Determine:

  • Timing of herbicide application
  • Timing of cultivation
  • Timing of hand weeding
  • Weed control priorities
  • Need for integrated weed management
Weed management should focus on protecting crops during the critical period of weed competition.

VII. Methods to Reduce Crop-Weed Competition

1. Early Weed Control

Removes weeds before they become highly competitive.

2. Proper Crop Spacing

Allows crops to develop a strong canopy and compete effectively.

3. Higher Crop Population

Helps crops occupy available space and suppress weeds.

4. Mulching

Suppresses weed emergence by blocking light and covering the soil surface.

5. Crop Rotation

Disrupts weed life cycles and reduces buildup of specific weed species.

6. Herbicide Application

Provides effective weed suppression when properly selected and timed.

7. Integrated Weed Management

Combines multiple weed control methods for long-term effectiveness.

VIII. Weed Competition vs Allelopathy

Competition and allelopathy are different concepts and are often confused in board examinations.

Competition

Competition occurs when crops and weeds struggle for limited resources.

  • Light
  • Water
  • Nutrients
  • Space

Allelopathy

Allelopathy occurs when one plant releases chemicals that affect the growth or germination of another plant.

Board Tip:
Competition = Resource Struggle
Allelopathy = Chemical Interaction

  • Competition differs from allelopathy.

Summary

Crop-weed competition is the struggle between crops and weeds for limited environmental resources such as light, water, nutrients, space, and carbon dioxide. It is the most important harmful effect of weeds because it directly reduces crop growth, yield, and quality. The severity of competition depends on weed density, weed species, emergence timing, duration of competition, crop species, and environmental conditions. The critical period of weed competition is the stage when weeds must be controlled to prevent significant yield loss. Understanding crop-weed competition is fundamental to effective weed management and is one of the most important topics in Weed Science and the AgLE Board Examination.
