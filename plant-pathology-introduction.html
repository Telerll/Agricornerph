🦠 Introduction to Plant Pathology

Crop Protection Reviewer

1. Etymology

  • Pathos = suffering
  • Logos = study

Plant Pathology literally means the study of plant suffering or plant diseases.

2. Definition of Plant Pathology

Plant pathology or phytopathology is the branch of science that studies plant diseases, their causes, development, spread, prevention, and control.

  • Study of plant diseases
  • Causes of diseases
  • Development of diseases
  • Methods of prevention and control
  • Reduction of crop losses

3. Science and Art of Plant Pathology

As a Science

  • Study of pathogens and diseases
  • Plant-pathogen interaction
  • Disease development
  • Disease control principles

As an Art

  • Disease diagnosis
  • Disease assessment
  • Disease forecasting
  • Application of disease control methods

4. Objectives of Plant Pathology

  • Prevent plant diseases
  • Reduce crop losses
  • Increase food production
  • Maintain crop quality
  • Protect food supply and farmer income

5. Economic Importance of Plant Diseases

Plant diseases cause serious economic losses worldwide. They affect crop yield, crop quality, food supply, and farmer income.

Examples of Major Plant Diseases

  • Potato Late Blight – caused famine in Ireland
  • Coffee Rust – destroyed coffee plantations
  • Cadang-cadang Disease – affected coconut industry
  • Rice Tungro Disease – caused severe rice losses

6. Types of Crop Losses

Reduction in Yield

This refers to the decrease in the amount of harvest produced by crops because of plant diseases.

  • Diseased leaves reduce photosynthesis
  • Damaged roots reduce nutrient absorption
  • Fruit diseases lower harvestable produce

Result: lower crop production and lower farmer income.

Reduction in Crop Quality

Plant diseases reduce the appearance, texture, size, taste, and market value of crops.

  • Discolored grains
  • Deformed fruits
  • Moldy seeds
  • Poor seed germination

Result: lower selling price and rejection in the market.

Storage and Transport Losses

Losses may continue even after harvest while products are stored or transported.

  • Fruit rotting during transport
  • Mold growth in storage
  • Soft rot in vegetables
  • Spoilage caused by poor storage conditions

Result: wastage of agricultural products and economic losses.

Losses Due to Toxins Produced by Pathogens

Some fungi produce poisonous substances called mycotoxins which contaminate food and feeds.

  • Aflatoxin – causes liver cancer
  • Ochratoxin – damages liver and kidneys
  • Fumonisins – harmful to humans and livestock

Result: unsafe food supply and rejection of contaminated crops.

Increase in Production Costs

Plant diseases force farmers to spend additional money for disease prevention and management.

  • Purchase of fungicides and pesticides
  • Additional labor costs
  • Use of resistant varieties
  • Extra postharvest handling expenses

Result: reduced farmer profit and increased production expenses.
