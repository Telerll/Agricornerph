Animal Genetics

Animal Genetics

Animal genetics is the branch of animal science that studies heredity, inheritance, variation, chromosomes, gene action, and the transmission of traits in animals.

It explains how traits are passed from parents to offspring, why offspring resemble their parents, why animals of the same species still differ from one another, and how genes influence animal performance and productivity.

Animal genetics is the scientific foundation of animal breeding, livestock improvement, selection, and genetic progress.

Animal genetics = study of heredity, inheritance, variation, chromosomes, and gene action in animals.

Importance of Animal Genetics

Animal genetics is important because it explains the biological reason why animals inherit certain traits and why they differ in performance.

It helps animal scientists, breeders, and producers understand how to improve animals for production, reproduction, health, and adaptability.

  • Explains how traits are inherited
  • Explains why offspring resemble parents
  • Explains variation among animals
  • Helps pass desirable traits to offspring
  • Helps reduce undesirable traits
  • Supports animal breeding and livestock improvement
  • Improves productivity and profitability
Animal genetics is the scientific foundation of animal improvement.

Genetics

Genetics is the science of heredity and variation.

It studies how traits are passed from one generation to the next and why individuals differ from one another even if they belong to the same species.

In animal science, genetics is used to understand and improve economically important traits such as growth rate, milk yield, egg production, fertility, disease resistance, and carcass quality.

Genetics = study of heredity and variation.

Heredity

Heredity is the biological transmission of traits from parents to offspring through genes.

It explains why offspring may resemble their sire and dam in physical appearance, production ability, temperament, and other inherited characteristics.

Examples of inherited traits include coat color, horn presence, body size, growth potential, milk yield potential, and egg-laying ability.

Heredity = transmission of traits from parents to offspring.

Variation

Variation is the difference in traits among individuals of the same species.

No two animals are exactly alike because individuals differ in genes, environment, or both.

Variation is important because selection is only possible when animals differ from one another. If there is no variation, there is nothing to select and improve.

Variation = differences among individuals.

Genetic Variation

Genetic variation is variation caused by differences in genes.

This type of variation is inherited and can be passed from parents to offspring.

Genetic variation is important in breeding because it allows improvement through selection. Animals with superior genes may be selected as parents of the next generation.

Genetic variation = inherited variation.

Environmental Variation

Environmental variation is variation caused by non-genetic factors.

It affects the animal’s performance but is not directly inherited by the offspring.

Examples of environmental factors include nutrition, housing, disease, climate, temperature, sanitation, management, and health care.

Environmental variation = non-inherited variation caused by environment and management.

Gene

A gene is the basic unit of heredity.

It is a segment of DNA that carries instructions for a specific trait or biological function.

Genes influence characteristics such as coat color, growth rate, milk production, body size, fertility, and disease resistance.

Gene = basic unit of heredity.

DNA

DNA means Deoxyribonucleic Acid.

DNA is the hereditary material found in cells. It carries the genetic instructions needed for growth, development, reproduction, metabolism, and inheritance.

DNA is often called the genetic blueprint of life because it contains the coded information that determines how an organism develops and functions.

DNA = hereditary material / genetic blueprint.

Structure of DNA

DNA is made of smaller units called nucleotides.

Each nucleotide contains a sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogen base.

The four nitrogen bases are:

  • Adenine (A)
  • Thymine (T)
  • Guanine (G)
  • Cytosine (C)

The base-pairing rule is:

  • A pairs with T
  • G pairs with C
DNA = double helix with A-T and G-C base pairing.

Chromosome

A chromosome is a thread-like structure made of DNA and proteins.

Chromosomes are found in the nucleus of cells and carry genes.

Because chromosomes carry genes, they are important in inheritance, sex determination, and genetic variation.

Chromosome = structure that carries genes.

Homologous Chromosomes

Homologous chromosomes are matching chromosome pairs that carry the same type of genes at the same locations.

One chromosome of the pair comes from the sire, and the other comes from the dam.

This pairing is important because offspring inherit one set of chromosomes from each parent.

Homologous chromosomes = matching chromosome pair from sire and dam.

Genome

The genome is the complete set of genetic material in an organism.

It includes all DNA, genes, and genetic instructions found in the animal.

Genomics, a modern field of genetics, studies the whole genome to improve breeding decisions.

Genome = complete genetic makeup of an organism.

Locus

A locus is the exact position or location of a gene on a chromosome.

Genes are located at specific loci. The plural form of locus is loci.

Locus = gene position on chromosome.

Allele

An allele is an alternative form of a gene found at the same locus.

For example, one allele may code for black coat color while another allele may code for red coat color.

An animal usually receives one allele from the sire and one allele from the dam.

Allele = alternative form of a gene.

Genotype

Genotype is the genetic makeup of an animal.

It refers to the actual combination of genes or alleles that an animal possesses.

Examples of genotype symbols include BB, Bb, or bb.

Genotype = genetic makeup.

Phenotype

Phenotype is the observable or measurable expression of a trait.

It is what can be seen, measured, or evaluated in the animal.

Examples include coat color, body weight, milk yield, egg production, litter size, and growth rate.

Phenotype = observable or measurable trait.

Genotype vs Phenotype

Genotype refers to the inherited genetic makeup of an animal.

Phenotype refers to the visible or measurable expression of that genetic makeup.

Phenotype is affected by both genotype and environment.

Genotype is inherited; phenotype is expressed and may be influenced by environment.

Dominant Gene

A dominant gene or dominant allele is expressed even when only one copy is present.

This means the dominant allele can mask the effect of a recessive allele in a heterozygous animal.

Example: if B is dominant over b, then both BB and Bb may show the dominant trait.

Dominant = expressed even in heterozygous condition.

Recessive Gene

A recessive gene or recessive allele is expressed only when both alleles are recessive.

This means the recessive trait appears only in the homozygous recessive condition.

Example: bb expresses the recessive trait.

Recessive = expressed only in homozygous condition.

Homozygous

Homozygous means the two alleles for a trait are the same.

Examples include BB and bb.

Homozygous animals may be homozygous dominant or homozygous recessive.

Homozygous = same alleles.

Heterozygous

Heterozygous means the two alleles for a trait are different.

Example: Bb.

In simple dominance, the dominant allele is expressed in the heterozygous condition.

Heterozygous = different alleles.

Gregor Mendel

Gregor Mendel is known as the Father of Genetics.

He discovered the basic principles of inheritance through experiments with pea plants.

His work became the foundation of Mendelian genetics and the study of heredity.

Gregor Mendel = Father of Genetics.

Mendelian Genetics

Mendelian genetics refers to inheritance patterns that follow Mendel’s laws.

These patterns usually involve traits controlled by one gene with dominant and recessive alleles.

Mendelian genetics is important because it explains simple inheritance patterns such as dominant, recessive, homozygous, and heterozygous traits.

Mendelian genetics = simple inheritance following Mendel’s laws.

Law of Segregation

The Law of Segregation states that the two alleles of a gene separate during gamete formation.

Each gamete receives only one allele from each pair.

During fertilization, one allele from the sire and one allele from the dam combine to form the offspring’s genotype.

Law of Segregation = allele separation during gamete formation.

Law of Independent Assortment

The Law of Independent Assortment states that genes for different traits assort independently during gamete formation.

This means inheritance of one trait does not always affect the inheritance of another trait, especially when genes are located on different chromosomes.

Independent assortment = different traits assort independently.

Non-Mendelian Genetics

Non-Mendelian genetics refers to inheritance patterns that do not strictly follow simple Mendelian dominant-recessive inheritance.

Many animal traits are more complex than simple single-gene inheritance.

Non-Mendelian inheritance includes incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, sex-linked inheritance, sex-limited traits, polygenic traits, pleiotropy, epistasis, maternal effect, cytoplasmic inheritance, imprinting, penetrance, and expressivity.

Non-Mendelian genetics = inheritance beyond simple Mendelian rules.

Incomplete Dominance

Incomplete dominance occurs when neither allele completely masks the other.

The result is an intermediate or blended phenotype.

Example: red crossed with white may produce an intermediate color in some organisms.

Incomplete dominance = blended phenotype.

Codominance

Codominance occurs when both alleles are fully expressed in the phenotype.

Neither allele masks the other.

A common animal example is roan coat color in cattle, where both red and white hairs are expressed.

Codominance = both alleles are expressed equally.

Multiple Alleles

Multiple alleles occur when more than two forms of a gene exist in a population.

An individual animal still carries only two alleles for a gene, but the population may contain many different allele forms.

Blood group inheritance is a common example of multiple alleles.

Multiple alleles = more than two gene forms in a population.

Lethal Genes

Lethal genes are genes that cause death when present in certain genotypes.

They may be dominant lethal or recessive lethal.

In animal breeding, lethal genes are important because they may reduce survivability, fertility, and productivity.

Lethal genes = genes that cause death in certain genotypes.

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Sex-linked inheritance involves genes located on the sex chromosomes.

These traits may be inherited differently in males and females because males and females have different sex chromosome combinations.

Sex-linked traits are commonly associated with genes on the X chromosome in mammals.

Sex-linked inheritance = gene located on sex chromosome.

Sex-Limited Traits

Sex-limited traits are traits expressed only in one sex, although both sexes may carry the genes.

Examples include milk production in females and egg production in hens.

A male may carry genes for milk production but cannot express milk yield as a phenotype.

Sex-limited traits = expressed in only one sex.

Sex-Influenced Traits

Sex-influenced traits are traits expressed differently in males and females because of hormonal differences.

The same genotype may show different expression depending on the sex of the animal.

Horn development in some sheep breeds is a common example.

Sex-influenced traits = expression affected by sex hormones.

Polygenic Inheritance

Polygenic inheritance occurs when one trait is controlled by many genes.

Most economically important livestock traits are polygenic.

Examples include growth rate, milk yield, egg production, litter size, body weight, and carcass traits.

Polygenic = many genes control one trait.

Pleiotropy

Pleiotropy occurs when one gene affects several traits.

This means a single gene may influence multiple body functions or characteristics.

Pleiotropy is important in animal breeding because selecting for one trait may indirectly affect another trait.

Pleiotropy = one gene affects many traits.

Epistasis

Epistasis occurs when one gene masks or modifies the effect of another gene at a different locus.

It is a type of gene interaction where one gene affects the expression of another gene.

This can make inheritance patterns more complex than simple dominance.

Epistasis = one gene masks or modifies another gene.

Maternal Effect

Maternal effect occurs when the phenotype of the offspring is influenced by the genotype or environment of the mother.

Examples include birth weight, egg size, early growth, milk supply, and maternal immunity.

In livestock production, maternal effects are important in early-life performance of offspring.

Maternal effect = mother influences offspring phenotype.

Cytoplasmic Inheritance

Cytoplasmic inheritance is inheritance through genetic material outside the nucleus.

It usually involves mitochondrial DNA.

Because mitochondria are generally inherited from the mother, cytoplasmic inheritance is commonly maternal in pattern.

Cytoplasmic inheritance = extranuclear inheritance, usually through mitochondria.

Genomic Imprinting

Genomic imprinting occurs when gene expression depends on whether the gene was inherited from the sire or the dam.

This is also called a parent-of-origin effect.

In imprinting, the same gene may behave differently depending on which parent transmitted it.

Genomic imprinting = parent-of-origin effect.

Penetrance

Penetrance is the proportion of individuals with a genotype who actually express the expected phenotype.

If all animals with a certain genotype show the trait, penetrance is complete.

If only some animals show the trait, penetrance is incomplete.

Penetrance = proportion of individuals expressing the trait.

Expressivity

Expressivity is the degree or intensity with which a genotype is expressed in the phenotype.

Two animals may have the same genotype but show different levels of trait expression.

This is important in traits that vary in severity or intensity.

Expressivity = degree or intensity of expression.

Sex Chromosomes

Sex chromosomes are chromosomes that determine the sex of an animal.

They differ from autosomes because they are specifically involved in sex determination.

Sex chromosomes vary depending on the animal group. Mammals generally use the XX-XY system, while birds use the ZZ-ZW system.

Sex chromosomes = chromosomes that determine sex.

Autosomes

Autosomes are chromosomes that are not involved in sex determination.

They carry genes for most body traits such as growth, body structure, production, metabolism, and many physical characteristics.

Autosomes = non-sex chromosomes.

XX–XY System

The XX–XY system is the common sex determination system in mammals.

In this system:

  • Female = XX
  • Male = XY

The male determines the sex of the offspring because the male can contribute either an X chromosome or a Y chromosome, while the female contributes only X.

Mammals = female XX, male XY; male determines sex.

ZZ–ZW System

The ZZ–ZW system is the common sex determination system in birds.

In this system:

  • Male = ZZ
  • Female = ZW

The female determines the sex of the offspring because the female can contribute either a Z chromosome or a W chromosome, while the male contributes only Z.

Birds = male ZZ, female ZW; female determines sex.

Chromosome Number in Farm Animals

Chromosome number refers to the total number of chromosomes found in the body cells of an animal.

The diploid chromosome number is written as 2n. This means the chromosomes are in pairs, with one set from the sire and one set from the dam.

This is a common board exam recall topic, especially for cattle, carabao, goat, sheep, swine, horse, chicken, duck, turkey, and quail.

2n = diploid chromosome number in body cells.

Qualitative Traits

Qualitative traits are traits controlled by one or a few genes.

They are usually expressed in distinct categories rather than measured on a continuous scale.

Examples include coat color, horned or polled condition, and some blood types.

Qualitative traits = simple categorical traits controlled by one or few genes.

Quantitative Traits

Quantitative traits are traits controlled by many genes and strongly influenced by environment.

They are measured numerically and usually show continuous variation.

Examples include growth rate, milk yield, egg production, litter size, body weight, feed efficiency, and carcass quality.

Quantitative traits = measurable production traits controlled by many genes and environment.

Heritability

Heritability is the proportion of observed variation in a trait that is due to genetic differences among animals.

High heritability means a trait is strongly influenced by genetics and responds well to selection.

Low heritability means the trait is strongly affected by environment and may improve more slowly through selection.

Heritability = degree of genetic control of a trait.

Repeatability

Repeatability is the consistency of repeated records of the same trait in the same animal.

It is useful for traits measured more than once, such as milk yield, litter size, or egg production.

High repeatability means an animal’s future performance is likely to be similar to its past performance.

Repeatability = consistency of repeated performance.

Population Genetics

Population genetics is the study of genes and allele frequencies in a population.

It explains how genetic composition changes in a group of animals over time.

It is important in breeding programs because selection changes gene frequencies across generations.

Population genetics = genetics of groups or populations.

Gene Frequency

Gene frequency or allele frequency is the proportion of a particular allele in a population.

It shows how common or rare an allele is within a group of animals.

Selection, mutation, migration, and genetic drift can change gene frequencies.

Gene frequency = proportion of an allele in a population.

Genotype Frequency

Genotype frequency is the proportion of a particular genotype in a population.

For example, it may refer to the proportion of animals that are BB, Bb, or bb.

Genotype frequency = proportion of a genotype in a population.

Cytogenetics

Cytogenetics is the study of chromosomes and chromosomal abnormalities.

It connects cytology, the study of cells, with genetics.

Cytogenetics is important for identifying chromosome number, sex chromosome systems, and chromosomal defects.

Cytogenetics = chromosome genetics.

Mutation

A mutation is a sudden heritable change in genetic material.

Mutations may occur naturally or may be caused by environmental factors.

Some mutations are harmful, some are neutral, and some may be beneficial.

Mutation = sudden heritable change in DNA.

Chromosomal Abnormalities

Chromosomal abnormalities are changes in chromosome number or chromosome structure.

These abnormalities may affect fertility, development, survival, and productivity.

Examples include deletion, duplication, inversion, and translocation.

Chromosomal abnormalities = chromosome defects in number or structure.

Deletion

Deletion is a chromosomal abnormality where a segment of a chromosome is lost.

This may remove important genes and may cause abnormal development or reduced performance.

Deletion = loss of chromosome segment.

Duplication

Duplication is a chromosomal abnormality where a chromosome segment is repeated.

This results in extra copies of some genes.

Duplication = repeated chromosome segment.

Inversion

Inversion occurs when a chromosome segment breaks and reattaches in reverse order.

This changes the arrangement of genes within the chromosome.

Inversion = reversed chromosome segment.

Translocation

Translocation occurs when a chromosome segment moves to another chromosome.

This may disrupt normal gene function and may affect fertility or development.

Translocation = chromosome segment moves to another chromosome.
