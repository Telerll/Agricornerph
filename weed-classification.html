🌾 Part 2: Classification of Weeds

Comprehensive Weed Science Reviewer

Introduction

Proper weed management begins with proper weed classification. Classification helps in weed identification, understanding weed biology, selecting appropriate control methods, recommending herbicides, and predicting weed behavior.

Different weed groups differ in growth habit, reproduction, life cycle, habitat, and response to control measures.

Definition of Weed Classification

Weed classification is the process of grouping weeds according to common characteristics.

  • Life cycle
  • Morphology
  • Habitat
  • Reproduction
  • Origin
  • Growth habit

Importance of Weed Classification

  • Helps identify weeds correctly.
  • Helps understand weed biology.
  • Improves weed control decisions.
  • Reduces herbicide misuse.
  • Helps determine the best timing of control.
  • Supports effective Integrated Weed Management.

Classification According to Life Cycle

Life cycle refers to the length of time required for a weed to complete its growth, reproduction, and death.

I. Annual Weeds

Annual weeds complete their entire life cycle within one growing season or one year.

Life Cycle: Seed → Germination → Growth → Flowering → Seed Production → Death

Characteristics

  • Depend mainly on seeds for reproduction.
  • Grow rapidly.
  • Produce large quantities of seeds.
  • Die after seed production.
  • Usually aggressive during early crop growth.

Examples

  • Goosegrass — Eleusine indica
  • Barnyard grass — Echinochloa crus-galli
  • Pigweed — Amaranthus spinosus
  • Purslane — Portulaca oleracea

Board Tip: Annual weeds survive unfavorable conditions mainly through seeds.

II. Biennial Weeds

Biennial weeds require two growing seasons to complete their life cycle.

First Year

  • Vegetative growth only
  • Development of roots, leaves, and stems
  • Food storage

Second Year

  • Flowering
  • Seed production
  • Death after reproduction

Examples

  • Wild carrot — Daucus carota
  • Bull thistle — Cirsium vulgare

Board Tip: Biennials complete their life cycle in two years and are less common in tropical agriculture.

III. Perennial Weeds

Perennial weeds live for more than two years.

Characteristics

  • Long-lived.
  • Difficult to control.
  • May reproduce by seeds and vegetative structures.
  • Can regrow after cutting, cultivation, or burning.
  • Often survive through underground storage organs.

Vegetative Structures

  • Rhizomes
  • Stolons
  • Tubers
  • Bulbs
  • Rootstocks

Examples

  • Purple nutsedge — Cyperus rotundus
  • Cogon grass — Imperata cylindrica
  • Johnson grass — Sorghum halepense

Board Tip: Perennial weeds are usually the most difficult to control because they survive through underground vegetative structures.

Comparison of Life Cycle Groups

Characteristic Annual Biennial Perennial
Life Span Less than 1 year 2 years More than 2 years
Main Survival Structure Seed Root and seed Seeds and vegetative parts
Difficulty of Control Relatively easy Moderate Difficult
Regrowth Ability Low Moderate High

Classification According to Morphology

Morphological classification is one of the most important classification systems in Weed Science because it is used in field identification and herbicide selection.

  • Grasses
  • Sedges
  • Broadleaf weeds

I. Grasses

Grasses belong to the family Poaceae, formerly called Gramineae.

Characteristics

  • Stem is usually round.
  • Stem is hollow between nodes.
  • Stem is jointed.
  • Leaves are narrow.
  • Leaf veins are parallel.
  • Root system is fibrous.
  • Growth habit is usually tufted.

Examples

  • Barnyard grass — Echinochloa crus-galli
  • Jungle rice — Echinochloa colona
  • Goosegrass — Eleusine indica
  • Cogon grass — Imperata cylindrica

Board Tip: Grasses generally have round stems, hollow internodes, narrow leaves, and parallel venation.

II. Sedges

Sedges belong to the family Cyperaceae.

Characteristics

  • Stem is triangular.
  • Stem is usually solid.
  • Stem has three sides.
  • Leaves are commonly arranged in three rows.
  • Root system is fibrous.

Examples

  • Purple nutsedge — Cyperus rotundus
  • Rice flatsedge — Cyperus iria
  • Smallflower umbrella sedge — Cyperus difformis

Famous Board Exam Rule: “Sedges have edges.” This means sedges have triangular stems.

III. Broadleaf Weeds

Broadleaf weeds are weeds with broad leaves and net-like venation.

Characteristics

  • Leaves are broad.
  • Leaf venation is reticulate or net-like.
  • Stem is usually solid.
  • Root system is usually taproot.
  • Many belong to dicotyledonous plants.

Examples

  • Pigweed — Amaranthus spinosus
  • Purslane — Portulaca oleracea
  • Coat buttons — Tridax procumbens

Board Tip: Broadleaf weeds usually have broad leaves, reticulate venation, and taproot system.

Comparison of Grasses, Sedges, and Broadleaves

Characteristic Grasses Sedges Broadleaves
Stem Shape Round Triangular Usually round or solid
Internodes Hollow Solid Solid
Leaf Veins Parallel Parallel Reticulate
Root System Fibrous Fibrous Usually taproot
Example Goosegrass Purple nutsedge Pigweed

Classification According to Habitat

Habitat refers to the place or environment where weeds grow.

I. Terrestrial Weeds

Terrestrial weeds grow on land.

Examples

  • Cogon grass
  • Goosegrass
  • Pigweed
  • Purple nutsedge

II. Aquatic Weeds

Aquatic weeds grow in water or saturated areas.

Examples

  • Water hyacinth — Eichhornia crassipes
  • Water lettuce — Pistia stratiotes
  • Duckweed — Lemna spp.

Importance of Aquatic Weeds

  • Interfere with irrigation canals.
  • Block waterways.
  • Affect fisheries.
  • Interfere with rice production.
  • Reduce water flow and oxygen levels.

Classification According to Origin

I. Native Weeds

Native weeds are weeds naturally occurring in a region.

II. Introduced Weeds

Introduced weeds are weeds brought from other countries or regions. Some introduced weeds become invasive because they lack natural enemies in the new area.

Example: Water hyacinth was introduced but later became an invasive aquatic weed.

Classification According to Growth Habit

I. Erect Weeds

Erect weeds grow upright.

  • Pigweed
  • Spiny amaranth

II. Creeping Weeds

Creeping weeds spread horizontally along the soil surface.

  • Purslane
  • Some grasses with stolons

III. Climbing Weeds

Climbing weeds climb using stems, tendrils, or twining growth.

  • Morning glory
  • Other vine-type weeds

Importance of Weed Classification in Management

Weed classification is important because management methods differ depending on the type of weed.

  • Annual weeds are often controlled before seed production.
  • Perennial weeds require control of underground vegetative structures.
  • Grasses, sedges, and broadleaves may require different herbicides.
  • Aquatic weeds need different management compared with terrestrial weeds.
  • Correct classification prevents wrong herbicide selection.

ALE Quick Facts

  • Annual weeds complete life cycle in one year.
  • Biennial weeds complete life cycle in two years.
  • Perennial weeds live for more than two years.
  • Grasses belong to Poaceae.
  • Sedges belong to Cyperaceae.
  • Broadleaf weeds have broad leaves and reticulate veins.
  • Purple nutsedge is a sedge.
  • Cogon grass is a perennial grass.
  • Water hyacinth is an aquatic weed.
  • Perennial weeds are usually the hardest to control.

ALE High-Yield Exam Tips

Board Trap #1

“Sedges have edges.” A triangular stem usually indicates a sedge.

Board Trap #2

Grasses usually have round stems, hollow internodes, and parallel veins.

Board Trap #3

Broadleaf weeds usually have broad leaves and reticulate venation.

Board Trap #4

Purple nutsedge, Cyperus rotundus, is a sedge, not a grass.

Board Trap #5

Perennial weeds are difficult to control because they possess underground vegetative structures such as rhizomes, stolons, tubers, bulbs, and rootstocks.

Summary

Weed classification groups weeds according to life cycle, morphology, habitat, origin, and growth habit. Based on life cycle, weeds may be annual, biennial, or perennial. Based on morphology, weeds are classified as grasses, sedges, or broadleaves. Sedges are distinguished by triangular stems, grasses by round and usually hollow stems, and broadleaf weeds by broad leaves and reticulate venation. Proper classification is essential for weed identification, herbicide selection, and effective weed management.
