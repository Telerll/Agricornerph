Parasitic or Biotic Agents of Plant Diseases

Definition

Parasitic or biotic agents are living organisms that cause infectious diseases in plants. These organisms invade plant tissues, obtain nutrients from the host, and interfere with normal plant growth and development.

Major Biotic Agents

  • Viruses
  • Viroids
  • Bacteria
  • Mollicutes
  • Fastidious vascular bacteria
  • Plant pathogenic protists
  • Nematodes
  • Fungi
  • Parasitic higher plants

A. Viruses

Virus Characteristics

Viruses are extremely small infectious particles that can only multiply inside living host cells. They are obligate parasites because they cannot survive or reproduce independently.

Important Characteristics

  • Submicroscopic organisms
  • Contain either RNA or DNA
  • Covered by a protein coat called capsid
  • Lack cellular structures and respiratory machinery
  • Depend entirely on host cells for multiplication

Components of Viruses

1. Protein Coat or Capsid

Functions:

  • Protects the nucleic acid
  • Helps movement between plant cells
  • Determines type of symptoms
  • Assists transmission

2. Nucleic Acid

  • Infective part of the virus
  • Contains genetic information
  • May be RNA or DNA
  • Most plant viruses contain single-stranded RNA

Common Symptoms of Viral Infection

1. Mosaic

Alternating patches of green and yellow coloration on leaves.

2. Ring Spot

Circular chlorotic or necrotic rings on leaves and fruits.

3. Witches’ Broom

Excessive branching causing broom-like appearance.

4. Vein Clearing

Leaf veins become transparent or pale.

5. Color Breaking

Irregular color development in flowers or fruits.

6. Stunting

Reduced plant growth and dwarfing.

7. Chlorosis

Yellowing due to lack of chlorophyll.

8. Leaf Curling

Abnormal curling or distortion of leaves.

Entry Into Plants

Viruses enter plants through:

  • Wounds
  • Insect feeding injuries
  • Grafting
  • Infected pollen
  • Mechanical damage

Means of Transmission

1. Infected Seeds and Planting Materials

Virus spreads through infected propagative materials.

2. Grafting

Virus transfers from infected stock to healthy plant.

3. Mechanical Transmission

Spread through contaminated tools, hands, and equipment.

4. Vectors

Common vectors:

  • Insects
  • Mites
  • Nematodes
  • Soil-borne fungi

Virus-Vector Relationship

1. Non-persistent

Virus transmitted quickly and retained briefly.

2. Semi-persistent

Virus retained for several hours or days.

3. Persistent

Virus remains inside vector for long periods.

Types of persistent transmission:

  • Stylet-borne
  • Circulative
  • Propagative

Methods of Identifying Viruses

  • Indicator host testing
  • Electron microscopy
  • Serological tests such as ELISA
  • Symptom observation
  • Host range studies

Control of Virus Diseases

Preventive Measures

  • Quarantine
  • Use virus-free seeds
  • Certification programs

Eradicative Measures

  • Rogueing infected plants
  • Burning infected materials
  • Hot water treatment

Protective Measures

  • Vector control
  • Cross protection

Other Measures

  • Genetic engineering
  • Early detection and destruction

B. Viroids

Viroid Characteristics

Viroids are very small infectious RNA particles lacking protein coats.

Characteristics

  • Naked RNA molecules
  • Smaller than viruses
  • Infect plant cells
  • Associated with cell nuclei

Symptoms of Viroid-Infected Plants

  • Yellowing
  • Stunting
  • Leaf twisting
  • Mottling
  • Bark cracking

Transmission and Spread

  • Contaminated tools
  • Vegetative propagation
  • Mechanical transmission
  • Insects

Control of Viroids

  • Tool disinfection
  • Use viroid-free materials
  • Early destruction of infected plants

C. Bacteria

General Characteristics

Bacteria are microscopic single-celled organisms lacking a true nucleus.

Characteristics

  • Prokaryotic
  • One-celled
  • Reproduce by binary fission
  • Have rigid cell walls

Gene Transfer in Bacteria

1. Transformation

Uptake of genetic material from surroundings.

2. Transduction

Transfer through bacteriophages.

3. Conjugation

Direct transfer between bacterial cells.

Shapes of Bacteria

  • Cocci – spherical
  • Bacilli – rod-shaped
  • Spirilla – spiral-shaped

Symptoms Caused by Bacteria

1. Leaf Spot

Localized dead spots on leaves.

2. Soft Rot

Soft watery decay caused by enzymes.

3. Blight

Rapid death of plant tissues.

4. Gall

Abnormal swelling of tissues.

5. Canker

Sunken lesions on stems or branches.

6. Wilting

Loss of water movement in plants.

7. Scab

Rough corky lesions.

8. Chlorosis

Yellowing of leaves.

9. Streak

Long narrow lesions.

Control of Bacterial Diseases

Cultural Control

  • Sanitation
  • Crop rotation
  • Proper drainage

Seed Treatment

Use disinfectants before planting.

Chemical Control

Use antibiotics carefully.

Resistant Cultivars

Use resistant plant varieties.

Examples of Bacterial Plant Pathogens and Diseases in the Philippines

1. Acetobacter aceti

  • Causes: Pink disease of pineapple

2. Agrobacterium tumefaciens

  • Causes: Crown gall of roses
  • Produces tumor-like swellings on stems and roots.

3. Clavibacter xyli subsp. xyli

  • Causes: Ratoon stunting disease of sugarcane
  • Leads to stunted growth and reduced cane yield.

4. Enterobacter dissolvens or Erwinia dissolvens

  • Causes: Stalk rot of corn
  • Rotting and weakening of corn stalks.

5. Erwinia tracheiphila

  • Causes: Bacterial wilt of cucurbits
  • Common in cucumber, squash, and melon.

6. Gluconobacter oxydans

  • Causes: Pink disease of pineapple

7. Pantoea stewartii subsp. stewartii

  • Causes: Bacterial wilt of corn

Important Soft Rot and Wilt Pathogens

8. Pectobacterium carotovorum

Diseases:

  • Potato black leg
  • Soft rot of vegetables

Symptoms:

  • Soft watery decay
  • Foul odor
  • Tissue collapse

9. Ralstonia solanacearum

Causes:

  • Bacterial wilt of solanaceous crops

Affected crops:

  • Tomato
  • Eggplant
  • Pepper
  • Potato

Symptoms:

  • Sudden wilting
  • Browning of vascular tissues
  • Plant death

Streptomyces Diseases

10. Streptomyces scabies

  • Causes: Potato scab
  • Rough corky lesions on potato tubers.

11. Streptomyces albilineans

  • Causes: Red stripe of sugarcane

Xanthomonas Diseases

12. Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. citri

  • Causes: Citrus canker
  • Produces raised corky lesions on citrus.

13. Xanthomonas campestris pv. dieffenbachiae

  • Causes: Bacterial blight of anthurium

14. Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. manihotis

  • Causes: Cassava leaf blight

15. Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. phaseoli

  • Causes: Blight of lima bean

16. Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. vignicola

  • Causes: Leaf spot of cowpea

17. Xanthomonas campestris pv. campestris

  • Causes: Black rot of crucifers
  • Affects cabbage and related crops.

18. Xanthomonas campestris pv. vesicatoria

  • Causes: Leaf spot of tomato and pepper

19. Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae

  • Causes: Bacterial leaf blight of rice
  • One of the most important bacterial diseases of rice.

Other Important Bacterial Diseases

20. Pseudomonas syringae pv. tabaci

  • Causes: Tobacco wildfire disease
  • Produces necrotic lesions surrounded by yellow halos.

21. Pseudomonas avastoni subsp. phaseolicola

  • Causes: Halo blight of beans

D. Mollicutes

Mollicutes Characteristics

Mollicutes are wall-less bacteria-like organisms.

Characteristics

  • No cell wall
  • Pleomorphic shape
  • Contain RNA and DNA
  • Found in phloem tissues

Common Symptoms

  • Yellows
  • Virescence
  • Stunting
  • Phyllody
  • Bunchy growth

Classes of Mollicutes

1. Phytoplasmas

  • Previously called MLOs
  • Found in phloem
  • Difficult to culture

2. Spiroplasmas

  • Spiral-shaped
  • Easier to grow
  • Require sterols

Transmission

Mainly by:

  • Leafhoppers
  • Planthoppers
  • Psyllids

Management

  • Resistant cultivars
  • Vector control
  • Disease-free materials
  • Sanitation

E. Fastidious Vascular Bacteria

Characteristics

These bacteria inhabit xylem or phloem tissues and are difficult to culture.

Features

  • Usually rod-shaped
  • Gram negative
  • Tiny colonies
  • Sensitive to temperature

Important Diseases

  • Pierce’s disease of grapes
  • Citrus greening
  • Ratoon stunting of sugarcane

F. Plant Pathogenic Protists

Protozoa-like Protists

Simple organisms that feed by engulfing food.

Examples

  • Slime molds
  • Clubroot organisms

Fungal-like Protists

Have cellulose cell walls instead of chitin.

Examples

  • Pythium
  • Phytophthora
  • Downy mildews

G. Nematodes

Nematode Characteristics

Microscopic thread-like worms that parasitize plants.

Characteristics

  • Unsegmented
  • Cylindrical
  • Possess stylet
  • Feed on plant tissues

Nematode Biology

Life Cycle

  • Egg
  • Juvenile
  • Adult

Reproduction

  • Amphimixis
  • Parthenogenesis

Survival Strategies

  • Diapause
  • Quiescence

Groups of Plant Parasitic Nematodes

1. Ectoparasites

Feed externally.

2. Semi-endoparasites

Partly enter plant tissues.

3. Endoparasites

Completely enter host tissues.

Important Diseases in the Philippines

  • Root knot
  • Slow decline of citrus
  • Rice root knot
  • Red ring disease of coconut

H. Fungi

Characteristics of Fungi

  • Form mycelium
  • Contain chitin and glucan
  • Reproduce through spores
  • Heterotrophic

Four Major Phyla

1. Chytridiomycota

Produce motile zoospores.

2. Zygomycota

Produce zygospores.

3. Basidiomycota

Club fungi and rust fungi.

4. Ascomycota

Sac fungi producing ascospores.

Deuteromycetes or Imperfect Fungi

Fungi lacking known sexual reproduction.

Examples

  • Alternaria
  • Bipolaris
  • Rhizoctonia
  • Sclerotium

Fungicides

1. Protective Fungicides

Prevent infection.

2. Eradicant Fungicides

Kill established fungi.

3. Systemic Fungicides

Absorbed and transported inside plants.

I. Parasitic Higher Plants

Hemiparasites

Contain chlorophyll but depend on hosts for water and minerals.

Examples

  • Witchweed
  • Mistletoe

True Parasites

Lack chlorophyll and depend entirely on host plants.

Examples

  • Dodder
  • Broomrapes
  • Bunga ng tubo (Aeginetia indica)
