Feed Resources and Feeding Management

Feed resources and feeding management refer to the proper selection, preparation, utilization, and management of feeds given to goats and sheep to support maintenance, growth, reproduction, lactation, health, and productivity.

Feed is one of the most important factors affecting growth, fertility, milk production, meat production, disease resistance, body condition, and profitability.

Poor feeding may result in slow growth, infertility, weak offspring, poor milk production, weight loss, disease susceptibility, and death.

Feeding management directly affects animal productivity and survival.

Feed Resources

Feed resources are materials or feedstuffs used as food for goats and sheep.

Feed resources may come from grasses, legumes, shrubs, crop residues, concentrates, by-products, and mineral supplements.

Good feed resources should provide energy, protein, vitamins, minerals, and water needed for maintenance and production.

Feed resources = materials used as animal feed.

Roughages

Roughages are bulky feeds high in fiber and relatively low in digestible nutrients.

Examples include grasses, hay, silage, forage crops, and crop residues.

Roughages are very important because goats and sheep are ruminants that need fiber for proper rumen function, rumination, and digestion.

  • High in crude fiber
  • Bulky
  • Stimulate rumination
  • Usually lower in digestibility than concentrates
Roughages = bulky high-fiber feeds.

Concentrates

Concentrates are feeds low in fiber but rich in digestible nutrients and energy.

Examples include corn, rice bran, copra meal, soybean meal, and commercial feeds.

Concentrates are commonly given to fast-growing animals, pregnant animals, lactating animals, and animals under fattening.

Concentrates = low-fiber nutrient-rich feeds.

Forages

Forages are edible plant materials consumed by livestock.

Forages include grasses, legumes, browse plants, leaves, and shrubs.

Forages are the primary feed source of goats and sheep, especially under pasture-based and backyard production systems.

Forages = edible plant materials for livestock.

Pasture

Pasture is a land area planted or covered with grasses and legumes where animals graze.

Good pasture management improves feed availability, animal nutrition, carrying capacity, and pasture longevity.

Pasture = grazing area for animals.

Grazing

Grazing is the feeding behavior of animals consuming forage directly from pasture.

Sheep are mainly grazers because they primarily consume grasses close to the ground.

  • Lower feed cost
  • Allows natural feeding behavior
  • Provides exercise
  • Improves pasture utilization when properly managed
Grazing = feeding directly on pasture.

Browsing

Browsing is the feeding behavior of animals consuming leaves, shrubs, vines, twigs, and young shoots.

Goats are natural browsers and prefer shrubs, leaves, vines, and young shoots.

Browsing = feeding on shrubs and leaves.

Legumes

Legumes are forage plants rich in protein and capable of fixing nitrogen in the soil.

Examples include ipil-ipil, centrosema, stylo, and madre de cacao.

Legumes improve feed quality and soil fertility.

Legumes = protein-rich forage plants.

Grasses

Grasses are important forage crops used in feeding goats and sheep.

Examples include napier grass, guinea grass, para grass, and carabao grass.

Good grasses provide energy and fiber needed for rumen function.

Grasses = major forage source for ruminants.

Crop Residues

Crop residues are plant materials remaining after harvest.

Examples include rice straw, corn stover, peanut tops, and sugarcane tops.

Crop residues may be used during feed shortage, especially during dry season.

Crop residues = agricultural leftovers used as feed.

Hay

Hay is forage preserved through drying.

Hay is prepared to store forage, preserve nutrients, and provide feed during feed shortage or dry season.

Good hay should be greenish, leafy, mold-free, and properly dried.

Hay = dried preserved forage.

Silage

Silage is succulent forage preserved through fermentation under anaerobic conditions.

Common silage materials include corn, napier grass, and sorghum.

Silage is useful because it preserves feed supply and remains palatable when properly prepared.

Silage = fermented preserved forage.

Water

Water is the most important nutrient required by animals.

Water is needed for digestion, nutrient transport, temperature regulation, metabolism, and milk production.

Lack of water may cause dehydration, poor growth, reduced milk production, and death.

Water is the most essential nutrient.

Feeding Management

Feeding management refers to the proper planning, scheduling, and control of feeding practices to meet animal nutritional requirements.

Proper feeding management includes ration formulation, feeding schedule, feed storage, feed quality control, and proper supplementation.

Feeding management = proper planning and control of feeding.

Balanced Ration

A balanced ration contains all nutrients needed by the animal in proper amount and proportion.

A balanced ration should supply energy, protein, minerals, vitamins, and water.

Balanced ration = complete and proper nutrient supply.

Nutrients

Nutrients are chemical substances in feed needed by animals for growth, reproduction, maintenance, and production.

Major nutrients include carbohydrates, protein, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water.

Nutrients = substances needed for life and production.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the major energy source in animal feeds.

They provide energy, maintain body heat, and support normal body functions.

Common sources include grasses, corn, and grains.

Carbohydrates = primary energy source.

Protein

Protein is a body-building nutrient needed for growth, muscle development, milk production, tissue repair, and enzyme formation.

Protein deficiency may cause poor growth, low productivity, weight loss, and weak body condition.

Protein = body-building nutrient.

Minerals

Minerals are inorganic nutrients needed for body functions.

Examples include calcium, phosphorus, sodium, and iron.

Minerals support bone formation, muscle function, reproduction, metabolism, and body regulation.

Minerals regulate body processes.

Vitamins

Vitamins are organic compounds needed in small amounts for normal body function.

Vitamin deficiency may cause poor growth, weakness, poor immunity, and reproductive problems.

Vitamins support normal metabolism.

Salt Supplementation

Salt supplementation is important because salt helps maintain appetite, nerve function, and water balance.

Salt may be given through salt blocks, mineral blocks, or mixed feeds.

Salt is an essential mineral supplement.

Mineral Supplementation

Mineral supplementation prevents deficiencies and improves productivity.

Mineral deficiency may cause poor growth, weak bones, infertility, and low milk production.

Mineral supplementation improves animal performance.

Feed Intake

Feed intake refers to the amount of feed consumed by the animal.

Feed intake is affected by palatability, feed quality, water availability, health, and environmental temperature.

Feed intake affects productivity.

Palatability

Palatability refers to the attractiveness or acceptability of feed to animals.

Feeds that are fresh, clean, and acceptable in smell, taste, and texture are consumed more readily.

Palatability influences feed consumption.

Feed Conversion Efficiency

Feed conversion efficiency is the ability of animals to convert feed into body weight or production.

Good feed conversion means less feed is needed for faster growth and better profitability.

Feed conversion efficiency affects profitability.

Supplemental Feeding

Supplemental feeding is the addition of extra nutrients to the regular diet.

Supplementation is commonly given during pregnancy, lactation, fattening, and dry season.

Supplemental feeding improves nutrition.

Flushing

Flushing is increasing feed intake before breeding to improve fertility and ovulation rate.

It helps improve conception, increase ovulation, and increase litter size.

Flushing = increased feeding before breeding.

Feeding During Pregnancy

Pregnant animals require adequate nutrients, sufficient protein, minerals, and vitamins.

Poor feeding during pregnancy may result in abortion, weak offspring, and low birth weight.

Pregnant animals need adequate nutrition.

Feeding During Lactation

Lactating animals require higher nutrient intake because milk production demands more energy and protein.

Poor feeding during lactation may reduce milk yield, body condition, and offspring growth.

Lactating animals require more nutrients.

Feeding Young Animals

Young goats and sheep require colostrum, milk, quality forage, clean water, and gradual transition to solid feed.

Colostrum is important because it contains antibodies that protect newborn animals against disease.

Colostrum provides passive immunity.

Colostrum

Colostrum is the first milk produced after birth.

It is rich in antibodies, nutrients, and protective factors essential for newborn survival.

Newborn animals should receive colostrum immediately after birth.

Colostrum = first milk rich in antibodies.

Creep Feeding

Creep feeding is the practice of providing supplemental feed to young animals while preventing access by adults.

It supports faster growth, earlier weaning, and improved body weight.

Creep feeding improves young animal growth.

Dry Season Feeding

Feed shortage commonly occurs during the dry season.

Dry season feeding strategies include hay making, silage preparation, feed conservation, and supplemental feeding.

Feed conservation is important during dry season.

Feed Storage

Feed storage prevents spoilage, mold growth, contamination, and nutrient loss.

Feeds should be kept dry, clean, and protected from pests.

Proper storage preserves feed quality.

Moldy Feed

Moldy feed should not be given because it may contain toxins harmful to animals.

It may cause poisoning, digestive problems, reduced performance, and death.

Moldy feed is dangerous to livestock.

Anti-Nutritional Factors

Anti-nutritional factors are substances that reduce feed utilization or interfere with nutrient absorption.

Examples include tannins, toxic compounds, and certain plant chemicals.

Anti-nutritional factors reduce feed value.

Carrying Capacity

Carrying capacity is the number of animals that a pasture can support without deterioration.

Overstocking may lead to pasture destruction, feed shortage, and poor animal performance.

Carrying capacity = pasture support capability.
