Philosophy of Extension Education

What is Philosophy?

  • A body of principles underlying a given branch of learning, religious system, or human activity.
  • A philosophy is a background of theory, knowledge, and beliefs explaining and justifying a way of life.
  • “View of Life” — what ought to be and its components, and how to bridge the gap between “what is” and “what ought to be.”
  • A guide to a person’s actions.

View of Life

What Ought To Be refers to the ideal view of life.

The Gap refers to the problem areas or needs of the people.

What Is refers to the actual or current state of people in terms of knowledge, skills, attitudes, resources, and living conditions.

Philosophy of Extension

A philosophy of extension includes the set of beliefs and principles that serve as the basis for making decisions and judgments in the field of extension.

Structure of Philosophy

  • Beliefs — one’s mental convictions, worldview, or acceptance of something true or actual.
  • Ethics — the basic principles of right actions and conduct as defined by profession, society, and nation.
  • Values — something regarded as desirable, worthy, or right.

Philosophy of Extension Education

1. Extension Programs Start Where the People Are

Extension programs should begin based on the present social, cultural, financial, and intellectual condition of the target group.

Extension workers must identify the people’s habits, beliefs, attitudes, traditions, interests, aspirations, farming practices, problems, and available resources.

2. The Classroom is Where the People Are

In extension education, learning does not only happen inside formal classrooms.

Teaching can happen in farms, homes, barangays, and wherever the clientele are found.

Technology should be communicated under actual field conditions.

3. Extension Programs are Based on People’s Needs

Extension workers must determine the needs, interests, problems, and aspirations of the people through community surveys, meetings, and consultations.

People should understand their own problems and explore possible solutions.

Well-planned extension programs encourage enthusiasm and active participation among the people.

4. People Learn by Doing

Extension workers should provide opportunities for farmers and homemakers to participate actively in learning activities.

Demonstration and actual practice help people learn more effectively.

5. Education is Carried on With Groups or Individuals

Extension education may be conducted through group methods or individual approaches.

Group approaches can reach more people and promote leadership and cooperation.

Individual approaches are more effective for solving specific personal problems.

6. Extension Works With and Through People

Extension workers cooperate with local leaders and community members in implementing extension activities.

Community participation promotes unity, cooperation, and successful project implementation.

7. The Spirit of Self-help is Essential in Democratic Living

Technical assistance and learning-by-doing help develop the capabilities and self-reliance of the clientele.

Extension workers provide guidance while people themselves participate actively in planning and implementing projects.

This develops productive and self-reliant citizens who contribute to societal development.
