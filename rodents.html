🐀 Rodents and Other Vertebrate Pests

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

Introduction

Agricultural production is affected not only by insects, diseases, weeds, and mollusks but also by vertebrate animals that damage crops and stored products.

These animals are collectively known as vertebrate pests. They cause significant economic losses through feeding, contamination, destruction of farm structures, and transmission of diseases.

Among vertebrate pests, rodents are considered the most important and destructive worldwide.

Definition of Vertebrate Pests

Vertebrate pests are animals possessing a vertebral column or backbone that cause economic damage to crops, livestock, stored products, farm structures, and agricultural operations.

  • Crop destruction
  • Food contamination
  • Reduced yield
  • Damage to property
  • Disease transmission
  • Increased production costs

Major Groups of Vertebrate Pests

  • Rodents – rats and mice
  • Birds – sparrows, blackbirds, and pigeons
  • Mammals – bats, monkeys, and wild pigs

Rodents

Rodents belong to the Order Rodentia. The word Rodentia comes from the Latin word rodere, meaning to gnaw.

Rodents are characterized by their continuously growing incisors and strong gnawing habits.

Economic Importance of Rodents

  • Consume crops
  • Destroy stored grains
  • Damage farm and household structures
  • Contaminate food products
  • Spread diseases
  • Reduce crop productivity

General Characteristics of Rodents

Physical Characteristics

  • Small to medium-sized mammals
  • Covered with fur
  • Long tail
  • Strong jaws
  • Sharp incisors

Behavioral Characteristics

  • Mostly nocturnal
  • Highly adaptable
  • Excellent climbers
  • Excellent swimmers
  • Excellent burrowers
  • Highly reproductive

Distinguishing Characteristic of Rodents

Continuously Growing Incisors

Rodents possess one pair of upper incisors and one pair of lower incisors. These incisors lack roots and continuously grow throughout life.

Rodents have a total of four incisors. They must gnaw constantly to prevent overgrowth of these teeth.

ALE Exam Trap

Do not confuse rodents with rabbits. Rodents have one pair of upper incisors, while rabbits or lagomorphs have two pairs of upper incisors.

Why Rodents Gnaw Continuously

Rodents gnaw because their incisors continuously grow. Gnawing keeps the teeth from becoming excessively long.

  • Wood
  • Plastic
  • Crops
  • Seeds
  • Electrical wires
  • Stored products

Major Rodent Pests

1. Rice Field Rat

Scientific Name: Rattus argentiventer

The Rice Field Rat is one of the most important vertebrate pests in rice production.

Habitat

  • Rice fields
  • Irrigated lowlands
  • Agricultural areas

Damage

  • Feeds on rice seedlings
  • Attacks tillering rice
  • Damages flowering rice
  • Feeds on ripening grains

Symptoms

  • Missing hills
  • Cut stems
  • Lodged rice plants
  • Empty panicles

2. Roof Rat

Scientific Name: Rattus rattus

  • Excellent climber
  • Slender body
  • Long tail
  • Common in roof spaces, warehouses, grain storage facilities, and houses

Roof rats cause grain losses, food contamination, and structural damage.

3. Norway Rat

Scientific Name: Rattus norvegicus

  • Larger body size
  • Aggressive
  • Strong burrowing habit
  • Common near drainage systems, irrigation canals, dikes, and sewer systems

Norway rats damage stored products, irrigation structures, and farm embankments through burrowing.

4. House Mouse

Scientific Name: Mus musculus

  • Small body
  • Rapid reproduction
  • Common household pest
  • Causes feed contamination, food losses, and damage to packaging materials

Damage Caused by Rodents

1. Direct Feeding Damage

Rodents directly consume rice, corn, vegetables, fruits, and root crops.

2. Seedling Damage

Young seedlings are highly vulnerable. Rodents may cut stems, uproot seedlings, and destroy planting stands.

3. Damage to Mature Crops

Rodents feed on developing grains, fruits, tubers, and seeds.

4. Postharvest Damage

Rodents attack warehouses, grain bins, and storage facilities, resulting in grain loss, feed loss, and product spoilage.

5. Contamination Damage

Rodents contaminate food through urine, feces, hair, and dead bodies.

High-Yield Exam Fact: A rodent often destroys far more food through contamination than through actual consumption. Contamination losses may exceed feeding losses.

Signs of Rodent Infestation

Droppings

Presence of fecal pellets.

Gnaw Marks

Chewed products, wires, containers, and structures.

Burrows

Holes near dikes, irrigation canals, and storage facilities.

Runways

Frequently used rodent pathways.

Footprints and Tail Marks

Visible on dusty surfaces.

Reproduction of Rodents

Rodents reproduce extremely rapidly, allowing their populations to increase quickly and cause outbreaks.

  • Early sexual maturity
  • Short gestation period
  • Large litter size
  • Multiple litters annually

Other Important Vertebrate Pests

Birds

Birds feed on rice grains, corn, fruits, and seeds.

Examples: Sparrows, blackbirds, and pigeons.

Birds cause grain loss, fruit damage, and seed destruction.

Bats

Certain bats become agricultural pests by feeding on fruits such as mango, banana, guava, and other fruit crops.

Monkeys

In some regions, monkeys damage banana, corn, coconut, and root crops.

Wild Pigs

Wild pigs are serious vertebrate pests because they uproot crops, trample plants, disturb soil, and destroy seedlings.

Management of Rodents and Vertebrate Pests

Effective management requires integration of several approaches.

A. Cultural Control

Field Sanitation

  • Remove weeds
  • Remove trash
  • Remove hiding places

Synchronized Planting

Planting crops at the same time reduces prolonged food availability and limits rodent population growth.

Proper Storage

Store products in rodent-proof structures.

B. Mechanical Control

Trapping

  • Snap traps
  • Cage traps
  • Multiple-catch traps

Barriers

Physical barriers prevent access to crops and storage areas.

Bird Scaring Devices

  • Scarecrows
  • Reflective tapes
  • Noise makers

Trap Barrier System (TBS)

Trap Barrier System is a highly effective rodent management technique used in rice production.

Components

  • Plastic fence barrier
  • Trap crop
  • Multiple-catch traps

Procedure

A trap crop is planted 2–3 weeks earlier than the main crop. The earlier planted crop attracts rodents, while the plastic fence directs rodents into traps.

Advantages

  • Environmentally friendly
  • Reduces pesticide use
  • Effective against field rats

ALE Exam Tip: Trap Barrier System is a favorite board examination topic in Integrated Rodent Management.

C. Biological Control

Natural enemies help suppress rodent populations.

  • Owls
  • Snakes
  • Cats
  • Dogs

Barn Owl

Scientific Name: Tyto alba

The Barn Owl is one of the most effective biological control agents against rats in agricultural fields.

ALE Exam Tip: If asked for the most important biological control agent against field rats, answer: Barn Owl (Tyto alba).

D. Chemical Control

Rodenticides

Rodenticides are chemicals used to kill rodents.

1. Acute Rodenticides

Acute rodenticides cause rapid death after a single feeding.

Example: Zinc Phosphide

Zinc phosphide produces toxic phosphine gas inside the stomach. It is fast acting but may cause bait shyness.

Bait Shyness

Bait shyness occurs when a rodent consumes a sub-lethal dose of poison, becomes ill, survives, and later avoids the bait.

ALE Exam Tip: Bait shyness is why acute rodenticides are often less preferred for long-term control.

2. Anticoagulant Rodenticides

Anticoagulant rodenticides cause death through internal bleeding.

  • Warfarin
  • Bromadiolone
  • Brodifacoum

They interfere with Vitamin K metabolism and blood clotting.

Advantages of Anticoagulants

  • Delayed action
  • Reduced bait shyness
  • Better population control

Integrated Rodent Management (IRM)

Integrated Rodent Management combines multiple methods for long-term rodent suppression.

Components

  • Monitoring
  • Sanitation
  • Trapping
  • Biological control
  • Chemical control
  • Trap Barrier System

Advantages

  • Sustainable
  • Environmentally safe
  • Reduced chemical dependence
  • Better long-term results

Economic Importance of Rodent Control

  • Reduces crop losses
  • Protects stored products
  • Improves food security
  • Increases farmer income
  • Improves agricultural productivity

Summary

Rodents are the most economically important vertebrate pests in agriculture. They cause losses through direct feeding, contamination, structural damage, and rapid population growth. Major species include the Rice Field Rat (Rattus argentiventer), Roof Rat (Rattus rattus), Norway Rat (Rattus norvegicus), and House Mouse (Mus musculus). Effective management requires an Integrated Rodent Management approach that combines sanitation, trapping, biological control, chemical control, and Trap Barrier Systems.
