Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the economy as a whole. It focuses on broad economic activities such as national income, employment, unemployment, inflation, savings, investment, taxation, government spending, and economic growth.

In the Philippine setting, macroeconomics helps explain issues such as inflation, unemployment, rising food prices, fuel price increases, agricultural imports, government subsidies, and the contribution of agriculture to national development.

National Income Accounting (NIA)

National Income Accounting measures the economic performance of a country. It records the total value of goods and services produced and the income earned by households, firms, and government within a given period.

In agriculture, NIA helps measure the contribution of crop production, livestock, fisheries, forestry, and agribusiness to the Philippine economy.

Circular Flow Diagram

The circular flow diagram illustrates the movement of goods, services, income, and resources within the economy. It shows the interaction among households, firms, government, and the foreign sector.

Households provide factors of production such as land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship. Firms use these resources to produce goods and services. In return, households receive wages, rent, interest, and profit.

Two Indicators of Aggregate Output

Aggregate output refers to the total value of goods and services produced in the economy.

  1. Gross National Product (GNP)
  2. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

Gross National Product (GNP)

Gross National Product refers to the market value of all final goods and services produced by the citizens of a country within a given period, whether production occurs inside or outside the country.

For the Philippines, GNP includes income earned by Overseas Filipino Workers and Filipino-owned businesses abroad.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

Gross Domestic Product refers to the market value of all final goods and services produced within the territorial boundaries of a country within a given period, regardless of nationality.

GDP includes agricultural products produced inside the Philippines such as rice, corn, vegetables, fruits, fisheries, poultry, and livestock products.

Final Goods

Final goods are goods ready for consumption and not intended for further processing. They are counted in GDP and GNP.

Rice sold directly to consumers is a final good. Palay sold to rice mills is usually an intermediate good because it still needs processing.

Net Factor Income from the Rest of the World (NFIRW)

Net Factor Income from the Rest of the World refers to the difference between income earned by Filipinos abroad and income earned by foreigners inside the Philippines.

NFIRW = Income from Abroad − Income Paid to Foreigners

Relationship of GDP and GNP

GNP = GDP + NFIRW

GDP measures production within the country, while GNP measures income earned by citizens whether inside or outside the country.

Approaches to GDP/GNP Computation

  1. Expenditure Approach
  2. Income Approach
  3. Value-Added Approach

Expenditure Approach

The Expenditure Approach computes GDP by adding all spending on final goods and services.

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • C: Personal Consumption Expenditure
  • I: Investment or Gross Domestic Capital Formation
  • G: Government Consumption Expenditure
  • X: Exports
  • M: Imports

Personal Consumption Expenditure

Personal Consumption Expenditure refers to spending by households on goods and services such as food, rice, transportation, clothing, electricity, education, and health services.

Government Consumption Expenditure

Government Consumption Expenditure refers to government spending on goods and services such as irrigation projects, farm-to-market roads, agricultural subsidies, extension programs, and salaries of government workers.

Gross Domestic Capital Formation or Investment

Investment refers to spending on capital goods used in future production. In agriculture, this includes farm machinery, irrigation systems, processing facilities, warehouses, and buildings.

Exports

Exports are goods sold to other countries. Philippine agricultural exports may include bananas, coconut products, tuna, pineapples, and mangoes.

Imports

Imports are goods purchased from other countries such as imported rice, fuel, fertilizers, feeds, and machinery.

Net Exports

Net Exports = Exports − Imports

Income Approach

The Income Approach computes GDP by adding all incomes earned by factors of production.

GDP = Compensation of Employees + Net Operating Surplus + Depreciation + Indirect Business Taxes less Subsidies

Compensation of Employees

Compensation of Employees refers to wages, salaries, and benefits paid to workers, including farm laborers, agricultural technicians, and agribusiness workers.

Net Operating Surplus

Net Operating Surplus refers to income earned by businesses such as rent, profit, and interest.

Depreciation

Depreciation refers to the wear and tear of physical capital over time, such as tractors, irrigation pumps, farm equipment, and processing machines.

Indirect Business Taxes less Subsidies (IBTS)

These are taxes imposed on production, sale, or purchase of goods and services, minus government subsidies.

Value Added Tax or VAT is an example of an indirect business tax.

Value-Added Approach

The Value-Added Approach computes GDP by adding the value added at each stage of production.

Value Added = Sales − Purchases from Other Firms

This approach avoids double counting because it counts only the additional value created at each stage.

National Income Accounts

Nominal GDP

Nominal GDP refers to GDP measured using current prices. It does not adjust for inflation.

Real GDP

Real GDP refers to GDP measured using constant prices from a selected base year. It removes the effect of changing prices and reflects actual production growth.

Real GDP = (Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator) × 100

GDP Deflator

GDP Deflator is a price index used to convert nominal GDP into real GDP. It measures changes in the overall price level.

GDP per Capita

GDP per capita refers to GDP divided by total population.

GDP per capita = GDP ÷ Population

PPP Adjusted GDP

PPP Adjusted GDP or Purchasing Power Parity Adjusted GDP accounts for differences in purchasing power between countries.

Personal Disposable Income

Personal Disposable Income refers to income available to households after taxes. This income may be used for consumption, savings, and investments.

Equivalence of Approaches

The Expenditure Approach, Income Approach, and Value-Added Approach are equivalent because spending, production, and income are interconnected in the economy.

Price Index

A Price Index measures changes in the prices of goods and services over time. It helps monitor inflation and purchasing power.

Consumer Price Index (CPI)

Consumer Price Index measures changes in prices of commonly purchased goods and services such as rice, meat, fish, transportation, fuel, and electricity.

CPI is commonly used in measuring inflation in the Philippines.

Inflation

Inflation refers to the general increase in prices over time. When inflation rises, purchasing power decreases, production costs increase, and cost of living rises.

In agriculture, inflation may increase the prices of fertilizer, fuel, feeds, pesticides, and transportation.

Demand-Pull Inflation

Demand-pull inflation occurs when consumers suddenly or seasonally increase demand for products, allowing sellers to increase prices.

Examples include Christmas season demand, Holy Week demand, or panic buying before typhoons.

Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation occurs because of rising production costs such as fuel price increases, higher fertilizer prices, wage increases, and expensive raw materials.

Inflation Rate

Inflation Rate refers to the annual or year-on-year rate of change in CPI.

Inflation Rate = [(Current CPI − Previous CPI) ÷ Previous CPI] × 100

Deflation

Deflation occurs when prices decline over time. Although lower prices may benefit consumers, deflation may reduce business income and discourage production.

Disinflation

Disinflation refers to a decrease in the rate of inflation. Prices are still increasing, but at a slower rate.

Consumption, Savings, and Investments

Consumption refers to spending on goods and services. Savings refer to the portion of income not spent. Investment refers to spending on capital goods for future production.

Examples of agricultural investments include tractors, irrigation systems, warehouses, and processing facilities.

Consumption Function

The Consumption Function explains the relationship between income and consumption. As income increases, consumption usually increases, but households may save part of additional income.

Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC)

MPC = Change in Consumption ÷ Change in Income

Marginal Propensity to Save (MPS)

MPS = Change in Savings ÷ Change in Income

Unemployment

Unemployment refers to the condition where people willing and able to work cannot find jobs. In agriculture, unemployment is common during off-season periods.

Labor Force

Labor force refers to employed and unemployed persons who are actively seeking work.

Employed

Employed persons are those who worked during the reference period or had jobs or businesses.

Unemployed

Unemployed persons are people without work but actively seeking employment.

Employment Rate

Employment Rate refers to the proportion of employed persons to the total labor force.

Underemployment

Underemployment refers to employed persons seeking additional work or longer working hours.

Visible Underemployment

Visible underemployment refers to workers working less than normal hours and seeking additional work.

Invisible Underemployment

Invisible underemployment refers to workers working full-time but earning insufficient income or performing jobs below their qualifications.

Unemployment Rate

Unemployment Rate refers to the proportion of unemployed persons to the total labor force.

Types of Unemployment

Frictional Unemployment

Frictional unemployment occurs while workers are searching for jobs. It is temporary and unavoidable.

Structural Unemployment

Structural unemployment occurs because of changes in the economy, modernization, mechanization, technological change, or lack of skills.

Cyclical Unemployment

Cyclical unemployment occurs because of changes in business cycles. It increases during economic recessions.

Disguised Unemployment

Disguised unemployment occurs when more workers are employed than necessary.

A small farm requiring only 5 workers but employing 10 workers is an example of disguised unemployment.

Seasonal Unemployment

Seasonal unemployment occurs when work is available only during certain seasons, such as planting or harvest periods.

Monetary and Fiscal Policy

Monetary and Fiscal Policies are government tools used to stabilize the economy.

Monetary Policy

Monetary Policy refers to regulation of money supply and interest rates by the central bank. In the Philippines, this is managed by the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas.

Monetary policy affects inflation, borrowing, investment, spending, and employment.

Money

Money refers to assets regularly used to buy goods and services.

Functions of Money

  • Medium of Exchange: money is used in buying and selling goods and services.
  • Unit of Account: money serves as a standard measure of value.
  • Store of Value: money can preserve purchasing power for future use.

Kinds of Money

  • Commodity Money: money with intrinsic value, such as gold.
  • Fiat Money: money with value because of government decree, such as the Philippine peso.

Types or Forms of Money

  • Peso notes
  • Coins
  • Checks
  • Demand deposits
  • Current accounts

Fiscal Policy

Fiscal Policy refers to government policy regarding taxation, government spending, and public borrowing. It may be used to stabilize the economy during recession or inflation.

Taxation and Tax Burden

Taxation refers to the power of the government to impose charges or contributions on individuals, businesses, and organizations to generate revenue for public services and government operations.

Taxes finance infrastructure projects, education, health services, irrigation systems, agricultural subsidies, farm-to-market roads, public safety, and other government programs.

In the Philippines, taxes are collected primarily through the Bureau of Internal Revenue and the Bureau of Customs.

Tax Burden

Tax Burden refers to the proportion of taxes paid relative to a taxpayer’s income. It measures how much of a person’s or firm’s income goes to taxes.

Direct Tax

Direct taxes are imposed directly on a person’s income, wealth, or property. The burden of payment cannot easily be shifted to another person.

  • Income tax
  • Estate tax
  • Donor’s tax
  • Property tax

Indirect Tax

Indirect taxes are imposed on goods and services. The burden may be transferred from producers or sellers to consumers through higher prices.

  • Value Added Tax (VAT)
  • Excise tax
  • Customs duties

Progressive Tax

Progressive tax is a tax system where higher-income individuals pay a higher percentage of tax compared to lower-income individuals.

This system follows the principle of ability to pay and may help reduce income inequality.

Proportional Tax

Proportional tax is a tax system where all taxpayers pay the same percentage of tax regardless of income level. It is also called a flat tax system.

Regressive Tax

Regressive tax is a tax system where lower-income individuals carry a heavier tax burden compared to higher-income individuals.

Poor households may feel fuel price increases more heavily because transportation and food expenses consume a larger portion of their income.

Importance of Taxation

Taxation enables the government to provide public services, support agricultural programs, build infrastructure, maintain peace and order, support disaster response, and finance economic development.
