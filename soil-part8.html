🌿 Part 8: Organic Matter & Microbiology

Soil Organic Matter (SOM)

  • Organic matter consists of decomposed plant and animal residues.
  • It includes fresh residues, decomposing material, and stable humus.
  • It is an important component of soil that improves overall soil quality.

Components of Organic Matter

  • Fresh residues – newly added plant and animal materials
  • Active organic matter – partially decomposed materials
  • Humus – stable, dark-colored, well-decomposed organic matter

Functions of Organic Matter

  • Improves soil structure and aggregation.
  • Increases water holding capacity.
  • Enhances nutrient availability.
  • Improves soil aeration.
  • Supports microbial activity.
More organic matter = better soil fertility

Humus

  • Highly decomposed organic matter.
  • Dark in color and stable.
  • Has high nutrient-holding capacity.
  • Improves soil structure and CEC.

Carbon to Nitrogen Ratio (C:N Ratio)

  • Ratio of carbon to nitrogen in organic materials.
  • Important in decomposition and nutrient release.
  • Ideal C:N ratio is about 10:1 to 12:1.
  • High C:N ratio slows decomposition.

Soil Organisms

  • Living organisms found in the soil.
  • Include microorganisms and larger organisms.

Types of Soil Organisms

Bacteria

  • Most abundant microorganisms in soil.
  • Responsible for decomposition and nutrient cycling.

Fungi

  • Break down organic materials.
  • Help in nutrient cycling and soil aggregation.

Actinomycetes

  • Intermediate between bacteria and fungi.
  • Decompose resistant organic materials.

Algae

  • Photosynthetic organisms found in moist soils.

Protozoa

  • Feed on bacteria and help regulate microbial populations.

Earthworms

  • Improve soil structure through burrowing.
  • Enhance aeration and drainage.

Role of Soil Microorganisms

  • Decompose organic matter.
  • Release nutrients for plant use.
  • Improve soil structure.
  • Fix atmospheric nitrogen (in some bacteria).

Decomposition

  • Breakdown of organic materials into simpler substances.
  • Releases nutrients back into the soil.
  • Essential for nutrient cycling.

Mineralization

  • Conversion of organic nutrients into inorganic forms.
  • Makes nutrients available for plant uptake.

Immobilization

  • Conversion of inorganic nutrients into organic forms by microorganisms.
  • Temporarily reduces nutrient availability to plants.

Good Biological Condition of Soil

  • High organic matter content.
  • Active and diverse microorganisms.
  • Balanced decomposition and nutrient cycling.
