Financial Ratios for Cooperatives

I. Liquidity Ratios (Short-Term Debt-Paying Ability)

Liquidity refers to the ability of a cooperative to settle its short-term obligations as they become due. These obligations may include supplier payments, operational expenses, member withdrawals, and short-term loans.

1. Current Ratio

The Current Ratio measures the cooperative’s ability to pay current liabilities using current assets.

Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

CDA Standard: The commonly accepted standard is around 1.25 : 1 to 1.5 : 1.

A ratio that is too high may indicate idle resources that are not being properly invested or circulated.

Example:

₱500,000 ÷ ₱250,000 = 2.0

The cooperative has ₱2.00 of current assets for every ₱1.00 of short-term obligation.

2. Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio)

The Quick Ratio is a stricter measure of liquidity because inventories and prepaid expenses are excluded from the computation.

Quick Ratio =
(Current Assets − Inventory − Prepaid Expenses) ÷ Current Liabilities

This ratio is highly important for agricultural and multipurpose cooperatives that maintain large inventories such as feeds, fertilizers, rice, grocery stocks, and seeds.

II. Solvency and Leverage Ratios (Long-Term Stability)

These ratios evaluate the cooperative’s long-term financial strength and determine how much financing comes from debt versus members’ funds.

3. Solvency Ratio (Equity-to-Asset Ratio)

The Solvency Ratio measures the percentage of total assets financed by members’ equity.

Solvency Ratio =
(Members’ Equity ÷ Total Assets) × 100

A high solvency ratio reflects stronger institutional stability because the cooperative relies more on members’ capital rather than borrowed funds.

4. Debt-to-Asset Ratio (Debt Ratio)

The Debt Ratio measures the portion of assets financed through liabilities or external debt.

Debt Ratio =
(Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets) × 100
Board Exam Reminder:
If the ratio reaches around 70% to 80%, the cooperative may already be highly leveraged and financially risky.

5. Debt-to-Equity Ratio

This ratio compares the amount provided by creditors against the amount invested by members.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio =
Total Liabilities ÷ Members’ Equity

For credit cooperatives, this ratio is naturally higher because member savings and deposits are classified as liabilities.

III. Profitability and Surplus Ratios (Operational Viability)

Board Exam Reminder:
Cooperatives generate a Net Surplus, not a Net Profit.

The excess earnings are eventually returned to members through:

  • Patronage Refunds
  • Interest on Share Capital

6. Net Surplus Margin

The Net Surplus Margin measures the percentage of surplus remaining after all expenses are deducted from total revenues.

Net Surplus Margin =
(Net Surplus ÷ Total Gross Revenue) × 100

Example:

(₱15,000 ÷ ₱100,000) × 100 = 15%

7. Return on Assets (ROA)

ROA measures how efficiently the cooperative uses its assets to generate surplus.

Return on Assets =
(Net Surplus ÷ Total Assets) × 100

8. Return on Members’ Equity (ROE)

ROE measures the earning capacity of the members’ invested capital.

Return on Equity =
(Net Surplus ÷ Members’ Equity) × 100

IV. Operational Efficiency Ratios (Management and Cost Control)

These ratios evaluate how effectively the cooperative controls expenses and manages daily operations.

9. Operating Expense Ratio (OER)

The OER shows how much of every peso earned is consumed by administrative and operating expenses.

Operating Expense Ratio =
(Operating Expenses ÷ Total Gross Revenue) × 100

Standard: Lower ratios are better.

If the ratio exceeds around 70% to 80%, the cooperative may already be suffering from excessive operating costs.

10. Collection Efficiency Ratio

This ratio measures the cooperative’s effectiveness in collecting receivables and loan payments.

Collection Efficiency =
(Actual Amount Collected ÷ Total Amount Due for Collection) × 100

Standard: The ideal industry standard is around 95% or higher.

Poor collection efficiency may lead to serious cash flow problems.

11. Past Due Ratio (Non-Performing Loans Ratio)

The Past Due Ratio measures the percentage of loans that remain unpaid beyond their due dates.

Past Due Ratio =
(Total Past Due Loans ÷ Total Outstanding Loan Portfolio) × 100

Under the PESOS framework, the recommended level is below 10%.

Higher ratios indicate weak loan quality and potential bad debt problems.

V. Institutional Capital and Mandated Reserves

These are unique financial requirements under Philippine Cooperative Laws.

12. Institutional Capital Ratio (Capital Adequacy)

This ratio measures the amount of permanent institutional capital retained within the cooperative.

These funds cannot normally be withdrawn by members.

Institutional Capital Ratio =
(Institutional Capital ÷ Total Assets) × 100

Components of Institutional Capital

  • General Reserve Fund
  • Retained Earnings
  • Institutional Reserves
Philippine Cooperative Code (RA 9520):
Cooperatives are required to allocate at least 10% of annual Net Surplus to the General Reserve Fund.
