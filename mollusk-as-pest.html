🐌 Mollusks as Agricultural Pests

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

Definition of Mollusks

Mollusks are soft-bodied invertebrate animals belonging to the phylum Mollusca. Many species possess shells that protect their bodies, while others have reduced or absent shells.

Although many mollusks are beneficial in ecosystems, some species become serious agricultural pests by feeding on crops and causing economic losses.

General Characteristics of Mollusks

  • Soft and unsegmented body
  • Bilateral symmetry
  • Muscular foot used for movement
  • Mantle that may produce a shell
  • Radula or rasping tongue-like structure used for feeding
  • Moist body surface
  • Slow movement

Importance of Mollusks in Agriculture

Some mollusks become pests because they feed on crops, especially young and tender plant parts.

  • Feed on crops
  • Damage seedlings
  • Reduce crop yield
  • Lower crop quality
  • Cause economic losses to farmers

Mollusks are particularly destructive during rainy seasons and in moist environments.

Major Agricultural Mollusk Pests

1. Snails

Snails are mollusks that usually possess a coiled shell. They move using a muscular foot and leave slime trails as they move.

  • Possess a coiled shell
  • Move using a muscular foot
  • Leave slime trails
  • Feed using a radula

Examples: Golden Apple Snail and Garden Snails.

2. Slugs

Slugs are soft-bodied mollusks with absent or greatly reduced shells. They produce abundant mucus and are usually active at night.

  • Shell absent or greatly reduced
  • Soft-bodied
  • Produce abundant mucus
  • Usually active at night

Examples: Field Slugs and Garden Slugs.

Golden Apple Snail

Scientific Name

Pomacea canaliculata

Importance

The Golden Apple Snail is considered one of the most destructive pests of rice in many Asian countries. It was originally introduced as a food source but later became a major pest.

Distribution

  • Philippines
  • Thailand
  • Vietnam
  • Indonesia
  • Other rice-growing countries

Identification of Golden Apple Snail

Adult Snail

  • Large golden-brown shell
  • Rounded shell opening
  • Soft body enclosed by shell

Egg Mass

  • Bright pink to reddish color
  • Deposited above the water surface
  • Usually found on rice stems and other structures
  • Easily visible in the field

Life Cycle of Golden Apple Snail

Egg

Eggs are deposited in clusters above water.

Juvenile

Juveniles hatch and fall into the water where they begin feeding.

Adult

Adults feed actively and reproduce rapidly.

Damage Caused by Golden Apple Snail

Direct Feeding Damage

The snail feeds on newly transplanted rice seedlings, direct-seeded rice, and young aquatic plants.

Symptoms

  • Missing hills
  • Cut seedlings
  • Floating plant parts
  • Irregular gaps in the field

Economic Effects

  • Reduced plant population
  • Lower yield
  • Increased replanting costs
  • Additional labor expenses

Favorable Conditions for Mollusk Infestation

  • High moisture
  • Standing water
  • Frequent rainfall
  • Dense vegetation
  • Poor field sanitation

Feeding Mechanism

Mollusks feed using a specialized structure called the radula.

The radula acts like a rasping tongue that scrapes plant tissues during feeding.

Types of Damage Caused by Mollusks

1. Defoliation

Mollusks may remove leaves and young shoots.

2. Seedling Destruction

Young seedlings may be consumed completely, especially in rice fields.

3. Root Damage

Some species may attack underground plant parts.

4. Reduced Plant Stand

Loss of seedlings creates gaps in crop establishment.

Economic Importance of Mollusk Pests

Yield Reduction

Heavy infestations may significantly reduce crop yield.

Increased Production Cost

Farmers may spend additional money for control measures, replanting, and labor.

Crop Quality Reduction

Damaged crops usually have lower market value.

Management of Mollusk Pests

A. Cultural Control

  • Remove weeds and alternate hosts
  • Clean irrigation canals
  • Practice proper water management
  • Drain fields when possible
  • Collect snails manually
  • Destroy egg masses

B. Mechanical Control

Mechanical control involves the use of physical methods to reduce snail and slug populations.

  • Use banana leaves as traps
  • Use papaya leaves as attractants
  • Install screens and barriers
  • Prevent movement of snails into fields

C. Biological Control

Biological control uses natural enemies to suppress mollusk populations.

  • Ducks
  • Fish
  • Certain birds

D. Chemical Control

Molluscicides are chemicals specifically designed to kill snails and slugs.

Examples: Niclosamide and Metaldehyde.

Precautions in Chemical Control

  • Apply chemicals according to recommendations.
  • Avoid contamination of water sources.
  • Protect non-target organisms.
  • Use chemicals only when necessary.

Integrated Management of Mollusks

Effective management combines monitoring, cultural control, mechanical control, biological control, and chemical control.

Advantages of Integrated Management

  • Environmentally safer
  • More sustainable
  • Lower risk of resistance
  • Reduced chemical use
  • Better long-term control

ALE High-Yield Exam Tips

1. Direct-Seeded vs. Transplanted Rice

Golden Apple Snail (GAS) is most destructive during the early growth stages of rice, particularly in direct-seeded rice (DSR) and newly transplanted seedlings.

  • Highest damage occurs during the first 15–20 days after planting.
  • Young rice seedlings are soft and easily consumed.
  • Damage decreases as rice plants mature.

Once rice enters the tillering stage (about 3–4 weeks old), the stems become tougher and less susceptible to feeding damage.

Board Exam Trap: The most susceptible stage is the seedling stage, not the mature rice stage.

2. Water Management Trick

Water management is one of the most effective cultural methods for controlling Golden Apple Snails.

  • Drain fields whenever possible.
  • Reduce standing water.
  • Concentrate snails in low areas for easier collection.

Draining reduces snail mobility and exposes them to collection or other control measures.

Board Exam Trap: Drain fields to control snails. Flooding favors snail movement and survival.

3. Historical Introduction of Golden Apple Snail

Golden Apple Snail (Pomacea canaliculata) originated from South America.

It was intentionally introduced into the Philippines during the 1980s as a potential food source and backyard aquaculture enterprise.

After escaping into irrigation systems and rice fields, it became one of the most destructive invasive agricultural pests in Asia.

Board Exam Trap: GAS was introduced as a food source, not for biological control or pest management.

4. Pink Egg Masses

Female Golden Apple Snails lay bright pink egg masses above the water surface on rice stems, bamboo stakes, canal walls, and other objects.

  • Egg masses are bright pink when newly laid.
  • They become lighter as they mature.
  • Destroying egg masses helps reduce future populations.

Board Exam Trap: Golden Apple Snail eggs are laid above water, not underwater.

5. Feeding Structure of Mollusks

Mollusks feed using a specialized rasping structure called the radula.

The radula functions like a rough tongue that scrapes plant tissues during feeding.

Board Exam Trap: Radula is the feeding organ of mollusks, while mandibles are the feeding structures of insects.

Summary

Mollusks are soft-bodied animals belonging to the phylum Mollusca. The most important agricultural mollusk pests are snails and slugs. Among these, the Golden Apple Snail, Pomacea canaliculata, is one of the most destructive pests of rice. Effective management involves cultural, mechanical, biological, and chemical control integrated into a sustainable pest management program.
