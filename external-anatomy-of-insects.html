🦗 External Anatomy of Insects

Entomology and Pest Management Reviewer

1. External Anatomy

External anatomy refers to the study of the outside structures of insects. These structures are important because they help insects move, feed, sense their surroundings, reproduce, and protect themselves from injury and water loss.

  • Shows how insects are identified and classified
  • Explains feeding, movement, and survival adaptations
  • Helps distinguish insects from other arthropods
  • Useful in insect pest identification and management

2. Distinguishing Characteristics of Insects

Insects may be distinguished from other arthropods by their body organization, appendages, and external features.

  • Body divided into three distinct regions: head, thorax, and abdomen
  • One pair of antennae
  • Three pairs of legs attached to the thorax
  • Often one or two pairs of wings
  • Posterior appendages of the head form specialized mouthparts

Three Distinct Body Regions

  • Head – sensory and feeding center
  • Thorax – locomotory region bearing legs and wings
  • Abdomen – contains digestive, reproductive, and respiratory structures

One Pair of Antennae

Antennae are sensory appendages used for detecting smell, touch, vibration, humidity, temperature, and other environmental signals.

Three Pairs of Legs

Adult insects normally have three pairs of legs. A few insects may be legless in some stages, while some larvae may possess additional fleshy appendages called prolegs.

Presence of Wings

Most adult insects possess one or two pairs of wings. Wings are usually borne by the second and third thoracic segments.

Specialized Mouthparts

Insect mouthparts are formed from head appendages and are adapted for different feeding habits such as chewing, piercing, sucking, siphoning, sponging, or lapping.

3. Insect Body Wall

The insect body wall forms the external covering of the insect body. It protects the body, gives shape and support, prevents water loss, and serves as the attachment point for muscles.

Parts of the Insect Body Wall

1. Epidermal Cell Layer

The epidermal cell layer is the living cellular layer of the body wall. It secretes the cuticle and produces molting fluid during growth.

2. Cuticle

The cuticle is the non-cellular layer of the body wall. It covers the entire body surface and also lines structures such as the air tubes, salivary glands, and parts of the digestive tract.

Layers of the Cuticle

  • Epicuticle – the outermost thin layer containing cuticulin, wax, and cement. It helps prevent water loss.
  • Exocuticle – the middle hardened layer that gives strength and resilience to the cuticle. It is formed largely of chitin.
  • Endocuticle – the innermost thick layer that provides flexibility and support.

3. Basement Membrane

The basement membrane separates the body wall from the internal organs.

Because the exoskeleton is rigid, insects must molt or shed the old cuticle in order to grow.

4. Body Regions of Insects

The insect body is divided into three main regions: head, thorax, and abdomen. Each region has specialized structures and functions.

5. Head

The head is the globular or capsule-like anterior body region of an insect. It bears the eyes, antennae, and mouthparts.

Functions of the Head

  • Sensory reception
  • Feeding
  • Coordination of movement and behavior

Structures Found in the Head

  • Antennae
  • Compound eyes
  • Simple eyes or ocelli
  • Mouthparts
  • Frons
  • Clypeus
  • Labrum

6. Head Position Based on the Long Axis of the Body

Insects may be classified according to the direction of their mouthparts in relation to the long axis of the body.

1. Hypognathous Head

In a hypognathous head, the mouthparts are directed downward at a right angle to the body axis.

Examples: grasshoppers, cockroaches, mantids, larvae of moths and butterflies.

2. Opisthognathous Head

In an opisthognathous head, the mouthparts project backward between the legs.

Examples: cicadas, bugs, and leafhoppers.

3. Prognathous Head

In a prognathous head, the mouthparts are directed forward and are in line with the body axis.

Examples: termites and ground beetles.

7. Mouthparts

Mouthparts vary depending on the kind of food the insect eats. Different insects have different mouthpart modifications suited for chewing, sucking, piercing, siphoning, or sponging.

Basic Mouthpart Structures

1. Mandibles

Mandibles are tooth-like structures used for chewing, grinding, tearing, cutting, or pinching solid food.

  • Heavily sclerotized
  • Paired structures
  • Unsegmented
  • Important in chewing insects

2. Maxillae

Maxillae are paired, segmented structures located behind the mandibles. They help manipulate food and assist in mastication.

  • Help put food into the mouth
  • Assist in chewing and handling food
  • Usually lie directly behind the mandibles

3. Labrum

The labrum is the upper lip of the insect mouth. It is movable and flap-like, helping hold food in place during feeding.

4. Labium

The labium is the lower lip found posterior to the maxillae. It helps support and manipulate food.

5. Hypopharynx

The hypopharynx is a central tongue-like structure that projects from the membranous floor of the head and bears the opening of the salivary ducts.

These basic mouthpart structures may be modified into different types depending on the feeding habits of the insect.

8. Thorax

The thorax is the middle region of the insect body and is known as the locomotory region because it bears the legs and wings.

Divisions of the Thorax

  • Prothorax – bears the first pair of legs
  • Mesothorax – bears the second pair of legs and usually the first pair of wings
  • Metathorax – bears the third pair of legs and usually the second pair of wings

9. Abdomen

The abdomen is the posterior body region of insects. It contains organs involved in digestion, reproduction, excretion, and respiration.

Structures Found in the Abdomen

  • Spiracles
  • Genitalia
  • Cerci
  • Ovipositor

Functions of the Abdomen

  • Digestion
  • Respiration
  • Excretion
  • Reproduction

10. Appendages

Appendages are movable structures attached to the insect body. They are usually jointed and may be modified for different functions.

Examples of Insect Appendages

  • Antennae
  • Legs
  • Wings
  • Mouthparts
  • Cerci

Insect appendages are modified according to function, such as walking, jumping, swimming, grasping, feeding, sensing, or reproduction.
